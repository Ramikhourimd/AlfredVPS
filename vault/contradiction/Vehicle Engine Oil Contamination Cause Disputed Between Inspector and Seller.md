# Vehicle Engine Oil Contamination Cause Disputed Between Inspector and Seller

## Claim A

The pre-purchase vehicle inspector identified excessive crankcase blowby (abnormal fumes from the dipstick opening), widespread oil contamination across both sides of the engine and the entire undercarriage, an unreadable engine number due to oil coating, and signs of prior hood removal and front bumper repair. The inspector concluded these findings indicate potential engine failure, engine replacement history, or front-end collision — and recommended professional garage verification before purchase.

## Claim B

The vehicle handler (seller's representative) attributed all oil contamination to a single cause: a worker checked the oil and left the dipstick air vent cap improperly seated. The dipstick has an unusually shaped, non-round opening, and the loose cap caused oil to spray during driving. The handler insisted this is not an oil leak and that their team had inspected the vehicle thoroughly.

## Analysis

The explanations are difficult to reconcile. A loose dipstick cap could cause oil spray in the immediate vicinity of the dipstick, but the inspector noted oil on the opposite side of the engine and along the entire undercarriage — suggesting a more widespread source. Additionally, the excessive crankcase blowby (fumes when pulling the dipstick) is an engine-internal condition that cannot be caused by an external cap issue. However, some blowby is normal for diesel engines, and the threshold for "excessive" involves subjective judgement.

## Resolution

Unresolved. A professional garage inspection was recommended to determine the true cause. See [[task/Get Vehicle Engine Inspected at Garage Before Purchase]].
