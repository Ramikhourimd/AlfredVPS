# Vehicle Engine Blowby Diagnosis vs Seller Loose Dipstick Cap Explanation

## Claim A

A professional pre-purchase vehicle inspector examined a diesel 4x4 (153,000 km) on 2025-11-03 and found: (1) excessive crankcase blowby — abnormal fumes escaping from the dipstick opening, (2) oil contamination across the entire engine, both sides, and along the undercarriage, (3) signs of prior hood removal and front bumper repair or replacement — possibly indicating an engine swap or front collision, (4) engine number unreadable due to oil coating. The inspector flagged the engine as requiring professional garage verification before purchase and stated he would not take responsibility for the engine's condition.

## Claim B

The vehicle handler/seller claims the oil contamination is caused by an improperly reseated dipstick cap. He states that when the vehicle was received, a worker checked the oil and left the air vent cap loose — the dipstick has a non-standard, non-round opening that makes proper reseating difficult. He insists this is not an oil leak, that they inspected the vehicle thoroughly, and that the engine is sound.

## Analysis

The inspector and seller offer fundamentally different explanations for the same physical evidence. The inspector sees systemic engine problems (excessive blowby, possible engine replacement history); the seller attributes it to a simple maintenance error (loose cap on an unusual dipstick). The oil distribution pattern — present not just near the dipstick but on the opposite side of the engine and along the entire underside — is difficult to explain by a single loose cap alone. However, the non-standard dipstick opening does make the loose-cap scenario at least plausible for some of the oil spray. Only professional garage diagnosis can resolve this.

## Resolution

Unresolved — pending garage inspection results. The inspector explicitly stated that if the garage certifies the engine as sound, the garage takes responsibility.
