# Vehicle Inspector Engine Assessment vs Seller Dipstick Explanation

## Claim A

The vehicle inspector found excessive crankcase blowby (abnormal fumes from dipstick opening), oil contamination across the entire engine and undercarriage (both sides, not localized), signs of hood removal and front bumper repair/replacement (indicating possible engine swap or front collision), and an unreadable engine number due to oil. The inspector flagged the engine as potentially compromised and refused to certify it — stating the vehicle must be checked at a garage before purchase, with the garage bearing responsibility for certification.

## Claim B

The vehicle handler (seller's representative) claims the oil contamination is entirely caused by a worker who checked the oil and left the dipstick air vent cap improperly seated. The dipstick has an unusually shaped, non-round opening, and the loose cap caused oil to spray during driving. The handler insists this is not an oil leak, that they inspected the vehicle thoroughly, and that the engine is sound.

## Analysis

The explanations are difficult to reconcile: a loose dipstick cap could cause oil spray in the immediate dipstick area, but the inspector noted oil on the opposite side of the engine and along the entire undercarriage — a distribution pattern inconsistent with a single localized spray source. Additionally, the signs of hood removal could indicate either engine replacement or front collision repair — neither of which the seller's dipstick explanation addresses. The unreadable engine number (due to oil coating) prevents independent verification of whether the engine is original to the vehicle.

## Resolution

Unresolved. The task to get the engine inspected at a professional garage was created. The garage inspection would be the definitive arbiter — if the engine is certified sound, the garage takes responsibility per the inspector's stated protocol.
