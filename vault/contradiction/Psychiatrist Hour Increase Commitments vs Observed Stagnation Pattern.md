# Psychiatrist Hour Increase Commitments vs Observed Stagnation Pattern

## Claim A

Management expects and requires that part-time psychiatrists will increase their clinical hours over time. Rami approved Eliezer's 25-hour start on the explicit condition that he commit to a timeline for reaching 40 hours. Similarly, Atef was asked to increase beyond 20 hours. The clinic's scaling model depends on existing staff growing their positions.

## Claim B

Observed psychiatrist behavior consistently contradicts these commitments:
- **Atef** logged only 20 hours despite prior requests to increase. He frames his participation as "doing Rami a favor" — treating the position as voluntary rather than obligatory.
- **Eliezer** requested to "start as late as possible while keeping the position reserved" and could only offer 25 hours due to research in China, an innovation center, and Stage B exam preparation. His multiple concurrent commitments suggest clinical work at Telia'z is not a priority.
- The pattern suggests psychiatrists view their quarter-position at the telepsychiatry clinic as secondary employment, not a position they intend to grow.

## Analysis

The contradiction stems from a structural mismatch: the clinic offers quarter-positions to attract psychiatrists who have other commitments (research, private practice, exam preparation). The very flexibility that makes the position attractive also means psychiatrists have no incentive to increase hours — their primary professional identity and income come from elsewhere. Management verbal agreements about hour increases lack enforcement mechanisms.

## Resolution

Unresolved. If the pattern holds across more psychiatrists, the scaling model needs revision — either requiring minimum hour commitments at hire (risking fewer applicants) or accepting that hour stagnation is structural and planning for 4-5x individual hires instead of assuming growth from existing staff.
