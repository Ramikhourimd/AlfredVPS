# Inspector Engine Diagnosis vs Seller Dipstick Explanation

## Claim A

The professional vehicle inspector found excessive crankcase blowby (abnormal fumes from dipstick opening), oil contamination across the entire engine and undercarriage (both sides, dripping underneath), signs of hood removal and front bumper repair/replacement (indicating possible engine swap or front collision), and an unreadable engine number due to oil. He flagged the engine as potentially compromised and refused to certify it — stating only a garage can take that responsibility.

## Claim B

The seller's vehicle handler claims the oil mess is entirely caused by an improperly reseated dipstick cap. He explains that their worker checked the oil and left the air vent cap loose on the non-standard dipstick opening, causing oil to spray across the engine bay. He insists there is no oil leak and that they inspected the vehicle thoroughly.

## Analysis

The inspector's findings (oil on both sides of engine, undercarriage coating, signs of hood removal) are broader than what a loose dipstick cap would typically cause. However, the handler's explanation is plausible for some of the oil presence — a diesel engine running with an open crankcase vent would spray oil. The excessive blowby symptom (fumes from dipstick hole) is a separate observation that the seller's explanation does not address. The signs of hood removal and bumper repair remain unexplained by either party's account.

## Resolution

Unresolved. A professional garage inspection was agreed as the arbiter — if the garage certifies the engine as sound, the garage bears responsibility.
