# Patient Discharge Targets Conflict With Clinician Long-Term Care Philosophy

## Claim A

Basel's patient lifecycle model with expected discharge percentages at time intervals is necessary to manage patient volume. Without structured discharge, the intake-to-follow-up bottleneck worsens indefinitely — patients wait 7+ months for appointments, clinicians open uncompensated overtime slots, and the clinic cannot scale.

## Claim B

Clinical staff view psychiatry as inherently requiring long-term therapeutic relationships. A time-limited treatment model with discharge targets may alienate clinicians who already feel like "orphaned children" in the organization. Given that retention is driven by convenience rather than commitment, adding a philosophical misalignment could trigger departures.

## Analysis

Both claims have merit. The operational need for discharge management is real — without it, the follow-up bottleneck is structurally unsolvable. But the clinical staff's professional identity is also real — psychiatrists and case managers chose the field partly because of long-term patient relationships. The contradiction is not between facts but between two legitimate organizational needs: operational sustainability and staff retention/satisfaction.

## Resolution

Unresolved as of 2026-03-17. Possible resolution paths:
1. Frame discharge as "graduation" rather than termination — preserving clinical dignity
2. Allow clinicians to retain a subset of long-term patients while discharging others
3. Validate with staff survey data before implementing rigid discharge targets
