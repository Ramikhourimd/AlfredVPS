# Switch From Sertraline to Venlafaxine for Treatment Tolerance

## Context

Patient has been on SSRI-class antidepressants (Sertraline 50mg, previously Cipralex/Escitalopram) for years with diminishing therapeutic response. Recurring depressive and dissociative episodes indicate the current medication is no longer effective.

## Options Considered

1. **Increase Sertraline dose** — Rejected. Patient has already been through dose adjustments, and the body has adapted to the SSRI mechanism of action.
2. **Switch to another SSRI** — Rejected. Patient already tried both Sertraline and Cipralex, both in the same SSRI family. Body has developed tolerance to this drug class.
3. **Switch to Venlafaxine (SNRI)** — Selected. Different mechanism of action (serotonin + noradrenaline), supported by research showing efficacy when patients stop responding to SSRIs alone.

## Decision

Switch from Sertraline (SSRI) to Venlafaxine (SNRI) using a structured tapering and initiation protocol. Maintain adjunct sleep and anxiolytic medications unchanged.

## Rationale

- Two SSRI medications have been tried and both lost efficacy
- Research supports switching to a dual-mechanism drug (SNRI) when SSRIs fail
- Venlafaxine adds noradrenaline activity, providing a pharmacologically distinct approach
- Manageable side effect profile (dry mouth/eyes, minor BP concerns at low doses)

## Consequences

- Requires family doctor involvement for prescription and blood pressure monitoring
- 3-4 week transition period with potential symptom variability during washout
- Patient must maintain psychological therapy throughout transition
- Follow-up psychiatric review needed in approximately 2 months
