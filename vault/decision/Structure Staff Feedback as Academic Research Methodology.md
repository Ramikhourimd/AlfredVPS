# Structure Staff Feedback as Academic Research Methodology

## Context

Rivi Idelman was collecting valuable qualitative feedback from clinic staff about burnout, satisfaction, and work conditions, but presenting it as informal bullet points and impressions. While the insights were genuine, they lacked the structure needed to track progress, compare cohorts, or convince leadership to act. Rami recognized the need to formalize this into a rigorous, repeatable research methodology.

## Options Considered

1. **Informal qualitative reporting** — Continue collecting impressions and sharing bullet points. Risk: dismissed as anecdotal, no baseline for tracking improvement.
2. **Academic research methodology** — Structure findings with background, methodology, measurable metrics, results, discussion, and recommendations. Enables periodic measurement and trend tracking.
3. **External consultant** — Hire an organizational psychologist to conduct the research. Risk: expensive, slow, lacks insider context.

## Decision

All staff experience research conducted by Rivi Idelman must follow academic research methodology: background, methodology, measurable scales, segmented results, discussion, and actionable recommendations. A periodic questionnaire will be built to enable longitudinal tracking.

## Rationale

- Converts gut feelings into measurable data points that can track improvement over time
- Enables segmentation (role type, hours, schedule) to identify specific interventions
- Produces artifacts that can persuade leadership (evidence-based, not anecdotal)
- Aligns with Rivi's researcher role definition
- Automated processing via Google Sheets enables overnight turnaround

## Consequences

- Rivi must build a formal questionnaire covering multiple satisfaction dimensions
- All future staff feedback reports must follow the academic structure
- Enables periodic re-measurement to track whether interventions are working
- Creates an evidence base for staffing and operational decisions
