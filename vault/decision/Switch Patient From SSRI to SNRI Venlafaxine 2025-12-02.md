# Switch Patient From SSRI to SNRI (Venlafaxine)

## Context

Patient has been on Sertraline 50mg for years and previously tried Cipralex (Escitalopram). Both medications belong to the SSRI family. The patient reports recurring depressive and dissociative episodes despite ongoing SSRI treatment, indicating treatment resistance within this drug class.

## Options Considered

1. **Option A: Increase Sertraline dose** — Not chosen. Patient has been on a stable low dose for years without adequate response, and previously failed on another SSRI (Cipralex), suggesting the entire SSRI class is inadequate.
2. **Option B: Switch to Venlafaxine (SNRI)** — Chosen. Dual mechanism (serotonin + noradrenaline) offers a different pharmacological approach. Research supports switching to SNRIs when SSRIs lose efficacy.
3. **Option C: Add augmentation agent** — Not discussed as primary strategy.

## Decision

Switch from Sertraline (SSRI) to Venlafaxine (SNRI) with a structured taper-and-start protocol managed through the family doctor, with blood pressure monitoring.

## Rationale

- Two SSRI medications have been tried (Sertraline, Cipralex) without sustained response
- The patient's body has adapted to the SSRI drug family
- Venlafaxine works on both serotonin and noradrenaline, providing a mechanistically different approach
- Research evidence supports SNRI switching in SSRI non-responders

## Consequences

- Requires a 3-week taper of Sertraline before starting Venlafaxine
- Family doctor must manage the transition and prescribe Venlafaxine
- Blood pressure monitoring required during Venlafaxine initiation
- Possible transient side effects: dry mouth, dry eyes
- Follow-up with psychiatrist in 2 months to assess response
