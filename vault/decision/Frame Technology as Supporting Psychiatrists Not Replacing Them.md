# Frame Technology as Supporting Psychiatrists Not Replacing Them

## Context

Preparing for the GP Confederation demo, the leadership team needed to decide how to position the AI and technology capabilities. The UK healthcare audience (NHS clinicians, governance specialists) could react negatively to technology positioned as replacing clinical judgment.

## Options Considered

1. **Lead with AI capabilities** — Position the AI as the core differentiator and efficiency driver
2. **Lead with clinical service, technology as enabler** — Position technology as supporting psychiatrists' workflow
3. **Minimize technology discussion** — Focus purely on clinical outcomes and hide the AI layer

## Decision

Position the demo as showing "how the virtual psychiatry service and technology are utilized by psychiatrists." The AI is framed as a tool that supports clinical decision-making, not one that replaces or overrides it. Psychiatrist remains the authoritative clinical figure in all presentations.

## Rationale

UK clinical governance culture is sensitive to AI replacing clinical judgment. CQC and NICE frameworks emphasize clinician accountability. Framing the AI as a support tool (pre-populating summaries, generating agendas, drafting reports for review) maintains the psychiatrist's authority while demonstrating efficiency gains. This aligns with the "service provider first, technology second" positioning decision.

## Consequences

All UK-facing materials, demos, and partner communications must maintain this framing. Marketing should emphasize clinical quality and psychiatrist-led care rather than AI automation. This constrains how aggressively the AI capabilities can be marketed but builds trust with NHS stakeholders. Internal efficiency metrics (60% time savings) should be presented as enabling psychiatrists to see more patients, not as reducing psychiatrist involvement.
