# Reduce One-on-One Meeting Frequency to Monthly

## Context

The clinic had been running both Sunday and Monday meetings, with significant content overlap between them. Additionally, one-on-one meetings were happening weekly. Rami recognized that the frequent one-on-one cadence was a product of the organizational structure he had been building, which needed close hands-on oversight. With Basel now in the operations manager role, the structure no longer requires that intensity.

## Options Considered

1. **Keep both Sunday and Monday meetings plus weekly one-on-ones** — status quo, redundant
2. **Weekly clinical meetings only, monthly one-on-ones** — reduces overhead while maintaining clinical oversight
3. **Eliminate one-on-ones entirely** — too aggressive, loses personal coordination channel

## Decision

Adopt Option 2: Keep weekly clinical team meetings but reduce one-on-one meetings to monthly. Basel has autonomy to adjust the exact cadence based on what works for his management style. Clinical matters may still warrant ad-hoc meetings (e.g., protocol, academia, case reviews).

## Rationale

- Sunday and Monday meetings cover largely the same content — no need for both
- The one-on-one structure was needed when Rami was building the organization personally; now that Basel is managing operations, ongoing weekly one-on-ones add overhead without proportional value
- Monthly one-on-ones still provide a regular touchpoint for strategic alignment

## Consequences

- Basel has more unstructured time for operational work
- May need to establish alternative channels for time-sensitive operational issues between monthly one-on-ones
- Clinical supervision and protocol discussions continue separately from one-on-ones
