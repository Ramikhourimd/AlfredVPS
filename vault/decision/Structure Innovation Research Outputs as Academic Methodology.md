# Structure Innovation Research Outputs as Academic Methodology

## Context

Rivi Idelman has been collecting qualitative staff experience data through informal conversations, producing unstructured notes and impressions. The findings are valuable but not actionable in their current form — they lack measurable metrics, segmentation, and systematic methodology.

## Options Considered

1. **Continue informal qualitative data collection** — easier but produces subjective, non-trackable insights
2. **Adopt academic research structure** — more rigorous; enables measurement, tracking, and evidence-based decisions

## Decision

All innovation research outputs from Rivi must follow academic research structure: background, methodology, results, discussion, and recommendations. Staff experience data must be converted from qualitative impressions to measurable, trackable metrics via periodic surveys.

## Rationale

Rami observed that Rivi's findings ("burnout is high") are important but unusable without structure. To drive action, the organization needs: (1) measurable baselines, (2) segmented analysis (by role, hours, shift pattern), (3) correlation between variables and satisfaction, and (4) periodic tracking to measure improvement. Academic methodology provides this framework naturally.

## Consequences

- Rivi needs to build a structured survey instrument
- Each research project requires a formal methodology document before data collection
- Rami will provide a methodology template and examples
- Automated literature review pipeline will be built to support Rivi's research questions
- Outputs become defensible evidence for board-level decisions
