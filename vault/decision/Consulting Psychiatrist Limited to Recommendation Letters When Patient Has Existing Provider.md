# Consulting Psychiatrist Limited to Recommendation Letters When Patient Has Existing Provider

## Context

During the 2025-12-02 residential facility psychiatric consultation, Rami Khouri established a clear clinical boundary: when assessing a patient who already has a treating psychiatrist at their facility, the consulting role must be limited to recommendation letters rather than direct prescriptions.

## Options Considered

1. **Option A** — Issue direct prescriptions to the residential patient, overriding the facility psychiatrist's treatment plan
2. **Option B** — Provide recommendation letters to the facility's treating psychiatrist, respecting the existing treatment relationship

## Decision

The consulting psychiatrist issues recommendation letters only. No direct prescriptions are issued when a patient has an existing treating psychiatrist at another facility.

## Rationale

Rami stated the principle explicitly: "I cannot treat you — you have a place that treats you. Having two treatment sources is not good." The reasoning:
- Dual prescribing creates clinical confusion — two doctors modifying the same medication regimen independently risks dangerous interactions or contradictory adjustments
- It undermines the treating relationship at the patient's primary facility
- The consulting psychiatrist lacks ongoing monitoring capability — they see the patient once, while the facility psychiatrist manages day-to-day care
- A recommendation letter preserves the consulting psychiatrist's clinical input while maintaining clear lines of treatment authority

## Consequences

- The consulting psychiatrist's recommendations may or may not be followed by the facility psychiatrist
- The patient must cooperate with their primary treating psychiatrist to implement changes
- Clinical documentation must clearly state the consulting role and the boundaries of the interaction
- The patient receives a physical letter to deliver to their treating psychiatrist
