# Basel Serves as Integration Point Above Three Clinic Pillars

## Context

The three-pillar clinic management model (Clinical Operations Manager, Medical Director, Administrative Manager) creates parallel tracks with no hierarchical relationship. The question arose: who integrates across these tracks when decisions require cross-functional coordination?

## Options Considered

1. **No single integration point** — The three managers coordinate peer-to-peer through regular meetings
2. **Basel as integration point** — Basel (Operations Manager for Israel) serves as the coordinator above the three pillars, without being a fourth manager
3. **CMO as integration point** — The company-level CMO integrates across all clinic functions

## Decision

Basel serves as the integration point for the Israeli clinic. The three parallel managers report upward to Basel, who coordinates across tracks without duplicating their domain authority. Basel is not a fourth pillar — he is the clinic-level leader who ensures alignment.

## Rationale

Someone must own cross-functional decisions at the clinic level. The three-pillar parallel structure prevents internal hierarchy (which was the design goal), but creates a coordination vacuum. Basel is already positioned as incoming operations lead and has visibility across all three domains.

## Consequences

- Basel's role becomes broader than "operations manager" — effectively clinic general manager
- Must be clearly communicated to avoid confusion with the Clinical Operations Manager (Shira) role
- Creates dependency on Basel for conflict resolution between the three pillars
- For future clinics (UK), a similar integration role must be defined
