# Adopt Structured Five-Level Suicidal Risk Assessment Protocol

## Context

Case managers at Telia'z Clinic Israel lacked a formalized assessment framework for evaluating suicidal risk. During a clinical supervision call on 2025-12-01, case manager Selina contacted Rami Khouri for urgent guidance on a patient with prior suicidal ideation and access to a weapon. The call revealed the need for a standardized clinical protocol.

## Options Considered

1. **Ad hoc supervision** — Continue relying on case-by-case phone consultations with CMO for risk assessment guidance
2. **Structured protocol** — Define a formal assessment hierarchy that all case managers follow independently before escalating

## Decision

Adopt a five-level suicidal risk assessment hierarchy as the standard clinical protocol for all case managers:

1. **Suicidal ideation** — General thoughts about death or dying
2. **Suicidal thoughts** — Specific thinking about self-harm
3. **Intention** — Patient has formed intent to act
4. **Plan** — Patient has a specific plan for how to carry it out
5. **Act** — Patient has taken preparatory or actual steps

Each level must be assessed during clinical evaluation. If any level was not assessed, the clinician must document why and what their clinical impression was despite the gap.

Additionally, two critical clinical principles were formalized:
- **Point-in-time assessment**: Immediate danger (sakana miyedit) can only be assessed at a specific moment. No clinical tool reliably predicts future danger.
- **Risk factors vs current state**: Prediction of future risk relies on risk factors (gormei sakana), which is a fundamentally different clinical activity from assessing current danger.

## Rationale

The Selina supervision call demonstrated that case managers needed structured guidance rather than ad hoc support. A formalized hierarchy ensures consistent documentation, proper escalation, and defensible clinical decision-making — especially critical given the military reporting obligations for certain patients.

## Consequences

All case managers are expected to follow this assessment hierarchy when encountering suicidal risk. Documentation must reflect which levels were assessed and findings at each level. This protocol feeds into the broader case manager supervision framework being developed for the clinic.
