# A detailed explanation of the ambidextrous organization model for innovation, addressing common conflicts and outlining its structural and team design principles.

## Addressing the inherent conflict between R&D and innovation teams by introducing the ambidextrous organizational model.

> [1](#startMs=1764588117728&endMs=1764588220728): a constant simmering conflict between your fast moving exploratory innovation team and the established execution focused R&D team, that's not an accident. It's practically baked into a flawed organizational design. We basically set them up to compete and then we act all surprised when they do. So, what's the solution? Well, a ton of research points to something called the ambidextrous organization. The easiest way to think about it is like this, your company needs two hands. One hand, that's your R&D team is an absolute expert at executing and optimizing today's business. The other hand, that's your innovation team is for exploring and building the business of tomorrow. They operate separately, but they're controlled by the same brain, your senior leadership.

## Validating the effectiveness of the ambidextrous organization and detailing its blueprint, focusing on the critical reporting structure and initial team components.

> [1](#startMs=1764588288728&endMs=1764588400882): You might be asking, does this actually work? Oh yeah. The research shows that organizations that use this ambidextrous model achieve a staggering 90% of their innovation goals. I mean, think about that number, 90%. That should make every single leader pay very, very close attention. This isn't magic, it's just really smart organizational design. Okay, so how do we actually build this thing? We're going to walk through the three core parts of the blueprint. First, getting the structure right. Second, assembling the perfect team, and third, setting some really clear rules of engagement. And it all starts with the foundation. The foundation of this entire structure, and honestly, this is the single most important decision you're going to make is all about the reporting line. If you get this wrong, you're pretty much building on sand. But if you get it right, you create the conditions for success right from the start. So here it is, the golden rule. The head of innovation must report directly to the CEO. This one move changes everything. It immediately establishes the innovation department as a peer to R&D, not some junior team tucked underneath it. This completely sidesteps all those territorial conflicts that kill progress. And you know, this lines up with a much bigger trend we're seeing. For example, 83% of physician informatics officers now have expanding roles, reporting directly into the C suite. This structure just recognizes reality. Innovation is a critical function that needs a direct line to the top. Okay, so with that solid structure in place, it's time to talk about people. Who do you actually need on this team to drive real results? Let's go ahead and assemble our dream team. I find it's helpful to think of the department is having three main parts or arms. First, you've got clinical and insights. Think of these folks as your sensors, your eyes and ears out in the real world, deeply understanding the pain points of clinicians and patients. Second, you have AI and data. This is your technical engine.

## Detailing the ambidextrous organization's team structure, governance mechanisms including a steering committee and Three Horizons framework, and the practical workflow for innovation, emphasizing structural solutions to team friction.

> [1](#startMs=1764588400902&endMs=1764588625902): The team that actually designs and builds the models. And third, projects and implementation. This is the operational hub that manages the road maps, the budgets and makes sure those great ideas actually become reality. And here's how those three arms break down into a really lean but incredibly effective five-person team. You've got the innovation lead who's setting the vision. Your senior AI/ML engineer and platform engineer make up your technical core. The clinical informatics specialist is that crucial bridge to the clinical world. You can't live without them. And finally, the product manager keeps the trains running on time, managing the road map and all the metrics. This is your core unit right here for driving change. All right, you've got the right structure. You've got the right people, but now for the tricky part, how do you manage that delicate interface between your new agile innovation team and your big established R&D department? This, my friends, is where governance comes in. Now, the goal of governance here isn't to create a bunch of bureaucracy, not at all. It's to prevent conflict before it even starts. And there are two incredibly powerful tools to do this: a dedicated steering committee and a portfolio framework called the three horizons. First up, the innovation steering committee. This is your high-level control tower. It's made up of the CEO, the head of R&D, the innovation lead, and key clinical leaders. Their job, to set the priorities and hand out the resources. But their most critical function, the one that makes this all work, is that they are the ultimate referee. They decide which projects belong to innovation and which belong to R&D. And just like that, you've pretty much eliminated turf wars. The second tool is the three horizons framework, and this is just a brilliantly simple way to give each team clear territory, a clear swim lane, and a shared language for talking about all the projects in the pipeline. Horizon One is all about the core business. We're talking about incremental improvements to your existing products and services, usually on a zero to two-year timeline. This is the undisputed territory of your traditional R&D team. They own this space, period. Now, Horizon 2, this is the adjacent space. This is all about extending what you currently do into new areas, maybe on a two to four-year timeline. Now, this, this is shared territory. Projects here need both teams to collaborate. And that steering committee we talked about, it plays a huge role in managing this space. And finally, we have Horizon 3. This is the world of breakthrough, disruptive stuff. Unproven AI, totally new business models, looking out three to seven years. And this is why the innovation team exists. This is their sandbox. They have clear ownership and the autonomy they need to go explore what's next. So, we have the structure, we have the team, and we have the rules of engagement. Let's walk through how this all comes together in a practical day-to-day workflow from a real-world problem to a real-world solution. It all starts on the ground, right? The insights team is out there collecting real problems from clinicians. The product manager then takes that and frames it up. Leadership through that steering committee decides if it gets prioritized. Then the AI team designs a solution, which then gets handed off for development and implementation. And this is so important, the loop closes with data on usage and impact being fed right back to the team for the next iteration. It's a clean, logical flow from need to impact. So look, if you take just one thing away from all this, let it be this. The friction you feel between your innovation and R&D teams, it's almost always structural. It's not personal. You've got good people trying their best to do their jobs inside a system that's just not set up for them to win. The solution is to stop blaming people.
