# Emotional Supervision Benefits Require Synchronous Presence Not Recorded Summaries

## Claim

The therapeutic and team-cohesion value of clinical supervision sessions (specifically the Moli Lahad burnout/self-care series) is inherently experiential — it depends on being present in the moment, not on the information content that could be captured in a recording or summary. "The value of the session is in being there, not in reading a summary afterward."

## Basis

During the 2025-11-10 debrief following the first Moli Lahad supervision session, the team explicitly articulated this principle. Ori Shukron reported the session "strongly united the case manager team" and described it as a "very positive experience." Shira summarized that "presence and emotional safety are essential." The session included guided meditation, personal sharing, and interactive exercises — all formats whose value is destroyed by time-shifting to a recording.

This is a distinct claim from the related assumption that recording undermines participant openness. Even in a hypothetical scenario where recording had no effect on openness, this assumption holds: a recorded summary would not convey the bonding, emotional regulation, and team cohesion benefits that require real-time co-presence.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-11-10: First Moli Lahad session debrief — team unanimously reported value was in the shared experience, not transferable information
- Related: Remote telepsychiatry burnout pattern makes these synchronous touchpoints particularly valuable for a dispersed workforce

## Impact

- Attendance at supervision sessions must be treated as non-optional for the cohesion benefit
- Asynchronous alternatives (recorded sessions, written summaries, self-paced modules) cannot substitute for live supervision
- Scheduling must accommodate all target participants simultaneously
- This reinforces the decision not to record sessions — but for a reason beyond privacy concerns
