# Academic Survey Methodology Will Produce Actionable Staff Data Where Informal Approaches Failed

## Claim

A structured, quantitative survey instrument designed with academic rigor — including proper segmentation variables (role type, shift timing, weekly hours, tenure) and measurable dimensions (financial satisfaction, isolation, technology experience) — will succeed in producing actionable staff satisfaction data where previous informal and semi-structured approaches failed.

## Basis

During the 2026-02-18 meeting, Rami directed Rivi Idelman to approach staff experience research with "academic rigor." Previous survey attempts produced statistically unreliable data (~16 responses, insufficient for meaningful analysis). The new approach prescribes specific dimensions and segmentation variables, treating it as a research problem rather than an HR exercise.

## Evidence Trail

- Previous formal surveys: ~16 responses, statistically meaningless (constraint/Staff Satisfaction Survey Data Statistically Unreliable)
- Previous informal channels: Produced qualitative insights but not trackable metrics (synthesis/Informal Staff Feedback Channels Outperform Formal Surveys)
- 2026-02-18: Rami prescribes academic methodology as the solution
- Survey dimensions defined: 8 categories, 6+ segmentation variables

## Impact

- If this assumption holds, the clinic gains a periodic, quantitative staff satisfaction tracking tool
- If it fails (low response rates persist regardless of methodology), the clinic remains dependent on informal intelligence channels
- The assumption implicitly bets that survey design, not staff engagement willingness, was the bottleneck — this is challenged by existing evidence that response rates, not question quality, were the core issue
- Resource investment: Rivi's time designing the instrument, plus ongoing administration costs
