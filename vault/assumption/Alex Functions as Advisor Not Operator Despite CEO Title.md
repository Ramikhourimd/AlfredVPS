# Alex Functions as Advisor Not Operator Despite CEO Title

## Claim

Alex Taliaz, who holds the CEO or board-level leadership position, functions as an advisor rather than an operational executive. Both Rami and Oren assess that Alex does not follow through on commitments — he suggests things and hopes they happen. Rami and Oren constantly need to remind him of commitments. This stands in stark contrast to Dekkel, who is described as thorough with follow-ups and monthly reviews.

## Basis

During the December 2, 2025 private discussion, both Rami and Oren characterized Alex's operating style. This is significant because it represents a shared assessment by two people with different perspectives and motivations, not a unilateral complaint. The assessment is reinforced by Rami's December 5 frustration debrief, where Alex's behavior in the planning session (challenging methodology rather than engaging with strategy) further illustrates the advisory-not-operational pattern.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-12-02: Rami and Oren jointly assess Alex as "essentially an advisor, not an operator"
- 2025-12-02: Both note Alex "doesn't follow through — he'll suggest things and hope they happen"
- 2025-12-04: Alex challenges Rami's calculations in planning session but doesn't provide operational direction
- Pattern: Alex engages at the challenge/question level but not at the execution/follow-through level

## Impact

This assumption directly explains the CEO vacuum that synthesis/Rami De Facto CEO While Rejecting the Title describes. If the person formally in the CEO-equivalent position operates as an advisor, operational leadership defaults to whoever fills the gap — currently Rami (clinical/innovation) and Dekkel (operational execution). Understanding Alex's actual operating mode is essential for designing the restructured governance: a steering committee must account for the fact that the board-level figure may challenge but not drive execution.
