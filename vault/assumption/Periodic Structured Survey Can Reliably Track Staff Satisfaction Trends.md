# Periodic Structured Survey Can Reliably Track Staff Satisfaction Trends

## Claim

A periodic staff satisfaction survey with structured dimensions (financial, workload, team cohesion, technology, management support) and segmentation variables (role type, hours, shift pattern, tenure) will produce reliable, measurable data that enables tracking improvement over time. The team assumes clinical staff will respond honestly and consistently across repeated administrations, and that the survey instrument will capture the nuances already surfaced in qualitative interviews.

## Basis

Rami directed Rivi to transform qualitative burnout and satisfaction findings into academic-grade research. The survey is the primary measurement tool. Multiple tasks were created to build the instrument with specific dimensions and segmentation variables. The assumption is that moving from rich qualitative conversations (where staff freely shared about burnout, isolation, and frustration) to a structured questionnaire will preserve the signal quality.

## Evidence Trail

- 2026-02-18: Rami directed academic methodology approach; survey instrument tasked
- Pending: No pilot test or validation of the instrument has been conducted yet

## Impact

If this assumption holds, the innovation team gains a repeatable metric for staff experience that can justify interventions and demonstrate ROI. If it fails — due to survey fatigue, social desirability bias, small sample size, or loss of qualitative richness — the team may make decisions based on incomplete data while believing they have measurement rigor. The qualitative interviews revealed depth (e.g., "orphaned children" metaphor, residency comparison) that structured scales may not capture.
