# Dar Household Naming Convention Predates Modern Surnames

## Claim

All three source notes consistently use the term "Dar Shaddad" (دار شدّاد) — meaning "the Shaddad household" — to describe the family unit before it acquired the Khouri surname. The "Dar" (دار, literally "house/household") naming convention was the primary identifier for extended family clans in the Levant before modern surnames became fixed. The transition from "Dar Shaddad" to "Dar Khouri" — triggered by a family member serving as a priest in Rameh — illustrates how occupational roles replaced clan identifiers as family surnames.

## Basis

All three research notes independently use "Dar Shaddad" as the pre-Khouri family identifier. Note 1: "The Shaddad family ('Dar Shaddad') moved to Hauran." Note 2: "The Shaddad family ('Dar Shaddad') left their original location around 1725." Note 3: "The Shaddad family (Dar Shaddad) left their original location." This consistency across separately authored summaries suggests Majdi Shaddad used this term deliberately as the historical family identifier.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-12-02: Majdi Shaddad uses "Dar Shaddad" consistently in oral history, with "Dar Khouri" emerging only after priestly role in Rameh.

## Impact

This assumption matters for genealogical research methodology: records predating the Khouri surname should be searched under "Shaddad" or "Dar Shaddad." The "Dar" convention also suggests that other Galilee Christian families may have similarly transitioned from clan names to occupational surnames, opening comparative research avenues. Understanding this naming transition is essential for tracing the family in Ottoman-era records, church registers, or tax documents where the Khouri name would not yet exist.
