# Extended Daily Session Hours Degrade Clinical Empathy and Attentiveness

## Claim

Beyond the subjective experience of burnout, longer daily telepsychiatry session blocks measurably degrade the clinical quality of care — specifically, clinicians report significantly reduced empathy and attentiveness at higher daily hours. This is a patient care quality concern, distinct from the staff wellbeing concern captured in burnout findings.

## Basis

A case manager explicitly noted feeling "much more empathetic and attentive" at 5 hours versus 9 hours of work. Rivi Idelman corroborated from personal experience that empathy and attentiveness are "noticeably better" at lower daily hours. This suggests a dose-response relationship between daily session hours and clinical care quality, not just clinician comfort.

## Evidence Trail

- 2026-02-18: Case manager self-report — empathy and attentiveness significantly better at 5 vs 9 hours
- 2026-02-18: Rivi personal observation — attentiveness degrades with longer daily blocks
- Corroborated by psychiatrist reports of finding shorter daily blocks more sustainable

## Impact

If empathy and attentiveness degrade with longer sessions, this is not just a staff retention issue — it is a clinical quality and patient safety issue. This assumption, if confirmed, would support policy changes to cap daily session lengths to preserve care quality, independent of any burnout or staff satisfaction argument.
