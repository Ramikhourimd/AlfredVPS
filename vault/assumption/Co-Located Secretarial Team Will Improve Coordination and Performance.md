## Assumption

Physical co-location of the secretarial and administrative team in a dedicated Haifa office will meaningfully improve operational coordination, team cohesion, and performance.

## Basis

- The secretarial team handles time-sensitive, interdependent workflows (scheduling, patient coordination, follow-ups)
- Remote work creates communication delays and coordination gaps in real-time operations
- Supervision and coaching are more effective in-person for operational roles
- Team building and informal knowledge sharing happen more naturally in co-located settings

## Evidence Supporting

- Common in healthcare operations where real-time coordination is critical
- The team discussed specific coordination challenges attributable to remote distribution
- [[person/Dekkel Taliaz]] and others expressed strong conviction about co-location benefits

## Risks If Wrong

- Office lease becomes a sunk cost if performance improvement is marginal
- Employees accustomed to remote work may resist or leave
- Commute burden may reduce effective working hours
- Hybrid arrangements may negate some co-location benefits

## Validation Approach

- Compare operational metrics (response times, error rates, coordination quality) before and after co-location
- Survey team satisfaction and perceived coordination quality
- Track attendance patterns and actual office utilization
- Review 3 months after move-in to assess impact
