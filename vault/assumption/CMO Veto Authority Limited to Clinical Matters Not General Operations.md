# CMO Veto Authority Limited to Clinical Matters Not General Operations

## Claim

The emerging consensus from the Feb 22 meeting is that the CMO will have veto authority specifically on clinical matters — not general operational authority over the clinic. Oren's framing positions the CMO as providing "clinical oversight and veto on medical matters specifically" while a COO manages operational consistency. This is a narrower scope than Rami's preferred model of combined clinical-operational authority.

## Basis

The meeting note records an "emerging consensus (not fully agreed)" where the CMO "ensures medical standards, has veto on clinical matters, reviews all contracts, oversees medical directors at each clinic" while the COO "manages operational consistency, shared services, KPI tracking across clinics." This formulation explicitly limits the CMO veto to clinical matters rather than granting broad operational authority. Rami's position (medical decisions permeate everything, therefore CMO needs operational authority) was heard but not adopted as the consensus position.

## Evidence Trail

- 2026-02-22: Emerging consensus listed in meeting note Section 4 — CMO at company level with veto on clinical matters, COO for operational consistency.

## Impact

This assumption is central to resolving the Combined vs Separated CMO-COO Authority contradiction. If this assumption holds, Rami's formal authority will be bounded to clinical domains, and influence over operational decisions will flow through the steering committee rather than direct line authority. This significantly shapes Rami's role definition task and may create ongoing friction if operational decisions with clinical implications fall into a grey zone between CMO veto scope and COO operational authority.
