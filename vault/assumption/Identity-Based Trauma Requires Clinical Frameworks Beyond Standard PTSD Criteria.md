# Identity-Based Trauma Requires Clinical Frameworks Beyond Standard PTSD Criteria

## Claim

Standard PTSD diagnostic frameworks (DSM-5 criteria) may be insufficient for capturing identity-based trauma — cases where the primary traumatic mechanism is forced identity betrayal rather than physical danger or threat to life. This form of trauma is particularly prevalent among Arab minorities in Israel who serve in the military or work in identity-conflicting contexts.

## Basis

Two independent recordings describe cases where trauma stems not from combat or physical threat but from identity rupture:
- A Bedouin soldier developed PTSD with flashbacks after being assigned to guard Al-Aqsa Mosque — a holy Muslim site — on behalf of the Israeli military. His trauma was identity dissonance, not combat exposure.
- Druze military veterans experience "compounded" PTSD because military service creates a fundamental identity conflict: serving a state that does not fully accept them while being alienated from their own community. The trauma is "doubled" by identity betrayal.
- The "nowhere identity" concept — "I am neither with the Arabs nor with the Jews" — describes individuals who are structurally positioned between communities, accepted by neither fully.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-09-21: Rami's social conversation analysis describes Druze/Bedouin post-military identity crisis and compounded PTSD
- 2025-11-13: Conference recording presents clinical Bedouin PTSD case with Al-Aqsa guarding as trigger, explicitly framing it as identity-based rather than combat-based
- Existing vault assumption: DSM may not be sufficient as standalone diagnostic framework (already captured from AI diagnostic pipeline findings)

## Impact

- AI diagnostic agents prompted with standard DSM criteria may miss identity-based trauma dimensions
- Clinical training for Telia'z staff (particularly for Arab, Druze, and Bedouin patients) may need supplementary frameworks
- The contextual priming finding in the AI pipeline may reflect this gap — psychiatrists who add contextual PTSD diagnoses may be capturing identity-based trauma that pure DSM criteria miss
- Future research papers could explore identity-based trauma as a distinct clinical phenomenon
