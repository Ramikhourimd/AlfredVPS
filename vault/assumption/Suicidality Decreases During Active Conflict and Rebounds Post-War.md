# Suicidality Decreases During Active Conflict and Rebounds Post-War

## Claim

During active military conflict, suicidality rates decrease — patients and populations are mobilized, focused on survival, and connected to collective purpose. After the conflict ends, suicidality rebounds as the mobilization effect dissipates, trauma surfaces, and meaning structures collapse.

## Basis

A Rambam Medical Center researcher described this hypothesis during a psychiatric conference on 2025-11-13. They are analyzing all ER psychiatric visits from the past two years at Rambam, examining pre-war vs. war patterns to test this theory. The hypothesis aligns with established Durkheimian sociology (social integration during crisis reduces anomic suicide) and has precedent in wartime psychiatry literature.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-11-13: Rambam researcher presents hypothesis at conference, with active ER data analysis underway
- Rambam dataset covers two years of ER psychiatric visits — pre-October 7 and post-October 7 periods

## Impact

Directly relevant to Telia'z post-October 7 patient cohort analysis. If suicidality rebounds post-conflict, the clinic should expect a delayed wave of acute presentations as the war context recedes. This affects both clinical preparedness (on-call protocols, risk assessment) and research framing (Paper 1 timing analysis should account for this pattern when interpreting symptom trajectories).
