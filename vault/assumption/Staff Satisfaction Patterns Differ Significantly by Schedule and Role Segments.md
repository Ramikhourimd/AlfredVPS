# Staff Satisfaction Patterns Differ Significantly by Schedule and Role Segments

## Claim

Staff satisfaction, burnout, and work experience at Telia'z vary meaningfully across specific subgroups — particularly by role type (psychiatrist vs. case manager), weekly hours worked, shift pattern (morning vs. evening vs. weekend), active caseload size, and tenure. The team designs every measurement instrument around these segmentation variables, implying they expect these dimensions to reveal actionable differences rather than uniform trends.

## Basis

Three independent survey design tasks all specify the same segmentation variables: role type, hours per week, shift patterns, caseload, and tenure. This consistency across task descriptions authored at different points indicates a deliberate belief that these variables mediate satisfaction differently. Qualitative evidence supports this — case managers describe burnout differently from psychiatrists (intensity vs. difficult patients), and the 6-8 hour threshold applies differently across roles.

## Evidence Trail

- 2026-02-18: Rami directs survey segmentation by role, shift, hours, caseload, tenure (task/Build Staff Satisfaction Research Questionnaire)
- 2026-02-18: Second task record independently specifies same variables with "enabling correlation analysis" (task/Build Structured Employee Satisfaction Survey Instrument)
- 2026-02-18: Third task record confirms same dimensional structure (task/Build Employee Satisfaction Survey Instrument)
- 2026-02-18: Qualitative data shows different burnout patterns by role — case managers report intensity-per-hour exhaustion, psychiatrists report frustration with difficult patients and lack of support (note/Employee Experience Research and System Prototype Planning 2026-02-18)

## Impact

If this assumption is correct, interventions can be targeted by segment (e.g., different hour caps for psychiatrists vs. case managers, shift-specific support). If incorrect, the survey may produce fragmented data without clear actionable patterns, wasting analytical effort on segmentation that reveals no meaningful differences.
