# Suicidal Risk Assessment Is Always a Point-in-Time Clinical Snapshot

## Claim

There is no clinical tool that reliably predicts future suicidal danger. Suicidal risk can only be assessed at a specific point in time (b'nekudat zman). Clinicians can attempt prediction based on risk factors (gormei sakana), but the assessment is fundamentally a snapshot — not a forecast. This shapes the clinic's documentation and liability approach: the goal is to demonstrate maximum assessment effort at the moment of evaluation, not to predict future behavior.

## Basis

Rami Khouri articulated this principle during a clinical supervision call on 2025-12-01 ([[note/Clinical Supervision Suicidal Risk Assessment Protocols]]). He referenced the McKree legal precedent (USA) where clinicians were found liable for failing to adequately assess immediate danger — the legal standard is whether the clinician did the maximum to assess at that point in time, not whether they predicted the outcome.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-12-01: Rami stated explicitly during supervision that "immediate danger can only be assessed at a specific point in time" and "there is no clinical tool that reliably predicts future danger"
- This principle directly informed the five-level assessment protocol (ideation → thoughts → intention → plan → act) — each level must be assessed and documented at the time of evaluation

## Impact

- Documentation standards: Every assessed risk factor must be documented; if a symptom was not assessed, the clinician must document why
- Legal protection: The standard is demonstrating maximum effort, not accuracy of prediction
- Training implications: Case managers and psychiatrists must understand this is a snapshot, reducing anxiety about "missing" future events while increasing rigor about present-moment assessment
- Informs the decision that case manager role does NOT include ruling out suicidality — that remains the psychiatrist's domain because it requires this specialized assessment framework
