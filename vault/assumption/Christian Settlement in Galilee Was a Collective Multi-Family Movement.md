# Christian Settlement in Galilee Was a Collective Multi-Family Movement

## Claim

The migration from the Palestinian coast to inland Galilee villages around 1865 was not a single-family event but a collective movement involving multiple Christian families simultaneously. All three source notes describe the settlers arriving at six or more Galilee villages at roughly the same time (Suata, Rameh, Eilabun/Mi'ilya, Maghar, Safed, Tiberias), and the language used is consistently plural — "Christians," "they settled," "coastal Christian communities" — indicating a demographic wave rather than an isolated family relocation.

## Basis

Note 1: "Circumstances forced the coastal Christian communities to move inland to Galilee villages. They settled in: Suata, Rameh, Eilabun, Maghar, Safed, Tiberias." Note 2: "Changing circumstances pushed the coastal Christian traders into the interior Galilee villages." Note 3: "Circumstances forced coastal families to move inland to the Galilee villages." The consistent use of plural "families" and "communities" indicates the Shaddad family's movement was part of a larger collective migration.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-12-02: Majdi Shaddad describes the migration in collective terms, situating the Shaddad family within a broader movement of Christian families.
- The simultaneous founding of a church in Maghar (~1860-1865) and settlement across multiple villages supports coordinated community movement.

## Impact

This assumption is significant for genealogy research because it means other Christian families in these Galilee villages likely share the same migration trajectory (Lebanon → coast → inland). Cross-referencing Shaddad records with other family histories from the same villages (Rameh, Eilabun, Maghar) could yield corroborating evidence or reveal shared migration networks. It also suggests that church records, land registries, and Ottoman tax documents from ~1865 in any of these villages might contain references to the Shaddad family alongside other arriving families.
