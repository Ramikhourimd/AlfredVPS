# Rami Clinical Self-Identity Is Therapeutic-Reactive Not Directive-Preventive

## Claim

Rami Khouri explicitly describes his clinical self-identity as therapeutic rather than preventive or directive: "I work in a therapeutic setting, not a preventive one. People come to me with problems, I treat them. I don't tell anyone what to do." This self-framing distinguishes him from an administrative or directive leadership style — he sees himself fundamentally as a clinician who responds to presented problems rather than someone who proactively structures or directs others' behavior.

## Basis

During the Telia'z Company Fun Day on 2025-09-21, in casual banter about health profession dynamics, Rami gave this self-description unprompted. The statement was made in a social context (not a formal meeting), suggesting it reflects his authentic self-perception rather than a strategic positioning.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-09-21: Explicit self-description at Company Fun Day: "I work in a therapeutic setting, not a preventive one"
- This aligns with his stated preference for "managing goals, not people" (captured in separate assumption)
- His resistance to the CEO title (preferring CMO or clinical authority) is consistent with this therapeutic-reactive identity

## Impact

This self-framing has direct implications for the restructuring role debates:
1. **Supports CMO over COO**: A therapeutic-reactive identity aligns with clinical authority (CMO) rather than operational command (COO)
2. **Explains resistance to directive roles**: Rami's reluctance to take on pure management roles stems not just from capacity constraints but from clinical identity — he does not see himself as someone who "tells people what to do"
3. **Informs delegation model**: His preference for hiring competent managers and setting goals (rather than directing) follows naturally from this clinical self-concept
4. **Risk factor**: If placed in a directive operational role, the identity mismatch may accelerate burnout beyond what workload alone would predict
