# Group Supervision Reduces Telepsychiatry Staff Burnout

## Claim

Group supervision sessions (hadracha) have a measurable positive effect on reducing clinical staff burnout. At least one case manager reported that burnout "noticeably decreased" after peer supervision sessions began. The effect may stem from the group contact itself, the supervision content, or both — the causal mechanism is unclear.

## Basis

Multiple staff interviews conducted by Rivi Idelman in February 2026 surfaced this pattern. One case manager explicitly linked the start of group forums to a reduction in burnout perception, though she was unsure whether it was the group dynamic or the clinical supervision content that helped.

## Evidence Trail

- 2026-02-18: Case manager reported burnout suddenly decreased once peer supervision sessions started
- Effect observed alongside broader burnout findings — staff working alone from home report significant isolation
- Group supervision may counteract isolation as much as it provides clinical support

## Impact

If confirmed, this assumption supports expanding group supervision frequency and investing in peer-support structures. It also suggests that combating isolation (not just providing clinical guidance) may be a key lever for retention. Decisions about scheduling, team-building initiatives, and the planned internal task system should account for this.
