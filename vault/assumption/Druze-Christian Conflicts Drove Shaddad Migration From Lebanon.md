# Druze-Christian Conflicts Drove Shaddad Migration From Lebanon

## Claim

Sectarian conflicts between Druze and Christian communities in Mount Lebanon during the early-to-mid 1800s were the primary driver that forced the Shaddad family (and other Christian families) to leave Lebanon and migrate southward into coastal Palestine. This is treated as the causal mechanism linking Phase 2 (Lebanon period) to Phase 3 (coastal Palestine settlement) in the family migration timeline.

## Basis

All three conversation records and all three research notes independently cite Druze-Christian conflict as the reason the family left Lebanon. Majdi Shaddad presented this as established family knowledge during the 2025-12-02 discussion. The 1860 Mount Lebanon civil war is a historically documented event that aligns with this oral tradition.

## Evidence Trail

- 2025-12-02: Majdi Shaddad described conflicts "beginning around the early 1800s" that "forced many Christians to migrate southward into Palestine"
- Multiple source records consistently reference this as the migration trigger
- Historical record of 1860 Mount Lebanon civil war provides partial corroboration of the general narrative

## Impact

This assumption anchors the entire Phase 2→Phase 3 transition in the migration timeline. If the actual migration drivers were different (economic, political, or other), the chronology and narrative of the family history would need revision. The assumption also connects the Shaddad family story to broader Levantine Christian migration patterns that could be cross-referenced with historical scholarship.
