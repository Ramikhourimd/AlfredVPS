# Distributed Staff Share Common Struggles Without Visibility Into Each Other's Experiences

## Constraint

Clinical staff working remotely at Telia'z experience common challenges — burnout, isolation, difficult patients, unanswered messages — but do not know that their colleagues face the same struggles. Staff explicitly reported that "things they struggle with, others also experience, but nobody knows because they don't see each other's messages in the groups." This information asymmetry is distinct from isolation itself: staff are not just separated from each other, they are invisible to each other.

## Source

Qualitative staff interviews conducted by Rivi Idelman, reported during the 2026-02-18 working session. Multiple staff members independently described this pattern of invisible shared experience.

## Implications

This constraint compounds the isolation effect: not only do staff work alone, they believe their struggles are unique to them. This prevents collective problem-solving, reduces motivation to raise issues (because they assume it is personal), and undermines the sense of shared professional identity. The partial success of group supervision in reducing burnout may be partly explained by its function as a visibility mechanism — staff discover that others share their experiences. Solving this requires transparency mechanisms (shared dashboards, regular experience-sharing forums, anonymized feedback loops) rather than just more communication channels.

## Expiry / Review

Should be reassessed after group supervision is fully rolled out and after any transparency mechanisms are introduced. If staff begin to report awareness of shared challenges, this constraint may shift from active to mitigated.
