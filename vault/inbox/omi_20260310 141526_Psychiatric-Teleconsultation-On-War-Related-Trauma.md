## Summary

This is a remote psychiatric consultation between Dr. Rami Khouri and Avishai, who is coping with severe anxiety and trauma symptoms following his experience at the Nova music festival on October 7. Avishai describes being vegan, losing many friends in the attack, having friends kidnapped to Gaza, and suffering his first-ever panic attack in the weeks after, leading to a psychiatric referral and weekly follow-ups at HaEmek Hospital in Afula. He reports ongoing panic attacks, flashbacks during sirens, physical anxiety symptoms, nightmares, depressive episodes, and reliance on medications such as Clonex (clonazepam) and Bondormin for acute relief. He also details frustration and disappointment with prior psychological and psychiatric care, feeling misunderstood, stigmatized, and treated coldly in the public system, which has discouraged him from seeking consistent help.

Dr. Khouri normalizes Avishai’s reactions as a natural post-traumatic response to the ongoing war and recurrent triggers like sirens and news. He explains how the brain is reactivating the original Nova trauma and emphasizes that meaningful recovery will require structured psychological therapy, not just medications. He advises reducing exposure to news/Telegram, avoiding being alone during symptom spikes, and spending time around other people for nervous system regulation. He stresses that therapy success should not be judged by immediate relief and compares it to a necessary but painful operation. Despite Avishai’s skepticism and past negative experiences, the doctor strongly encourages him to give psychotherapy another chance and to build a therapeutic relationship primarily with a psychologist rather than relying solely on psychiatrists as anchors.

Regarding medication, Avishai lists many drugs he has already tried (including Seroquel and others) and expresses worry about dependence on pills, though he continues to use Clonex and Bondormin as needed during acute distress. Dr. Khouri proposes a new treatment plan divided into three components: (1) a daily antidepressant similar to Cipralex but with fewer side effects (Melustran 50 mg each morning); (2) continuation of Clonex as an SOS medication, recommending 2 mg tablets with instructions to take half a tablet (1 mg) up to twice a day to avoid habituation; and (3) Bondormin for night-time use. He documents these recommendations in a letter to Avishai’s family doctor so the prescriptions can be issued. The session ends with reassurance that Avishai’s current state is temporary, encouragement to stay engaged in treatment even when symptoms ease, and wishes for a full recovery.
