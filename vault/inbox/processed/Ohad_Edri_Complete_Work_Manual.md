## 11. OPTICA Overview

**OPTICA** (Organizational PerspecTIve Checklist for AI solutions adoption) is Taliaz's disciplined, stage-gated framework for evaluating and deploying clinical AI solutions. The framework was established in the February 19, 2026, Innovation Team meeting and provides a structured pathway for innovation, ensuring that projects are clinically relevant, technically feasible, and strategically aligned.

> **Guiding Principle:** *"Quality > speed. Stop early on showstoppers. Document everything."*

The framework consists of 8 stages (mapped to 13 granular steps labeled A through M), organized into four phases:

| Phase | Steps | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | A (Clinical Need), B (Alternatives) | Is this the right problem? |
| **Feasibility** | C (Population), D (Input Data), E (Output Data) | Can this work in our environment? |
| **Validation** | F (Build + Validate), G (Performance), H (Explainability), I (Regulation/Security/Privacy) | Does the solution work as intended? |
| **Integration** | J (Deployment Plan), K (Monitoring Plan), L (Evaluation Plan), M (Strategy Fit) | How do we deploy and manage this responsibly? |

## 14. Detailed OPTICA Playbook

This section provides Ohad's step-by-step playbook for his four **owned** OPTICA steps and his key primary contributions.

### Step B: Alternatives (OWNER)

As the owner of this step, Ohad leads the analysis to determine if AI is genuinely the best solution. His technical expertise is crucial for a rigorous and unbiased assessment.

**Key Action:** Create the **Alternatives Matrix**. Research and document all viable options, including: (1) simple process changes, (2) rule-based software (non-AI), (3) off-the-shelf commercial AI tools, and (4) building a custom AI solution. For each, estimate cost, timeline, complexity, and risk.

**Collaborators:** R&D Team (for technical feasibility), Product Team (for market analysis).

**Taliaz-Specific Tip:** Leverage knowledge of the existing Taliaz tech stack. Could a simpler solution be built with existing tools before committing to a full-blown AI project?

### Step D: Input Data (OWNER)

This is one of Ohad's most critical ownership areas. A brilliant model is useless if it cannot get the data it needs in the real world.

**Key Action:** Create the **Data Fit Assessment** document. This involves a deep dive into data sources (Xano, BigQuery, EMRs) to assess: (1) **Availability** — Can we get the required data at the point of care? (2) **Quality** — How much missing or inconsistent data is there? (3) **Format** — Is the data structured, unstructured, etc.? (4) **Shift** — Is there a risk of data drift between training data and live production data?

**Collaborators:** Data Team Lead, BI Analysts.

**Taliaz-Specific Tip:** Pay special attention to the challenges of processing Hebrew and Arabic text from clinical notes. This is a common stumbling block.

### Step F: Build + Validate (OWNER)

This is where Ohad's core development skills come into play. He owns the entire process of building, validating, and documenting the AI model itself.

**Key Action:** Produce the **Model Card**. This central technical document must include: (1) the model architecture, (2) the training data used, (3) detailed performance metrics (including by subgroups), (4) the results of bias audits, and (5) the explainability methods used.

**Collaborators:** R&D Team, Data Scientists.

**Taliaz-Specific Tip:** All model code must be stored in the GitHub repository, and the final Model Card should be saved to the shared Google Drive for accessibility.

### Step J: Deployment Plan (OWNER)

As the owner of the deployment plan, Ohad is responsible for designing the technical strategy for rolling out the AI solution into the clinical workflow without causing disruption.

**Key Action:** Create the **Deployment + Monitoring Plan**. This technical document must specify: (1) the target infrastructure (e.g., Taliaz cloud, specific dashboards), (2) the rollout strategy (e.g., pilot at one clinic first), (3) the technical monitoring plan for tracking model performance and drift, and (4) the training and support plan for clinicians.

**Taliaz-Specific Tip:** The plan must include a detailed rollback strategy. If the model causes unforeseen problems, how do we disable it quickly and safely?
