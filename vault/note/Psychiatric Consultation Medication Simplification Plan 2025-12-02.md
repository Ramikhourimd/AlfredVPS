## Current Medication Discussion

### Medications Under Review
| Medication | Status | Rami's Recommendation |
|------------|--------|----------------------|
| Seroquel (quetiapine) | Recently reduced by facility psychiatrist at patient's request | **Increase** — primary mood stabilizer, should be the cornerstone of treatment |
| Zyprexa (olanzapine) | Currently prescribed | **Gradually taper and discontinue** — overlaps with Seroquel's function |
| Antidepressant ("L" — likely escitalopram/Cipralex) | Currently prescribed | **Discontinue** — not appropriate for this patient's condition; patient needs a stabilizer, not an antidepressant |
| Resital (mentioned by patient) | Patient claims never received | Patient unfamiliar with this medication; Rami clarified both it and Cipralex are antidepressants, not what the patient needs |

### Clinical Rationale

Rami's core recommendation is **medication consolidation to a single mood stabilizer** (Seroquel) to minimize polypharmacy side effects:

1. **Seroquel is the right drug** — acts as both mood stabilizer and antipsychotic; patient's facility psychiatrist reduced it at the patient's request, which Rami disagrees with
2. **Zyprexa overlaps** with Seroquel's mechanism; having both creates unnecessary side effect burden
3. **Antidepressant is contraindicated** — for bipolar/schizoaffective presentations, antidepressants without adequate mood stabilizer coverage can destabilize mood
4. **Target: one stabilizer, fewer side effects** — Rami explicitly stated the goal is to reduce the patient from three psychotropic medications to one primary stabilizer

### Orthostatic Hypotension

Rami identified that the patient's dizziness when standing is a known side effect of Seroquel (orthostatic hypotension — blood pressure drop from lying to standing position). He recommended blood pressure monitoring in supine and upright positions. This is a manageable side effect and should not be the reason to discontinue Seroquel.

## Consultation Boundaries

Rami explicitly established several important clinical boundaries during this session:

1. **No direct prescribing:** Cannot prescribe medications because the patient already has a treating psychiatrist at the facility. Dual treatment creates confusion and is clinically inappropriate.
2. **Recommendation letter:** Will write a detailed recommendation letter to the facility psychiatrist outlining the proposed medication changes
3. **Letter delivered to patient:** Patient will physically receive the letter to hand to the facility doctor
4. **Patient cooperation urged:** Rami asked the patient to cooperate with the facility psychiatrist despite his distrust, giving the treating doctor "benefit of the doubt." Addressed patient's concerns about possible racial bias from the facility psychiatrist and encouraged the patient to focus on medication compliance rather than perceived slights.
