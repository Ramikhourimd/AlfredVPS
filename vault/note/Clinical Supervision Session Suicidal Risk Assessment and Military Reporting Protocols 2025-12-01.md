# Clinical Supervision Session: Suicidal Risk Assessment and Military Reporting Protocols (2025-12-01)

## Context

Phone-based clinical supervision session between Rami Khouri (psychiatrist, CMO at Telia'z) and a clinic case manager/clinician referred to as "Lina." Lina sought urgent guidance regarding a patient — a military-affiliated individual — who had previously expressed suicidal thoughts. Lina was unable to see the patient for a follow-up and was anxious about the appropriate course of action.

## Key Clinical Guidance

### 1. Immediate Danger Assessment (Sakana Miyedit)

Rami emphasized that danger assessment is always **point-in-time** — clinicians can only evaluate immediate danger at the moment of examination, not predict future risk with certainty. There are no reliable tools for forward-looking danger prediction; only risk factor analysis (gormei sakana) can provide probabilistic guidance.

The structured assessment scale:
- **Suicidal ideation** (general thoughts about death)
- **Suicidal thoughts** (specific thoughts about ending one's life)
- **Intention** (desire to act)
- **Plan** (concrete method and timeline)
- **Act** (attempt or active preparation)

Each level must be assessed and documented. If a level was not assessed, the clinician must explain why and document their clinical impression of whether the symptom is present.

### 2. Documentation Requirements (Tiud)

Rami stressed the importance of thorough documentation for legal protection, referencing the **McKee case** (US precedent) where clinicians were held liable for inadequate assessment.

Key documentation principles:
- Document what was assessed and the findings
- If a symptom was not directly assessed, document the clinical impression and reasoning (e.g., "no behavior indicative of this symptom was observed")
- The record (rishum) does not need to be exclusively based on patient self-report — clinician observations count
- The problematic scenario is when a symptom is neither assessed nor mentioned at all
- If something was not assessed, attempt to assess it in a follow-up, or document why assessment was not possible

### 3. Self-Harm vs. Suicidality Differentiation

Rami distinguished between two sources of danger:

| Type | Source | Predictability | Clinical Approach |
|------|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Suicidality (Avdanot)** | Depression — gradual escalation | More predictable — look for farewell behaviors, account closures, giving away possessions | Can identify warning signs days in advance |
| **Self-harm (Pgi'a Atzmit)** | Personality disorder — impulsive | Very difficult to predict — can happen at any moment | Cannot reliably say "danger in 2-3 days" |

Understanding the source of the risk changes the clinical management approach. Clinicians must attempt to identify whether the behavior pattern is depressive (gradual) or personality-driven (impulsive).

### 4. Rami's Assessment of This Case

Based on what Lina described, Rami assessed **no immediate danger** at the time of Lina's last examination. However, he identified that Lina's current anxiety was driven by her inability to see the patient recently — a gap in follow-up — rather than a clinical deterioration she had observed.

Rami helped Lina distinguish between:
- **Clinical concern** — based on observed deterioration (which would require immediate action)
- **Personal anxiety** — based on not being able to verify the patient's current state (which requires arranging follow-up, not emergency intervention)

He clarified that if she had felt immediate danger during her last assessment, she should have acted then. Since she did not, the appropriate current action is to arrange close follow-up monitoring, not retroactive emergency measures.

### 5. Military Reporting Protocols (Israel-Specific)

The patient's military affiliation determines the reporting channel:

| Status | Hebrew | Reporting Channel | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------------------|-------|
| **Active duty soldier (Hayal Sadir)** | חייל סדיר | Part of military medical system (Ma'arechet Tsva'it). Can contact military psychiatrist (Kaban) or commander directly | Clinician can push for weapon removal and urgent assessment through military chain |
| **Reservist on active duty (Keva/Konenut)** | כונניות/קבע | Part of military system — same as active duty | Military psychiatrist and Rofeh Tsva'i are accessible |
| **Discharged/civilian** | | Cannot report to employer without patient consent | Can only work through the patient's therapist (Metapelet) directly |

**Key distinction:** If the patient is within the military system, the clinician can and should contact the military chain (Kaban, Mefaked, Psikhi'ater Tsva'i) to ensure urgent assessment. If outside the military system, confidentiality rules apply and reporting to external parties requires patient consent.

### 6. Concrete Action Plan Given to Lina

1. Contact the patient's therapist (Metapelet) immediately
2. Verify whether the patient is active military (Hayal Sadir) or reserve/civilian
3. If military: Contact the officer (Kaban) or military psychiatrist to arrange urgent assessment
4. Communicate clearly: "I assessed the patient — no immediate danger at the time, but there is potential risk. He holds a weapon, had prior suicidal thoughts, and needs close psychiatric follow-up as soon as possible."
5. If any signs of deterioration (Dirdur) emerge — take to emergency room (Miyon) immediately
6. Document everything thoroughly — assessment findings, clinical reasoning, actions taken
7. Stay available in the loop for ongoing consultation (Rami offered continued guidance)

## Broader Clinical Teaching Points

- **Duty to assess is the maximum standard** — clinicians cannot predict outcomes, but they can demonstrate they performed a thorough assessment
- **Point-in-time limitation** — suicidal risk assessment only reflects the moment of examination
- **Risk factors inform prediction, not assessment** — risk factors help estimate probability but do not constitute a clinical finding
- **Weapon access** is a critical risk amplifier that warrants urgent action regardless of other factors
- **The clinician's anxiety is not the patient's emergency** — distinguishing personal worry from clinical evidence is essential for appropriate response
