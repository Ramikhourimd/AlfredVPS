# Psychiatric Session — Antidepressant Medication Switch 2025-12-02

## Presenting Complaint

The patient described recurring episodes of a dissociative and depressive nature — feeling disconnected from their surroundings, a sense that things "weren't like this before," and entering states of emotional withdrawal. The episodes are not constant but recur periodically. The patient confirmed this pattern has not been lifelong but developed over time.

## Current Medication Assessment

- **Sertraline 50mg** — the patient has been on this dose for years
- **Previous trial of Cipralex** (escitalopram) — also in the SSRI family, also lost efficacy
- **Mondormin** — used as needed for sleep (partially effective)
- **Clonazepam (Clonex)** — used as needed for calming/anxiety (not regular)
- Patient is concurrently receiving **psychological therapy**

## Clinical Reasoning

Rami determined that the patient's body has habituated to the SSRI pharmacological family (both Sertraline and Cipralex are SSRIs — selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors). Research supports that when patients stop responding to SSRIs, switching to a medication with a different mechanism of action can "refresh" the therapeutic response.

The recommended switch is to **Venlafaxine** — an SNRI (serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitor) which acts on both serotonin and noradrenaline pathways, providing a different pharmacological approach.

## Medication Transition Plan

| Step | Duration | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Week 1 | Reduce Sertraline to 100mg |
| 2 | Week 2 | Reduce Sertraline to 50mg, then discontinue |
| 3 | Washout | Allow a few days for the body to clear the SSRI |
| 4 | Week 3 | Start Venlafaxine 37.5mg |
| 5 | Week 4+ | Increase Venlafaxine to 75mg |

**Concurrent medications to continue:** Mondormin (sleep) and Clonazepam (calming) — as needed, no changes.

## Side Effects Discussed

- **Dry mouth and dry eyes** — possible in the initial period
- **Blood pressure elevation** — theoretical possibility with Venlafaxine, though usually not clinically significant at low doses
- Patient has a history of blood pressure concerns — monitoring recommended
