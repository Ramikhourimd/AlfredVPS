# Moli Lahad Burnout Training Debrief and Recording Policy 2025-11-10

## Context

On 2025-11-09, the first biweekly clinical supervision/training session was held with **Prof. Moli Lahad** — a trauma psychology expert and founder of [[org/Mashabim]] (Community Stress Prevention Centre). The session was attended by case managers and facilitated an open discussion about burnout, self-care, and the unique challenges of remote telepsychiatry work. The following morning (2025-11-10), [[person/Shira]], [[person/Ori Shukron]], and [[person/Rami Khouri]] debriefed on the session and discussed recording policy.

## Training Session Summary

### Content and Format
- Prof. Moli Lahad delivered the first session in what will be a biweekly series for case managers
- Topics covered: burnout recognition, self-care strategies, boundary-setting in remote work, team cohesion
- Included practical exercises including guided meditation
- Interactive format with each participant sharing personal experiences
- Moli Lahad took notes during the session, capturing key themes raised by each participant

### Key Takeaways (Shira's Summary)
1. **Self-care before patient care**: Clinicians must prioritize their own wellbeing before they can effectively help patients
2. **Daily boundary rituals matter**: Unlike in-person clinical work (where removing a uniform, showering, etc. symbolically marks the end of a shift), working from home in telepsychiatry blurs work-life boundaries
3. **Remote work amplifies burnout**: The convenience of working from home is offset by the difficulty of "switching off" — doing laundry mid-session, staying in pajamas, and lacking clear start/end rituals
4. **Presence and emotional safety are essential**: The value of the session is in being there, not in reading a summary afterward

### Team Cohesion Impact
- Ori reported the session strongly united the case manager team
- Described as a "very positive experience" that brought the group together
- Emotional sharing component was a key bonding element

## Recording Policy Debate

### Positions
- **Ori**: Proposed recording sessions and generating anonymized summaries that strip emotional content, keeping only practical tools and techniques. Argued summaries help participants recall practical elements (meditation techniques, rituals, etc.)
- **Rami**: Wanted transcription to analyze recurring team issues, understand what problems have/haven't been addressed, and potentially send thematic summaries to Moli Lahad for targeted follow-up. Offered to build an automated pipeline: transcribe → generate anonymous thematic summary → delete raw transcription
- **Shira**: Expressed strong reservations — the emotional safety of the session depends on participants knowing there is no recording. Commitment to attend (not treating it as optional) is part of the therapeutic value. Written summaries cannot convey the experiential value. Personal note-taking by individuals is the appropriate approach.

### Resolution
Agreed to check with Prof. Moli Lahad and the case managers before making any decision about recording. Key considerations:
- Recording was not in Moli Lahad's original contract
- Need consent from all participants
- Case managers should be asked if they would find post-session summaries useful

## Extending to Psychiatrists

- Ori and Shira both agreed psychiatrists would benefit from similar sessions
- Psychiatrists face the same burnout challenges (remote work, emotional intensity, boundary blurring)
- Discussion about a separate track for psychiatrists — not joining the case manager sessions, but a parallel program

## Ahmad and Suicidal Patient Stress

Brief discussion at the end about [[person/Ahmed Masalha]]'s difficulty handling suicidal patients:
- Ahmad reportedly gets stressed and anxious when dealing with suicidal risk cases
- Referenced a past incident where Ahmad called staff in the middle of the night after a patient stated a specific suicide timeline
- Shira committed to speaking with Ahmad about protocols and support
- Noted that all patients in the clinic are potentially suicidal, so this is not novel territory — Ahmad needs guidance on appropriate clinical response proportionality
