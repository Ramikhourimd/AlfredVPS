# Clinical Supervision: Suicidal Patient Risk Assessment — 2025-12-01

## Context

Rami Khouri received an urgent clinical supervision call from a case manager (referred to as Selina) at Telia'z Clinic Israel. The clinician was seeking guidance about a patient exhibiting suicidal ideation who also has access to a weapon through military service. The call covered risk assessment methodology, documentation requirements, reporting channels, and an immediate action plan.

## Key Clinical Guidance Provided

### 1. Assessing Immediate Danger (Sakana Miyedit)

Rami emphasized that clinical danger assessment is always **point-in-time** — there is no tool that can assess future or past danger, only the state at the moment of examination. The framework for assessment follows a gradient:

- **Suicidal ideation** (machshavot) → **Thoughts** → **Intention** → **Plan** → **Act**

Each element must be assessed. If a symptom was not assessed, the clinician must document **why** and provide their clinical impression of whether it is likely present or absent based on observed behavior.

### 2. Documentation and Legal Protection

Rami referenced the **McKree case** (US legal precedent) where a clinician was held liable for failure to adequately assess danger. The key principle: when there is a concern about immediate danger to self or others, the clinician must demonstrate they did the **maximum** to assess the situation.

Documentation should include:
- What was assessed and findings
- What was **not** assessed and the clinical reasoning for why
- If a symptom was not directly reported, the clinician can note: "There was no behavior that would indicate the presence of this symptom" — this is an acceptable clinical notation
- The **problematic scenario** is when a symptom was neither assessed nor mentioned at all in the record

### 3. Differentiating Types of Suicidality

Rami distinguished between two types of suicidal risk:

- **Depression-driven suicidality (Avdanit):** Gradual escalation with observable warning signs — patient closing accounts, making farewell calls, giving away possessions. This is more predictable and allows intervention.
- **Personality-driven self-harm (Pgia Atzmit):** Impulsive, much harder to predict. This is the more clinically challenging scenario because the patient may act without warning signals.

Rami advised the clinician to consider the **diagnostic picture** — is the recurring pattern coming from a mood disorder or a personality organization? This distinction is highly relevant to what the patient might do.

### 4. Assessing the Clinician's Anxiety

Rami identified that the clinician's current anxiety was partly driven by the fact that she could not see the patient before Friday (a scheduling gap), rather than by a purely clinical assessment of immediate danger. He helped her separate:

- **Clinical assessment at time of last contact:** Patient was not in immediate danger at that point
- **Current anxiety:** Driven by inability to follow up, not by new clinical data
- **Implication:** If she felt at the time of assessment that the patient needed weapon removal, she should have pushed for it then. The current concern is about **potential** danger, which is broader and less specific.

### 5. Military vs. Civilian Reporting Channels

A critical distinction was made based on the patient's military status:

- **Active duty soldier (Chayal Sadir):** Falls under the military health system. The clinician **can** contact the military mental health officer (Kaban) or military psychiatrist directly without patient consent to arrange urgent evaluation.
- **Civilian security / reservist not on active duty:** Does **not** fall under military system. The clinician **cannot** disclose to the employer without patient consent. The only avenue is the patient's treating clinician (Metapelet).

### 6. Immediate Action Plan

Rami instructed the clinician to:

1. **Contact the patient's therapist (Metapelet)** immediately
2. Explain that the patient **requires urgent psychiatric evaluation**
3. If there are **any signs of deterioration**, take the patient to the ER (Miyun)
4. If the patient is active military, coordinate with the military chain of command for close follow-up (Ma'akav Tzamud)
5. Document all actions taken
6. Recommended message to convey: "I assessed the patient — there was no immediate danger at the time of assessment, but there is potential risk. The patient holds a weapon, has had suicidal thoughts before, and needs close psychiatric follow-up as soon as possible."

## Clinical Teaching Points

- Danger assessment is always at a **specific point in time** — never predictive
- Risk prediction uses **risk factors** (gormei sakana), not certainty
- The clinician's duty is to demonstrate they did everything possible to assess and act
- When unsure, err on the side of pushing for evaluation — even a small concern warrants action
- The McKree precedent establishes that failure to assess is worse than assessing and finding nothing
