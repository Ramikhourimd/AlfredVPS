# Psychiatric Evaluation — Residential Patient With Complex Diagnostic Profile 2025-12-02

## Context

Rami Khouri conducted a psychiatric evaluation of a male patient currently living in a residential psychiatric facility. The patient has been in treatment for over 15 years and is taking 21 pills daily. He presented with significant frustration about his treatment, perceived mistreatment by staff, and a desire for medication changes. The session involved a comprehensive review of the patient's psychiatric history, current symptoms, and psychosocial context.

## Patient Profile

- **Setting:** Residential psychiatric facility
- **Duration of illness:** 15+ years of depression
- **Current treating psychiatrist:** Attends weekly at the facility
- **Hospitalization history:** Involuntary psychiatric hospitalization approximately 2004; multiple court-ordered psychiatric observations (10-day periods)
- **Military history:** Reports injury during military service; claims PTSD not officially recognized

## Diagnostic Complexity

### Documented Diagnoses
- **Bipolar disorder** — The treating psychiatrist has diagnosed this as bipolar depression, not unipolar depression. The mood stabilizer (Depakote/Lamictal) is prescribed specifically for this diagnosis.
- **Schizophrenia** — Listed in the patient's chart, but the patient vehemently disputes this. Reports a previous doctor in Holland asked about auditory hallucinations; patient denied then and continues to deny any psychotic symptoms (no hallucinations, no unusual experiences, no grandiosity).

### Patient-Claimed Diagnosis
- **PTSD from military service** — Patient states he was injured in the army and suffers from post-traumatic stress. He says this is not officially recognized.

### Rami's Assessment
- Rami noted the bipolar disorder diagnosis and explained to the patient that his condition is not "regular depression" but bipolar, which is why standard antidepressants alone are insufficient.
- Rami acknowledged the PTSD claim but also noted the schizophrenia diagnosis in the chart, and attempted to differentiate whether current distress is reactive (from perceived mistreatment) or represents an exacerbation of the psychiatric illness.
- Rami screened for psychotic symptoms: patient denied auditory hallucinations, unusual experiences, and grandiosity throughout his life.

## Current Medication Regimen (21 pills/day total)

| Medication | Class | Dose/Schedule | Notes |
|------------|-------|---------------|-------|
| Cymbalta (Duloxetine) | SNRI | Half tablet morning, half tablet evening | Antidepressant |
| Depakote (Divalproex) | Mood stabilizer | Not specified | Patient considers this an "old drug from 40 years ago" and wants newer alternatives |
| Paxil (Paroxetine) | SSRI | Dose being reduced by treating psychiatrist | Patient wants more, treating doctor is reducing |
| Clonex (Clonazepam) | Benzodiazepine | As needed | Patient wants more; Rami warned about memory impairment |

## Perceived Mistreatment and Ethnic Discrimination

A significant portion of the session focused on the patient's perception of mistreatment at the residential facility:

- **Staff ignoring him:** Patient describes being ignored by social workers and other staff. Says young social workers approach him once, introduce themselves, then never return.
- **Ethnic isolation:** Patient identifies as the only "Israeli" (Sephardi/Mizrahi) resident among predominantly Ashkenazi residents. He perceives this ethnic difference as a possible factor in his treatment by staff.
- **Rami's exploration:** Rami probed whether the patient is certain the mistreatment is ethnically motivated. Patient acknowledged uncertainty ("How do I know?") but described a pattern of being ignored while other residents receive attention.
- **Social isolation:** Patient states he is alone, doesn't talk to anyone, and avoids the psychologists at the facility.
- **Distinction:** Patient explicitly separated two factors — his psychiatric condition (which exists independently) and the mistreatment (which he says worsens his state). He does not attribute all his suffering to mistreatment alone.

## Nocturnal Anxiety Episodes

- Patient reports anxiety attacks in the middle of the night
- Symptoms: fear, chest palpitations, fear of isolation
- Coping behavior: gets up, leaves the room, sits on the balcony until the episode passes
- Also reports paranoid feelings during the day
- Describes heaviness ("feeling medications weighing 200 kg"), pressure in the head, eyes "popping out"

## Key Clinical Considerations

1. **Medication frustration:** The patient is caught between his desire for sedatives (Clonex) and the treating psychiatrist's reluctance to prescribe more benzodiazepines. Rami explained that benzodiazepines impair memory but acknowledged the patient's distress.
2. **Diagnostic uncertainty:** The schizophrenia diagnosis is questionable given patient's denial of psychotic symptoms and the possibility that involuntary hospitalization in 2004 may have been associated with PTSD/crisis rather than psychosis.
3. **Treatment resistance:** 15 years of treatment with minimal perceived benefit suggests treatment-resistant or inadequately treated bipolar depression.
4. **Psychosocial factors:** Social isolation and perceived discrimination at the facility may be significant contributors to current distress, independent of the psychiatric condition.
5. **Patient's request:** Wants a change in treatment — specifically requesting more anxiolytics and newer mood stabilizers instead of Depakote.
