# Sensory Processing and Autism in Clinical Practice Lecture 2025-11-12

## Context

Voice memo recording from 2025-11-12 capturing a Hebrew-language lecture on sensory processing in autism spectrum disorder. The presentation is delivered by a female clinician at a clinical training session (likely part of the HealthyMind Academy or affiliated training program at Telia'z Clinic). The lecturer uses clinical case examples drawn from her own practice and references recent neuroscience research. The lecture covers the full range of sensory modalities affected in autism, with particular emphasis on interoception — the internal sense of bodily states.

## Sensory Sensitivities Overview

The lecturer organizes sensory processing differences in autism across multiple modalities, noting that these are not merely preferences but reflect fundamental differences in how the nervous system processes stimuli.

### Auditory Sensitivity

- Certain sounds can appear as specific colors and tones to individuals on the spectrum — a form of synesthetic or hypersensitive auditory processing
- Strong, sudden sounds are particularly distressing
- Clinical example: A mother reported that before every birthday party, she would approach the child's classroom to ask parents to buy non-popping balloons, because the sound of a balloon bursting was overwhelming for her child
- This is not simply disliking a loud noise — it represents a qualitatively different sensory experience

### Tactile Sensitivity

- Clothing is a major source of distress: jeans, certain fabrics, and seams can be intolerable
- The lecturer describes a case of an "autistic swinger" — a child who loves to swing and spin, seeking vestibular input, but simultaneously cannot tolerate the feeling of specific clothing textures
- Hugs and physical contact are complex: some autistic individuals find hugs distressing while others crave deep pressure
- Clinical example: A 25-year-old man reported that as a child (until age 10-12), he would refuse physical contact — including his mother's hugs. Today, when his mother hugs him, he is moved to tears. He explained: "On one hand, it's not entirely pleasant even now — it's less so than before — but on the other hand, I know this is her way of showing she loves me, so I allow it"
- This example illustrates the social-emotional complexity layered onto sensory experience: the person must consciously override sensory discomfort to participate in social bonding

### Olfactory Processing

- Smell has a unique neural pathway — it is described as going "straight to the brain" (the olfactory bulb connects directly to the limbic system without thalamic relay)
- This makes olfactory experiences particularly intense and immediate
- No filtering mechanism exists in the same way as for other senses
- When entering a room, some individuals are overwhelmed by a "wall of scent" that arrives before any visual or auditory information
- The lecturer describes this as highly specific: one lip product in one scent, one body cream in another scent, and skin itself in yet another scent — all simultaneously perceived as distinct, overwhelming layers
- Textures also overlap here: certain food textures (not tastes) trigger refusal due to oral-tactile sensitivity

### Vestibular Processing

- Weight shifts, sense of gravity, and balance are processed differently
- The dopamine reward system may still be present — some autistic individuals intensely enjoy spinning, swinging, or other vestibular stimulation
- A classic question in clinical assessments: "Was there a game in school and they were choosing teams — football for example — and you were always chosen last?" This taps into both motor coordination differences and the social consequence of vestibular/proprioceptive challenges

## Interoception: The Inner Sense

The lecturer transitions from exteroception (how we experience the world through external stimuli) to interoception — the sense of internal bodily states.

### Definition

- Interoception encompasses the brain's input about internal states: hunger, thirst, temperature, bladder fullness, fatigue, and emotional arousal
- Receptors (osmoreceptors and others) collect this data, and a system must translate these signals into conscious awareness and appropriate behavioral responses

### Clinical Significance in Autism

- Many autistic individuals have impaired interoceptive awareness — they may not recognize when they are hungry, thirsty, too hot, or need to use the bathroom
- This is not a cognitive deficit but a sensory processing difference affecting internal body signals
- Clinical example: A clinician described treating a child who, despite being old enough, could not reliably sense bladder fullness. The child would wet himself — not from behavioral issues but from a genuine inability to perceive the internal signal until it was too late
- Children in gardens (kindergartens) may connect to interoception issues: caregivers need to remind them to drink, eat, or go to the bathroom on schedule rather than relying on the child's self-reported needs

### Homeostasis Connection

- Interoception is ultimately about homeostasis — the body's need to maintain internal balance
- When interoceptive signals are not properly received or processed, basic self-care routines break down
- The lecturer noted this is "important for homeostasis — if there's thirst, you eventually need to translate that into going to drink, and even before that, to recognize that you're thirsty"

## Neuroimaging Research

The lecturer references a specific study:

- **Source**: Article in *Trends in Neurosciences* by researchers from the NIH
- **Topic**: Brain scan imaging during hugs — comparing autistic and non-autistic individuals
- **Key finding**: The brain activation patterns during physical affection (hugs) differ significantly between autistic and non-autistic populations
- **Implication**: This provides neurobiological evidence that the subjective experience of physical touch is qualitatively different, not merely a behavioral preference
- A student at Ben-Gurion University of the Negev (named Shiran) was mentioned as having strong capabilities in examining these patterns, suggesting ongoing academic research in this area

## Key Themes

### Sensory Experience as Qualitatively Different

The central message is that sensory processing in autism is not about being "more sensitive" — it is a fundamentally different way of receiving and processing sensory input. The lecturer repeatedly emphasizes that typical people cannot simply imagine "more intense" versions of their own experiences to understand autistic sensory reality.

### Exteroception vs. Interoception

The lecture creates a clear framework: first covering how autistic individuals experience external stimuli (sounds, touch, smell, vestibular input), then pivoting to the often-overlooked internal dimension — how they experience their own body's signals.

### Social Consequences of Sensory Differences

Multiple examples demonstrate how sensory processing differences cascade into social difficulties: the child who cannot wear jeans like peers, the person who must consciously tolerate hugs, the child chosen last for sports teams due to motor coordination differences.

### Clinical Assessment Implications

The lecture provides practical clinical tools: specific questions to ask ("Was there a game where you were chosen last?"), awareness of food texture versus taste refusal, understanding that bathroom accidents may reflect interoceptive processing rather than behavioral issues.

## Clinical Relevance

This lecture connects to the Telia'z Clinic training context through its practical clinical focus. Clinicians working with autistic clients need to understand sensory processing at this level of detail to avoid misattributing sensory-driven behaviors to willfulness, anxiety, or other psychological constructs. The interoception component is particularly relevant for treatment planning — clinicians must account for the possibility that clients may not reliably report internal states.
