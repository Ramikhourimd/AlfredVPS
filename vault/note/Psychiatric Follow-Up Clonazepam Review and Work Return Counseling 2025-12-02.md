# Psychiatric Follow-Up: Clonazepam Review and Work Return Counseling — 2025-12-02

## Context

Outpatient psychiatric follow-up conducted by Dr. Rami Khouri with a female patient currently on Clonazepam (Clonex). A family member was also present during the session. The patient has a long-standing therapeutic relationship with Dr. Rami ("the doctor has been with you for a long time"). The session was recorded as a voice memo and transcribed from Levantine Arabic.

## Patient Status

- **Subjective improvement:** Patient reports feeling "much better than before" — both she and her husband have noticed the improvement
- **Medication:** Clonex (Clonazepam) — patient currently takes 1 tablet in the morning and 1 at night. Doctor noted a noon dose was also prescribed but patient denies taking it. Doctor indicated this is an area to eventually taper, not increase
- **Recent incident:** Patient sustained a burn while making bread — hot oil or fire fell on her. She described pain and itching at the affected area
- **Side effects reported:** Patient complains of pain ("it stings") and persistent itching, which she attributes to medication. Doctor assessed this as non-concerning

## Work Return — Key Clinical Issue

The patient has been absent from work for approximately 2 months. Her workplace manager communicated that:
- She must attend on **Mondays and Wednesdays** to maintain her position
- Other people are waiting for the spot
- **Ministry of Health regulations** do not permit indefinite absence

Dr. Rami's clinical framing of work return:
- Work is therapeutic, not just economic: *"We need work not for money but for your brain's health"*
- If she doesn't return to work, the benefit of medication reduction would be wasted
- Staying at home without structured activity is detrimental to mental health
- He asked: "Can anyone stay in the house forever?" — emphasizing the need for routine and cognitive engagement

Patient showed ambivalence — she says she enjoys work but finds something about it difficult. She expressed interest in **secretarial/computer training** as an alternative or addition.

## Medication Decision

**Decision: No medication changes at this time.** Doctor explicitly stated that any small adjustment could destabilize the patient's current improved state. The current regimen is working and must be maintained consistently. The family member reinforced this — attributing the patient's good mood directly to consistent medication adherence: "what made you feel good is the medication you're taking on time."

## Family and Social Context

- A family member (likely a brother or male relative) was present and participated actively
- The family member discussed work as being better than medication — "better than medicine, didn't you determine we should work?"
- Religious perspective raised: from a Christian viewpoint, the question was posed whether medication is acceptable, with the family member affirming it is
- Family dynamics: patient praises her father ("the best person in the world"), references siblings
- Post-session plans: family planned to eat at "Sheikh restaurant in Tamra"

## Clinical Assessment

- Patient is **stable and improving** on current Clonazepam regimen
- Medication adherence appears partial (missing noon dose) — to monitor
- Work return is both a clinical and regulatory necessity
- Burn incident is acute but not clinically concerning from a psychiatric perspective
- Long-term plan: eventual gradual medication taper, contingent on sustained stability and occupational engagement
