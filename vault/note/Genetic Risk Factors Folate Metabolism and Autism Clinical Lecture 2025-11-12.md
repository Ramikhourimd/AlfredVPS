# Genetic Risk Factors, Folate Metabolism, and Autism Clinical Lecture 2025-11-12

## Context

Voice memo recording from 2025-11-12 capturing a Hebrew-language clinical lecture delivered as part of the same HealthyMind Academy training day that included lectures on sensory processing, psychedelics in psychiatry, PTSD, QEEG, and cognitive risk factors. The recording features two speakers (a male and a female clinician) presenting on genetic and environmental risk factors for developmental and psychiatric conditions, with deep focus on folate metabolism, MTHFR gene variants, autistic burnout, and clinical case studies.

This lecture is pitched at a professional clinical audience and covers material directly relevant to the Telia'z Clinic Israel diagnostic and treatment practice, particularly for psychiatrists and case managers working with autism spectrum disorder and neurodevelopmental conditions.

## Environmental Risk Factors for Psychiatric Conditions

The lecture opens with a discussion of environmental factors linked to psychiatric and developmental disorders, framed through an epigenetic lens:

- **Air pollution** — Identified as the most well-known environmental contaminant linked to psychiatric conditions. Exposure during pregnancy creates downstream effects on neurodevelopment
- **Infection during pregnancy** — A basic genetic-environmental interaction: infection triggers inflammatory processes that interact with genetic susceptibility. Noted as relevant across psychiatry, particularly in schizophrenia research
- **Heavy metals** — Even low-level exposure, combined with impaired metabolism, affects the fetus/infant. Mercury specifically mentioned
- **DDT pesticides** — Better known from Parkinson's research but also linked to neurodevelopmental conditions
- **Plastics (phthalates/BPA)** — Described as crossing the placenta and penetrating the protective amniotic environment. A correlation with autism severity was noted, with the lecturers expressing surprise at seeing plastics appear in these datasets

## Quantifying Genetic vs. Environmental Risk

The lecturers provided specific prevalence figures:

- **20-25%** of cases have a reproducible genetic component
- **Maximum 8%** have a solid, identifiable genetic cause that can be directly investigated
- **10%** of cases involve recognized genetic syndromes with autism features (e.g., Angelman syndrome), which also carry behavioral phenotypes
- Many gene-specific mutations remain classified as VUS (Variants of Uncertain Significance)

### Genetic Testing Guidance

A practical clinical recommendation: if a family had genetic chip testing done five years ago, they should now pursue **trio exome sequencing** (father, mother, and child) — especially relevant when an older sibling is considering having children. New variants are discovered annually, and the interpretation of existing data improves continuously.

### Iatrogenic Risk Factors

- **Valproic acid (Depakin)** — Explicitly flagged as a major risk: prescribing to women of childbearing age without warning and documentation was described as negligent. The lecturer stated having witnessed such cases
- **COVID-19 infection during pregnancy** — Symptomatic COVID during pregnancy was linked to higher risk compared to asymptomatic infection

### Chronic Disease Associations

- Diabetes, hypertension, and autoimmune conditions (specifically SLE, celiac disease) noted as having elevated associations with autism spectrum presentations

## Folic Acid and MTHFR Gene Variants

This was the most detailed clinical section, covering the biochemistry and clinical management of folate metabolism disorders.

### The Folate Family

The lecturer presented a comprehensive taxonomy of folate forms. The active form is **L-methylfolate** (5-MTHF). The **MTHFR gene** encodes the enzyme responsible for converting dietary folate into its biologically active form that cells can utilize.

### MTHFR C677T SNP

- **Heterozygous carriers** — 40% reduction in enzyme efficiency
- **Homozygous carriers** — 80% reduction in enzyme efficiency, meaning the body effectively "cannot see" folate
- The enzyme that should perform the conversion fails, and cells cannot absorb the nutrient

### Clinical Implications

- **Antidepressant non-response** — When a patient on antidepressants is not responding, MTHFR status should be checked. Active folate supplementation may be needed alongside or instead of standard folic acid
- **COMT gene interaction** — Certain COMT variants, when combined with heterozygous C677T, produce a symptomatic phenotype. Neither variant alone would be clinically significant, but in combination they become relevant
- **Pharmacogenetic testing** — If using pharmacogenetic kits (PGx panels), the C677T result is typically included. Clinicians should check this result routinely
- **Homocysteine as a biomarker** — Elevated homocysteine levels (homocysteinemia) can serve as a proxy marker for impaired folate metabolism, functioning as a simpler screening test

### Clinical Rule

"Next time you see MTHFR, and if the patient is homozygous for C677T — treat. Whether with folic acid or active folate, this needs to be addressed."

### Folate Receptor Antibodies

Brief mention of research on antibodies against folate receptors in brain cells — a separate mechanism from MTHFR variants, but contributing to the same clinical picture of cerebral folate deficiency.

## Folic Acid as Preventive Measure

- Maternal folic acid supplementation one month before conception is the most established intervention for reducing ASD risk
- Various forms of folic acid exist, with active forms being critical for individuals with impaired metabolism

## Recurrence Risk

A common clinical question: "My child has autism — what is the risk for the next child?" This is addressed primarily in child and adolescent psychiatry and pediatric neurology, but the question of prevention remains partially unanswered.

## Autistic Burnout

### Definition and Clinical Presentation

Autistic burnout was defined as significant physical, mental, and emotional exhaustion caused by:
- Functional overload
- Masking (the effort of appearing neurotypical)
- Ongoing struggles to fit into neurotypical environments

The cost of masking was emphasized — described as autistic individuals' attempts to integrate at enormous personal expense.

### Symptoms and Impact

- Affects all life domains and all types of functioning
- Increased anger and emotional dysregulation
- Heightened anxiety
- Overload and hypersensitivity to change
- Can lead to suicidality

The lecturer referenced a lecture on autism and suicidality at the National Center for Autism Research in Jerusalem as further reading.

### Adult Autistic Voice

A quote from an autistic adult was cited (from a high-impact-factor journal): *"All of your internal resources exhausted beyond measure, and being left with no clean-up crew."* The lecturer added: "There is no umbrella and no one to bring quiet back into your life."

### Questionnaire Context

A research questionnaire on burnout was briefly presented — described as less validated than others, but useful for clinicians to understand the emotional texture of autistic experience. The emphasis was on reading the questions themselves and observing the patient's response, rather than scoring.

## Clinical Case Studies

### Case 1: Eating Disorder Masking as Depression

A 20-year-old male was referred with suspected depression and prescribed Prozac as a first-line treatment. When blood work was finally ordered:
- **Vitamin D** — deficient
- **B12** — deficient
- **Folic acid** — deficient
- All nutritional markers were pathologically low

Investigation revealed a **restrictive eating disorder**: the patient had eaten only plain pasta (Totino's) for at least two years. The lecture's message: "Don't prescribe Prozac instead of B12, and don't prescribe it instead of protein."

### Case 2: Eating Disorder in a Young Female

A female patient was found to be vomiting (bulimia). When the family asked for an eating disorder treatment referral, the clinician identified the root cause as a **gastroscopy finding from 2022** — invasive mucosal findings in the esophagus — that had worsened by 2025, causing physical difficulty swallowing. The "eating disorder" was actually a medical condition requiring gastroenterological intervention, not CBT.

### Case 3: Challenging Behavior in Sheltered Housing

A patient in sheltered housing presenting with escalating challenging behaviors:
- Self-hitting, head-banging, breaking glasses
- Aggressive outbursts

On deep review of the medical file, a **cholesteatoma** was discovered — a non-cancerous growth (keratin accumulation) in the ear that had gone unmonitored for 20 years. Key facts:
- Cholesteatoma produces pain equivalent to cancer pain
- It can destroy the hardest bone in the body (temporal bone)
- CT scan revealed the cholesteatoma measured 14mm x 17mm — described as "enormous"
- An ENT doctor examined the patient but was unable to perform physical examination (endoscopy) due to the patient's behavior

The case illustrates the critical importance of thorough medical file review before attributing challenging behavior to psychiatric causes.

## Autism Spectrum Disorder: Diagnosis and Treatment

### Differential Diagnosis Overlap

The lecture covered conditions that can mimic autism:
- **Schizoid personality disorder** — Can present similarly due to social withdrawal
- **Social anxiety disorder** — Social difficulty can resemble autism spectrum features
- Any condition with significant social impairment may superficially resemble ASD

### Comorbidity Rates

- **80%** of autistic individuals have at least one comorbid condition
- **60%** have more than one comorbidity
- Each comorbidity must be addressed independently in the treatment plan

### Medication Warnings for Autism

Several medications were specifically flagged as inappropriate or ineffective for core autism features:
- **Ziprasidone (Zeldox)** — Not a treatment for autism spectrum conditions
- **Quetiapine (Seroquel)** — Not a treatment for autism spectrum conditions
- **Aripiprazole (Abilify variant)** — Not a treatment for autism spectrum conditions
- The lecturer expressed frustration at seeing these medications prescribed repeatedly by psychiatrists and psychologists
- **Melatonin** — While safer than the above, emerging data suggests cardiac insufficiency risk with use exceeding 200 days. Still considered preferable to "pressing them onto iPads" for sleep

### Sensory Regulation in Treatment

- Sensory processing differences are features, not bugs — they will not improve and should not be treated as pathology
- Treatment should focus on accommodation and environmental modification, not elimination of sensory responses
- Sensory regulation support (referencing earlier lecture on the same day) should be a core component of treatment planning

### Autism-Aware Medicine

The lecture called for development of **standards of care for autism-aware medicine**:
- NICE guidelines on autism were referenced as existing but insufficient
- The **Lurie Center at Massachusetts General Hospital** was cited as having published algorithms for investigating and treating comorbidities in autism
- These algorithms address each comorbidity separately, recognizing that autism complicates standard treatment approaches
- The National Center for Autism Research in Israel was mentioned as a venue for future guideline development

### Adult Autism Diagnosis

A structured adult autism diagnostic manual was referenced, described as a step-by-step guide that clinicians can follow for systematic assessment:
- Two characteristics must be assessed separately
- Risk factors must be identified
- The diagnostic summary must go beyond simply writing "has autism" — it must follow established rules and create a treatment pathway
- The process should involve consultation with someone experienced in the assessment

## Clinical Relevance to Telia'z Practice

This lecture has direct implications for the Telia'z Clinic Israel clinical practice:
1. **MTHFR screening** should be considered for treatment-resistant cases, particularly patients not responding to antidepressants
2. **Nutritional assessment** should precede psychopharmacological intervention, especially in young patients with restricted eating patterns
3. **Medical file review** must be thorough before attributing challenging behaviors to psychiatric causes
4. **Autism comorbidity assessment** requires independent evaluation and treatment of each co-occurring condition
5. **Medication prescribing** for autism needs to follow evidence-based guidelines rather than reflexive antipsychotic use
