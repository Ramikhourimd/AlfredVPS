# Psychiatric Session Outpatient Medication Review and Work Therapy 2025-12-02

## Context

Follow-up psychiatric session conducted by Dr. Rami Khouri with a long-term female outpatient. The session was recorded as a voice memo and transcribed from Levantine Arabic. A family member (Speaker [2]) was present during part of the session, contributing to discussions about medication, faith, and family life.

The patient has been under Dr. Rami's care for an extended period ("Rami has been your doctor for a long time" — stated by patient). The session took place in the context of Telia'z Clinic Israel operations.

## Patient Status

The patient reports subjective improvement: "I am better than before." Both the patient and her husband/family confirm she is doing better. The doctor corroborates observable improvement.

## Medication Review

**Current regimen:**
- **Clonex (Clonazepam):** Patient takes 1 tablet in the morning and 1 tablet at night
- **Midday dose:** Doctor confirmed a midday dose was prescribed, but the patient denies taking it — "No, I don't drink it"
- **Other medications:** Referenced but not specified by name (the doctor mentions wanting to eventually reduce other medications)

**Decision:** No medication changes at this time. The doctor explicitly stated: "We are not going to change the medication now." The rationale is that even small changes could destabilize the patient's current improvement — "any small change could cause her to [regress]."

The patient actively resisted any medication changes, expressing fear of side effects and pain ("it stings me... I'm in pain... here, here").

## Incident Report

The patient described a recent burn incident: while making bread, she was burned by fire. The severity appears moderate — she described having to push the fire away. This was noted but not flagged as requiring immediate medical intervention beyond the psychiatric session.

## Work Attendance — Key Clinical Issue

The facility manager reported that the patient has not attended work for two months, with only one visit in that period. The manager communicated that:
- The Ministry of Health does not accept this level of non-attendance
- Other people are waiting for the patient's placement
- The patient must attend on **Mondays and Wednesdays** to maintain her position

Dr. Rami framed work attendance as a therapeutic intervention, not merely an administrative requirement:
- "We need work not for money but for your brain to work"
- "If you don't go to work, the medication reduction we achieved would be wasted"
- "A person who does something [stays mentally healthy]"

The patient expressed some willingness but also resistance. She mentioned interest in secretarial/computer work as an alternative or aspiration.

## Religious and Cultural Context

A family member (Speaker [2]) raised religious perspectives on medication, asking why medication is needed from a Christian faith perspective. The doctor and family member concluded that taking medication is consistent with faith — "of course he [Jesus] would take medicine."

The family member also attributed the patient's well-being to consistent medication compliance: "What made you feel good is taking the medication on time. If you didn't take it on time, you wouldn't feel good at all."

## Family Dynamics

- A family member (Speaker [2], likely a sibling or relative) was present and actively engaged
- The patient expressed warmth toward her father: "My father is the best person in the whole world"
- A younger family member was briefly present, causing interruptions (mentions of a toy car "Turbo")
- Plans were discussed to go to a restaurant in Tamra ("Sheikh restaurant in Tamra") after the session

## Clinical Impressions

1. **Stable on current regimen** — Patient is improved and medication should not be changed
2. **Medication compliance concern** — Patient may not be taking the prescribed midday Clonazepam dose
3. **Work attendance critical** — Both therapeutically and administratively; risk of losing placement
4. **Supportive family environment** — Family is engaged and encouraging medication compliance
5. **Long-term relationship** — Patient has been with Dr. Rami for years, indicating chronic psychiatric condition requiring ongoing management
