# Therapy Session: Rami's Leadership Frustration and Role Conflict (2025-12-05)

## Context

Rami discusses his growing frustration with Telia'z management during a therapy session. The conversation reveals deep tensions between his professional vision for the clinic and management's approach to growth, role definition, and organizational leadership.

## Key Themes

### 1. Marketing Strategy Disagreement

Rami wants the marketing department to brand the clinic as a **premium, prestigious** service to attract salaried clinicians at lower cost, rather than the current strategy of simply pushing revenue growth. He feels the marketing team's mandate ("increase revenue") misaligns with his vision for sustainable clinic positioning. He expresses frustration at having to constantly push for changes with no apparent impact.

### 2. Organizational Health Score Model

Rami has built a quantitative model that calculates an **organizational health score** as a growth readiness indicator. The model incorporates:
- Patient satisfaction scores
- Staff workload metrics
- Multiple burnout-risk indicators

He has set a threshold that must be crossed before growth should begin, estimating it could be reached within six months. However, management ignores this framework and pushes for immediate expansion — citing opportunities like a Clalit South psychiatrist leaving with 350 unserved patients.

### 3. Consequences of Premature Growth

Rami warns that aggressive, uncontrolled growth will:
- Increase wait times
- Reduce the clinic to "just another clinic" without differentiation
- Erode the distinctive quality he has built

He expresses a feeling that the only way management will understand is if he leaves and the business collapses.

### 4. Feeling Unheard by Leadership

Rami feels unable to effectively communicate his concerns. He senses that management views him as a dreamer ("he dreams, he floats") because he always has ideas. He believes something dramatic would need to happen for them to take him seriously. The leadership team (including those who are "a bit afraid of him") still doesn't internalize his professional arguments.

### 5. The CEO Role Dilemma — The "Steering Wheel" Analogy

Rami uses a powerful driving analogy:
- He doesn't necessarily want the steering wheel himself
- But it's wrong for the current people to have it
- He wants the "car to get where it needs to go" — his motive is professional, not personal ambition
- The therapist observes this creates a complex "flutter" — "I don't want the wheel, but it shouldn't be in their hands either"

Rami is explicit: **his motive is not personal ambition.** He compares it to a lawyer telling a hospital "you need a lawyer, not a doctor, as a judge" — it's about professional competency, not personal desire. He's deeply frustrated when management interprets his advocacy as personal ambition, because it causes them to dismiss his professional arguments.

### 6. Reluctance to Take CEO Role

Concrete reasons Rami rejects the CEO position:
- Would require **three days per week on-site** — unsustainable
- Would **distance him from technology** and innovation work
- Would **distance him from home** and family
- Predicts burnout **within a month** ("playing while injured")
- Recognizes he needs **quiet, personal space** for creative thinking
- His "pampering" days spent thinking about new ideas are what sustains him mentally

### 7. Basel's Positive Impact

Since Basel Kanaaneh joined operations, things have improved at the clinic. Rami notes Basel's strength: "You present him a plan and he starts thinking about how to execute it" — contrasted with Roee's inability to do this.

### 8. Critique of Roee (Operations Manager)

Rami is sharply critical of Roee, the current operations manager:
- Got the role because he's "loyal, does what you tell him" — the opposite of what an ops manager needs
- An ops manager "needs to know what to do without being told anything"
- Lacks process thinking entirely
- Rami doesn't know what Roee does despite four years at the company
- Leadership sees it but there's no other operations to manage besides the clinic

### 9. Critique of Alex Taliaz (CEO)

Rami's assessment of Alex:
- "Doesn't do the work. He doesn't work."
- "Behaves like a consultant" — knows motivational phrases but doesn't execute
- The current budget/decision cycle makes Alex's shortcomings more visible
- Rami is functionally the CEO while Alex holds the title
- Oren has indicated they're "aware and not sure he'll continue"
- Rami doesn't want Alex replaced by himself — wants a **physician CEO**

### 10. Desired Outcome

Rami's preferred solution:
- A **physician CEO** (or CEO-equivalent with medical background) who would:
  - Advocate for the same professional positions Rami holds
  - Take seriously the need for medical/clinical leadership in strategic decisions
  - Free Rami from having to fight these battles himself
- Strong **operations people** around him (Basel model)
- Rami would serve as **strategic advisor** on how to build the strategy, but not as CEO
- A possible part-time CEO arrangement (one day per week) could work if supported by operational staff

### 11. The Personal Burden

Rami describes the accumulated weight:
- "Everything ultimately concentrates on me" — the good parts no longer feel good, just burden
- "I'm dying to take a step back and get out of this, it's a headache. But I can't afford to"
- He knows the operation would collapse without him, and he's invested too much
- Alex has never once sat with Rami to understand what's going on inside him

## Therapist Observations

- Notes the "flutter" in Rami's position regarding control
- Identifies that management attributing personal motives undermines Rami's professional credibility
- Connects Rami's reluctance to a real cost calculation, not lack of ambition
- Recalls someone (likely Oren) who thought Rami was offended — suggests they may have correctly identified his desire for more involvement, even if Rami frames it differently
- Ends with the driving/car imagery and the tension between wanting involvement and knowing its personal cost
