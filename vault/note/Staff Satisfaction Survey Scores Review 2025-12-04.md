# Staff Satisfaction Survey Scores Review 2025-12-04

## Context

During the 2025-12-04 clinic operations session, Rami and Basel reviewed patient/third-party satisfaction survey results for the psychiatrist team. The survey uses a 10-point scale, and the scores were discussed as a potential KPI metric.

## Survey Results

### Individual Scores
- **Elinor:** Average satisfaction score **10/10** (from 2 respondents out of ~200 patients) — perfect score but very small sample size
- **Ahmed (Masalha):** Referenced but specific score not clearly stated — appears to be in the 8-9 range
- **Said:** Below 9 threshold
- **Elias:** Below 9 threshold — this compounds with his previously identified performance and availability issues
- **Other staff:** Scores in the 7-8 range

### Sample Size Concerns
- Elinor's perfect score comes from only 2 survey respondents — too small to be statistically meaningful
- The question arose: "How many out of 200 patients responded?" — response rate appears very low
- Some psychiatrists have been sending surveys for only ~6 months ("half a year")
- One individual had only 7 responses out of 16 surveys sent

### Scoring Context
- The "third party satisfaction" metric relates to how satisfied patients are with their clinician
- This is distinct from clinical outcome measures
- Rami was examining this as part of a broader KPI framework discovery (see Clinical KPI Discovery note)

## Implications

- Low response rates undermine the reliability of satisfaction scores
- Elias's low satisfaction score adds to the pattern of underperformance (scheduled 8 hours but only works 6, cancels appointments)
- The satisfaction metric needs a minimum response threshold before scores are considered valid
- Elinor's high score is encouraging but needs more data points
