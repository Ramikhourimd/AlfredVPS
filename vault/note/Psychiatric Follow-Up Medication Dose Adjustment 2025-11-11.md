# Psychiatric Follow-Up Medication Dose Adjustment 2025-11-11

## Context

This is a follow-up psychiatric consultation conducted in Arabic between Rami Khouri and an unnamed female patient at the Telia'z clinic. The session focuses on medication adjustment following a transition away from venlafaxine (Effexor/Vybex).

## Clinical Summary

### Presenting Symptoms
- **Rapid heartbeat (tachycardia)** — patient reports heart racing, accompanied by strange sensations
- **Anticipatory anxiety episodes** — patient describes feeling something is about to happen, then it occurs simultaneously
- **Significantly increased appetite** — new symptom; patient reports constant hunger even immediately after eating, lasting 24 hours; previously had reduced appetite

### Medication History
- **Previous medication:** Venlafaxine (referred to as "Vybex" in Arabic) — was partially effective ("working but not fully")
- **Transition:** Patient was tapered off venlafaxine and switched to a new medication
- **Current dose:** 50mg (1 tablet)
- **Target dose:** 100mg (2 tablets) — not yet reached

### Clinical Assessment (Rami)
- The recurring anxiety-like episodes are likely a continuation of prior symptoms that were previously managed by venlafaxine
- Symptoms may be due to insufficient dose of the new medication after tapering off venlafaxine — the patient "came down from Vybex but didn't reach enough of the new one"
- Increased appetite is likely a medication side effect; Rami notes these reactions are sometimes observed and should be monitored

### Treatment Plan
1. **Increase dose to 100mg** (2 tablets) to reach therapeutic target
2. **Follow-up in 1.5 months** to reassess effectiveness at full dose
3. If medication provides partial benefit at 100mg, consider further dose increase
4. Patient advised to monitor and manage appetite changes
5. If medication proves ineffective even at adequate dose, reconsider treatment approach

## Key Clinical Observations
- Patient is Arabic-speaking, seen at clinic (Rami mentions "I work here, three mornings")
- This represents a common clinical pattern of symptom recurrence during medication transitions when the replacement dose is sub-therapeutic
- The appetite increase is a new symptom since the medication change, suggesting it is pharmacological rather than psychological
