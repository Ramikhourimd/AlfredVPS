# Clinical Supervision: Suicide Risk Assessment and Reporting Protocols

## Context

On 2025-12-01, Rami Khouri (psychiatrist and clinical supervisor at Telia'z) received an urgent phone call from a clinician named Selina seeking guidance on a patient case involving suicidal risk. The patient is a male affiliated with a military/security framework, who had previously expressed suicidal thoughts (machshavot) and is known to carry a weapon. The clinician was anxious because she could not see the patient in person for follow-up and was uncertain about the appropriate clinical and reporting steps.

The call covered audio troubleshooting initially, followed by identification verification for the patient, and then a detailed clinical supervision session.

## Key Clinical Guidance

### 1. Assessing Immediate Danger (Sakana Miyadedet)

Rami explained that clinicians assess danger **at a single point in time only**. When stating that there is or is not immediate danger, this refers exclusively to the moment of examination. There are no clinical tools to predict future danger with certainty — only risk factor-based prediction (gormei sakana).

The assessment follows a clinical hierarchy:
- **Suicidal ideation** (thoughts about death)
- **Suicidal thoughts** (specific thoughts about self-harm)
- **Suicidal intention** (desire to act)
- **Suicidal plan** (concrete plan formulated)
- **Suicidal act** (action taken)

The clinician must assess each level and document findings. If a level was not assessed, the clinician must explain why and provide their clinical impression.

### 2. Documentation Requirements

Rami emphasized that documentation serves as the clinician's protection. The key standard is demonstrating that the clinician did **the maximum** to assess whether immediate danger exists. Specifically:

- Document what was assessed at each level of the suicidality hierarchy
- If a symptom was not directly assessed, explain why (e.g., "the symptom was not reported by the patient, but based on behavior observed, I am of the impression that...")
- The record does not need to be strictly reported — clinical impression based on observed behavior is acceptable
- The problematic scenario is when a symptom was neither assessed nor mentioned at all in documentation

### 3. Legal Precedent — McKream Case

Rami referenced a US legal case (McKream/McCream) where a clinician was held liable. The case established that when there is any indication of immediate danger to self or others, the clinician must take maximum steps to assess and intervene, even when the clinical tools (hok/law) are not entirely clear about what constitutes immediate danger.

### 4. Differential Diagnosis of Suicidality

Rami distinguished two types of suicidality that have different clinical implications:

**Depression-driven suicidality (ovdanoot):**
- Gradual progression
- Observable behavioral markers (closing accounts, farewell conversations, giving away possessions)
- More predictable — allows the clinician to say "I believe within 2-3 days something may happen"
- Planful behavior suggests higher immediate risk but also offers intervention windows

**Personality-driven self-harm (pgia atzmit):**
- Impulsive — can happen at any moment
- Much harder to predict
- Harder to establish clinical timeline
- More clinically problematic because the clinician cannot easily justify a prediction

Rami also noted that when there is a recurring pattern of risk behavior, the clinician should try to understand whether it originates from an organized condition (me'urgan) or a personality disorder (ishiyuti), as this affects what the patient might actually do.

### 5. Rami's Clinical Assessment

Based on Selina's report, Rami assessed that there was **no immediate danger at the time of the examination**. However, he noted:
- The patient holds a weapon (neshek)
- The patient had previous suicidal thoughts
- The patient needs close follow-up (ma'akav tzamud) by a psychiatrist as soon as possible
- The clinician's current anxiety was partly driven by the inability to see the patient, rather than pure clinical indicators of escalation

Rami helped Selina distinguish between:
- Clinical concern based on objective findings at the time of examination
- Anxiety from being unable to follow up (which is valid but different)

### 6. Military vs. Civilian Reporting Protocols

**Active military (hayal sadir / keva):**
- The patient falls under the military health system (ma'arechet tzva'it)
- The clinician **can** report to the kaban (military mental health officer), the military psychiatrist, or the commander
- These are considered part of the same system — reporting is permitted and expected
- The most effective intervention is pushing for a military psychiatrist evaluation, not just filing a license revocation report (which can take a month)

**Civilian / discharged:**
- If the patient is outside the military system, the clinician **cannot** disclose to the employer/organization without patient consent
- The clinician can only speak directly with the patient and their existing therapist (metapelet)

**Key distinction:** A reservist (miluim) vs. regular soldier (sadir) matters. Regular soldiers and those on keva are within the military system. Civilians and discharged soldiers are not.

## Immediate Action Plan Given to Selina

1. Contact the patient's therapist (metapelet) immediately
2. Explain the clinical concern and that the patient needs urgent psychiatric evaluation
3. Try to speak with the kaban (military mental health officer) or the commander if the patient is within the military system
4. Communicate clearly: "I assessed him, there was no immediate danger at the time, but there is potential risk — he holds a weapon, had prior suicidal thoughts, and requires close follow-up by a psychiatrist as soon as possible"
5. If at any point there are signs of deterioration (dirdur), take the patient to the emergency room (miyon)
6. Document everything: assessment, findings, actions taken, and rationale

## Clinician's Emotional State

Rami recognized that Selina's anxiety was partly clinical and partly personal — she felt she should have pushed harder during the original assessment and was now unable to see the patient before the weekend. Rami validated this while helping her understand the difference between:
- A clinical error (not acting when action was warranted at the time)
- Retrospective anxiety (feeling worried now because of inability to follow up)

He assessed that based on the clinical picture at the time, she had not made an error — the level of immediate danger did not warrant emergency intervention at that moment. The current appropriate action was follow-up, not self-blame.
