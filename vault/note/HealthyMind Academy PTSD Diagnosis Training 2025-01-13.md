# HealthyMind Academy PTSD Diagnosis Training 2025-01-13

## Context

This was the second session in a biweekly HealthyMind Academy clinical training series (הדרכה) for case managers at the Telia'z clinic. The session continued from a previous session where Dr. Rami Khouri introduced PTSD criteria, prognosis, and Bituach Leumi (National Insurance) considerations. Elias Jahjah facilitated the discussion, which became a rich, open exploration of diagnostic dilemmas facing case managers in post-October 7 clinical practice.

## Key Discussion Themes

### 1. PTSD Over-Diagnosis Concerns (הבחנת יתר)

Sarah Pardes raised the central concern: she is seeing an increasing number of cases where patients present with trauma-related symptoms that do not fully meet PTSD diagnostic criteria — they may have anxiety, depression, or general distress responses to trauma, but not the hallmark PTSD symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive re-experiencing). She worries that labeling these patients with PTSD:
- Diminishes the seriousness of the diagnosis for those who genuinely meet criteria
- Creates a system where two very different clinical presentations carry the same label
- Contributes to the clinic being "an engine of the over-diagnosis phenomenon"

Rivi Idelman supported this observation, noting that many patients might better fit an anxiety disorder diagnosis rather than PTSD, but the structured assessment process tends to channel responses toward PTSD criteria. She also observed a significant shift over time: patients presenting in the early months after October 7 showed clear, dramatic PTSD presentations, while more recent arrivals often present with milder symptoms and secondary-gain motivations.

### 2. PTSD as a Spectrum vs. Binary Diagnosis

Maram Faris offered a counterpoint, arguing that PTSD exists on a spectrum. She cautioned against swinging to the opposite extreme of under-diagnosis, noting that:
- Even people not directly exposed to a single traumatic event may have been physically proximate to danger, seen graphic footage, and heard gunfire
- The collective scale of October 7 means the threshold for Criterion A is broadly met
- Denying the PTSD label risks denying legitimate suffering and access to treatment

### 3. Bituach Leumi (National Insurance) Eligibility Pressures

Multiple participants described how National Insurance policies drive patient behavior:
- **Initial automatic period:** Bituach Leumi initially granted automatic recognition as terror victims for a set period (approximately 6-12 months) without stringent documentation requirements
- **Current phase:** That automatic period has expired, and patients now need a psychiatric letter to continue benefits — driving a wave of patients seeking PTSD diagnosis specifically for eligibility, not necessarily for treatment
- **Patient expectations:** Rivi described a patient who openly stated he wanted the diagnosis to receive financial help toward purchasing a car. Sarah described a patient who was angry when a psychiatrist did not write a PTSD diagnosis, knowing it would affect his Bituach Leumi claim
- **Structural inequity:** Sarah and Shira noted that Bituach Leumi ties benefits specifically to the PTSD diagnosis, excluding people with other trauma-related conditions (anxiety, depression, substance use), which creates an unfair system

### 4. Clinical Documentation Integrity

The team discussed how to navigate the tension between patient advocacy and professional integrity:
- **Stav Zamir:** She reports what patients state in questionnaires even when her clinical impression differs, trusting the psychiatrist to validate or correct
- **Maram Faris:** She uses the comments field (קומנטס) to add her clinical impressions, observations about distress level, and any inconsistencies — ensuring the psychiatrist sees both the patient's self-report and the case manager's assessment
- **Sarah Pardes:** She has started correcting AI-generated summaries when they incorrectly attribute PTSD symptoms to patients who described depression or other conditions, not PTSD-specific symptoms
- **Elias Jahjah:** He emphasized that the comments field is prominently displayed on the psychiatrist's screen and is highly valued — it represents the case manager's direct clinical observation, distinct from both the patient's self-report and the AI summary

### 5. Collective Trauma Framework

Dr. Rami Khouri (joining remotely by car) introduced two important conceptual points:
1. **Collective trauma as a distinct phenomenon:** The October 7 aftermath represents a complex collective trauma that layers on top of previous collective traumas. Its symptomatology, etiology, relationship to treatment, and prognosis differ from individual PTSD. He recommended inviting Dr. Yael Kaspi, a recognized expert in the field, to deliver a seminar on collective trauma.
2. **Tension between commemoration and treatment:** There is a fundamental dissonance between the therapeutic goal of processing and integrating traumatic memories and the collective/national impulse to preserve and memorialize trauma (ceremonies, commemorations). This pattern is seen globally (Cambodia, post-Holocaust). The national commemoration culture can actively interfere with clinical recovery and may generate PTSD-like symptoms in people who do not strictly meet clinical criteria.

Shira Lachmann added that she heard psychiatrists at her training institution discussing the possibility of a new diagnostic category specifically for post-October 7 trauma, similar to "post-Holocaust syndrome" — acknowledging that DSM categories may not adequately capture complex, prolonged collective trauma.

### 6. Trauma Narrative in Intake Sessions

Stav Zamir asked how much space to give patients' trauma stories during intake:
- **Dilemma:** Some patients arrive wanting to recount their full trauma story in graphic detail; others reach it organically; some need prompting
- **Risk:** Allowing full trauma narrative in a time-limited intake opens an emotional wound that cannot be properly closed before session end
- **Elias's guidance:**
  - Get enough information to assess Criterion A (exposure) without requiring graphic details
  - Actively discourage highly visual/graphic descriptions ("where was the blood" level detail)
  - Use framing like: "We are not in trauma-focused therapy now; we are getting to know your situation"
  - If a trauma narrative opens, attempt some degree of closure before ending the session
  - Be aware that some patients' insistence on graphic retelling may itself be a clinical phenomenon (victimhood, identification with the aggressor, re-traumatization of the clinician)
  - Recognize that the clinician's secondary trauma exposure across multiple back-to-back sessions is a real occupational hazard

## Operational Observations

- The biweekly 45-minute session format feels compressed; discussion often starts late (around minute 5) and ends abruptly at minute 40-45
- Elias suggested exploring a longer or more frequent cadence
- The team requested bringing specific patient cases for discussion in future sessions
