# Clinical Supervision: Suicide Risk Assessment and Military Reporting Protocols

## Context

On 2025-12-01, Rami Khouri provided clinical supervision via phone to a case manager (referred to as Selina/Lina) at Telia'z Clinic Israel. The clinician sought urgent guidance about a military-affiliated patient presenting with suicidal ideation who also has access to a weapon. The clinician was anxious because she had been unable to see the patient for a follow-up session and was unsure how to proceed with risk assessment and reporting.

## Suicide Risk Assessment Framework

Rami walked through the structured approach to assessing immediate danger (סכנה מיידית):

### The Suicidality Spectrum
Risk assessment follows a graduated scale:
1. **Suicidal ideation** (אידיאציה) — passive thoughts about death
2. **Suicidal thoughts** (מחשבות) — active thinking about suicide
3. **Intention** — desire to act on thoughts
4. **Plan** — specific method and timing formulated
5. **Act** — attempt or completion

Each level must be assessed and documented. If any level was not assessed, the clinician must document why and provide their clinical impression of whether the symptom is present.

### Point-in-Time Limitation
A critical teaching point: danger assessment is only valid at the moment of evaluation (בנקודת זמן). There is no validated clinical tool for predicting future suicidality — clinicians can only attempt prediction based on risk factors (גורמי סכנה). Documentation should state: "At the time of assessment, the following was/was not present..."

### Differentiating Types of Suicidality
Rami distinguished between two patterns:
- **Depression-based suicidality (עובדנות)**: Gradual escalation with observable warning signs — closing accounts, farewell conversations, planning behavior. More predictable.
- **Personality-disorder-based self-harm (פגיעה עצמית)**: Impulsive, difficult to predict. The person may act without warning at any moment.

Understanding the source is clinically essential because it determines what interventions are appropriate and how urgently they must be deployed.

## Documentation Requirements

### Clinical Duty to Assess
When there is any indication of danger (to self or others), the clinician must demonstrate they did the maximum to assess immediate risk. This protects both the patient and the clinician.

### What to Document
- Every assessed symptom and its finding (present/absent)
- For symptoms not directly assessed: the clinician's impression based on observed behavior, with explanation
- The reasoning for clinical decisions made
- Whether the symptom was reported by the patient or inferred from behavior

### Legal Precedent: McKree Case
Rami referenced the McKree case (US legal precedent) to illustrate the duty of care: in situations where a patient presents with suicidal risk, clinicians are expected to act to protect the patient, even when the legal/clinical obligation is not perfectly clear. The standard of care requires demonstrating that the clinician took maximum reasonable steps.

## Rami's Clinical Assessment of This Case

Based on what the clinician described, Rami assessed that there was **no indication of immediate danger** at the time of the last clinical encounter. However, he identified that the clinician's current anxiety was arising from:
1. Being unable to follow up with the patient (missed session)
2. Uncertainty about the patient's current state
3. Conflating concern about future risk with assessment of past immediate danger

Rami helped the clinician distinguish between:
- **What she assessed at the time** (no immediate danger per her report)
- **What she fears now** (general concern about potential future harm, which is different from clinical assessment of immediate danger)

## Military Reporting Protocols

### Active Military (חייל סדיר / חייל בקבע)
If the patient is under the military system (מערכת צבאית):
- The clinician **can** and should contact the military officer (קבן), military therapist, or military psychiatrist
- These professionals are considered part of the military system and communication is permitted
- The clinician can push for urgent psychiatric evaluation through military channels

### Non-Active Military / Civilian
If the patient is a reservist or no longer in active service:
- The clinician **cannot** contact the patient's employer or commander without the patient's consent
- The primary channel is through the patient's treating therapist (מטפלת)
- Different rules apply for confidentiality and mandatory reporting

### Key Distinction
The patient's exact military status (active duty, reserves, security forces) determines which reporting channels are available. The clinician was instructed to verify this before acting.

## Immediate Action Plan

Rami provided concrete next steps for the clinician:
1. **Contact the patient's therapist (מטפלת)** immediately
2. **Speak with the commander (מפקד) or officer (קבן)** if the patient is active military
3. **Request urgent psychiatric evaluation** — the patient needs to be assessed by a military psychiatrist as soon as possible
4. **ER referral if deterioration** — if any signs of worsening (דרדרות), take the patient to the emergency room (מיון) immediately
5. **Document everything** — ensure the assessment, clinical reasoning, and actions taken are fully recorded
6. **Follow up** — the clinician was told to stay in contact with Rami for any further questions

## Key Teaching Points

- Danger assessment is point-in-time only — no tool predicts future suicidality reliably
- Distinguish between depression-based suicidality (gradual, more predictable) and personality-based self-harm (impulsive, harder to predict)
- Document what was assessed, what was not assessed (with reasoning), and clinical impressions
- The clinician's retrospective anxiety is not the same as a clinical assessment of danger
- Military status determines reporting channels — verify before acting
- When in doubt, push for urgent assessment rather than hesitate
- The legal standard is demonstrating maximum reasonable effort to assess and protect
