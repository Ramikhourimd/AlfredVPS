# Clinical Supervision — Suicidal Risk Assessment Protocols

Clinical guidance provided by Rami Khouri during a supervision call on 2025-12-01, addressing a case involving a patient with suicidal thoughts and weapon access through military/security service.

## 1. Assessing Immediate Danger (Sakana Miyedit)

When assessing whether a patient poses immediate danger to themselves or others, the clinician must evaluate the full suicidality escalation ladder:

1. **Suicidal Ideation** — passive thoughts about death
2. **Suicidal Thoughts** — active thoughts about ending one's life
3. **Intention** — desire or plan to act on those thoughts
4. **Plan** — specific method, timing, or means identified
5. **Act** — attempt or preparatory behavior

Each level must be explicitly assessed. If a level was not assessed, the clinician must document **why** it was not assessed and provide their clinical impression (e.g., "no behavioral indicators suggesting this symptom").

**Critical point:** Immediate danger can only be assessed **at a specific point in time** (b'nekudat zman). There is no clinical tool that reliably predicts future danger. Clinicians can attempt prediction based on risk factors (gormei sakana), but the assessment is always a snapshot.

## 2. Documentation Requirements

Proper documentation is essential for both clinical care and legal protection:

- Every assessed risk factor must be documented
- If a symptom was **not assessed**, the clinician must either:
  - Return to assess it in a follow-up, or
  - Document why it was not assessed and provide their clinical reasoning
- The clinician should be able to demonstrate they did **the maximum** to assess immediate danger
- The problematic scenario is when a symptom was **neither assessed nor mentioned** — this creates liability

**Legal context (McKree case, USA):** In certain legal precedents, clinicians were found liable when they failed to adequately assess immediate danger. The standard is not that the clinician must prevent all harm, but that they must demonstrate they performed a thorough assessment and took appropriate action based on their findings.

## 3. Differentiating Types of Suicidality

Not all suicidality carries the same risk profile. The source of suicidal behavior significantly affects predictability:

### Depression-Driven Suicidality (Ovdanut)
- **Gradual progression** — warning signs develop over time
- Observable behavioral indicators: settling accounts, farewell conversations, giving away possessions
- More predictable — clinician can identify escalation pattern
- Allows for intervention planning

### Personality Disorder Self-Harm (Pgi'a Atzmit)
- **Impulsive** — can happen at any moment without clear escalation
- Much harder to predict
- Behavioral cues may be absent before an act
- Clinician cannot reliably say "I think something will happen in 2-3 days"
- This is the more clinically challenging scenario

**Clinical implication:** When a patient's presentation is puzzling or cyclical, the clinician should consider whether the underlying driver is depression (gradual, predictable) or personality-related (impulsive, unpredictable), as this determines the urgency and type of intervention.

## 4. Distinguishing Clinical Concern from Anxiety

Rami highlighted an important meta-clinical observation: the supervisee's anxiety about the patient was partly driven by **inability to see the patient** rather than by a clinical assessment of immediate danger. This distinction matters:

- If the concern is clinical (based on observed indicators), the clinician should have acted at the time of assessment
- If the concern is anxiety-driven (based on inability to follow up), the recommended action is different — it's about ensuring monitoring, not about emergency intervention
- The supervisee's recommendation should focus on **close follow-up** (ma'akav tzamud) rather than emergency weapon confiscation (shlilat neshek)

## 5. Military vs. Civilian Reporting Protocols

The reporting pathway differs significantly based on the patient's military status:

### Active Duty Soldier (Chayal Sadir)
- Patient is part of the military system (ma'arechet tsva'it)
- Clinician **can** contact the military mental health officer (Kaban/Katz'in Briut Nefesh)
- Clinician **can** contact the military psychiatrist
- Clinician **can** contact the patient's commander (mefaked)
- These are considered within the same care system — no confidentiality barrier
- This is the most effective intervention channel for immediate risk

### Reservist or Civilian Security (Outside Active Service)
- Patient is **not** part of the active military system
- Clinician **cannot** report to the employer/commander without patient consent
- The primary channel is the patient's own therapist (metapelet)
- Confidentiality protections apply

### Weapon Confiscation (Shlilat Neshek)
- Reporting to authorities for weapon license revocation is a clinical tool but **not immediately effective** — it can take a month to process
- If immediate danger exists, the clinician should push for direct intervention through the chain of command, not wait for bureaucratic processes
- Even with low-level suspicion, the clinician should not hesitate to act

## 6. Concrete Action Plan (for this case)

1. **Talk to the patient's therapist (metapelet)** — inform them of the clinical concern
2. **Request urgent psychiatric assessment** — the patient should be seen by a psychiatrist as soon as possible
3. **If any signs of deterioration** — take the patient to the emergency room (miyon)
4. **Communicate the clinical picture clearly:** "I assessed the patient, there was no immediate danger at the time of assessment, but there is potential risk — specifically, the patient holds a weapon, had suicidal thoughts, and needs close follow-up by a psychiatrist as soon as possible"
5. **Document everything** — the assessment, the reasoning, the actions taken, and the communication with the therapist/commander
6. **Stay available** — Rami offered ongoing availability for follow-up questions
