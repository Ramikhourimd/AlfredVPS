# Clinical Supervision on Suicide Risk Assessment Protocol

## Context

On 2025-12-01, a clinician working under Telia'z Clinic Israel contacted Rami Khouri (psychiatrist, supervisor) for urgent clinical supervision. The clinician was concerned about a patient connected to military/security service who had expressed suicidal thoughts. The clinician had last seen the patient on 2025-10-25 and was unable to schedule a follow-up, creating anxiety about whether the patient was at risk.

Rami provided comprehensive clinical guidance covering risk assessment, documentation, differential diagnosis, reporting protocols, and an immediate action plan.

## Suicide Risk Assessment Framework

Rami outlined the structured assessment scale for evaluating immediate danger (סכנה מיידית):

1. **Suicidal ideation** (אידיאציה) — General thoughts about death/dying
2. **Suicidal thoughts** (מחשבות) — Specific thoughts about ending one's life
3. **Intention** — Active desire to act on thoughts
4. **Plan** — Concrete steps formulated
5. **Act** — Actual attempt or preparatory actions

**Key principle**: Clinicians can only assess danger at a single point in time (בנקודת זמן). There are no tools today to predict future danger — only risk factor-based prediction (גורמי סכנה) is possible.

## Documentation Requirements

Rami emphasized that documentation (תיעוד) is critical for clinical liability protection:

- **What was assessed**: Document each element of the risk assessment conducted
- **What was NOT assessed and why**: If a symptom was not evaluated, explain the clinical reasoning
- **Clinical impression**: Even without direct report, document behavioral observations that support or refute the presence of a symptom (e.g., "no behavior indicative of this symptom was observed")
- **The problematic scenario**: Failing to assess a symptom AND failing to mention it in documentation — this creates the highest liability risk

### Legal Precedent — McKree Case (US)

Rami referenced the McKree case from the United States as a legal precedent. The case established that clinicians have a duty to act (לדווח) when there is immediate danger to the patient or others. The duty requires making maximum effort to assess danger, even when the clinical picture is unclear.

## Differential Diagnosis of Suicidality

Rami distinguished three categories with different clinical implications:

### 1. Suicidality from Depression (אובדנות מדיכאון)
- **Pattern**: Gradual escalation (הדרגתי)
- **Signs**: Closing accounts, making farewell calls (שיחות פרידה), giving away possessions
- **Predictability**: Higher — behavioral warning signs emerge over days/weeks
- **Clinical response**: Can identify trajectory and intervene proactively

### 2. Personality-Driven Self-Harm (פגיעה עצמית מאישיותי)
- **Pattern**: Impulsive (אימפולסיבי)
- **Signs**: Can occur at any moment without warning
- **Predictability**: Very low — hardest to predict
- **Clinical response**: More challenging to prevent; requires ongoing monitoring

### 3. The Diagnostic Question
When a patient presents with recurrent distressing behavior, consider:
- Is this psychotic (מאורגן)?
- Is this personality-based (אישיותי)?
- Understanding the origin informs the risk profile and intervention strategy

## Military vs. Civilian Reporting Protocols

A critical distinction was made regarding the patient's military affiliation:

### If Active Military (חייל סדיר / כוננות)
- Patient falls under the military health system (מערכת צבאית)
- Clinician CAN contact the military mental health officer (קב"ן) or military psychiatrist (פסיכיאטר צבאי)
- Can also contact the patient's commanding officer (מפקד) to request weapon confiscation (שלילת נשק)
- These channels are considered part of the military system — reporting is permitted

### If Civilian / Discharged
- CANNOT report to the patient's employer or security contacts without consent
- The only channel is the patient's own therapist (מטפלת)
- Must work through the therapeutic relationship

### Reporting to Licensing Authority
- Filing with the licensing authority (שלילת רישיון) is possible but slow — can take a month
- More effective immediate action: push the treating psychiatrist or military commander to take action

## Assessment of This Specific Case

Rami's clinical impression based on the supervisee's report:
- **No immediate danger perceived** at the time of the last assessment
- **However**: Patient holds a weapon (נשק), has had suicidal thoughts (מחשבות), and the supervisee's anxiety stems from inability to follow up
- **Important distinction**: The supervisee's current worry is driven by not being able to see the patient, NOT by clinical observations of deterioration — this is more general anxiety than clinical-evidence-based concern
- **If the supervisee felt at the time of assessment that weapon removal was necessary**, that should have been acted on then — the fact it wasn't suggests the clinical picture at that time did not warrant it

## Immediate Action Plan

Rami instructed the clinician to:

1. **Contact the patient's therapist (מטפלת)** — speak with them directly
2. **Communicate that the patient needs urgent psychiatric evaluation** (פסיכיאטר assessment as soon as possible)
3. **If any signs of deterioration (דרדרות)** — take the patient to the emergency room (מיון) immediately
4. **If the patient is active military** — take additional steps through military channels (קב"ן, commander)
5. **Document everything** — the assessment, clinical impression, actions taken, and recommendations made
6. **Remain available** — Rami offered ongoing availability for follow-up questions

## Key Takeaway

The core clinical teaching in this session: **distinguish between what you assessed clinically at the time versus what you fear now**. Clinical decisions must be based on documented point-in-time assessments, not on retrospective anxiety. If the assessment was thorough at the time and did not indicate immediate danger, the appropriate next step is ensuring ongoing monitoring — not self-blame for not acting on information that wasn't available then.
