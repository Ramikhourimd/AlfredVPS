## Insight

Bonus structures are not merely compensation tools; they are attention-direction mechanisms that fundamentally shape where employees focus their effort.

## Source

Articulated by [[person/Oren Taliaz]] during [[conversation/Operational Processes and Office Planning Discussion 2025-11-09]].

## Core Principle

When you attach a bonus to a specific behavior (e.g., closing a private patient sale), that behavior becomes the employee's primary focus. Other responsibilities, even important ones, receive diminished attention. This creates a tunnel vision effect where the bonused activity crowds out broader operational excellence.

## Example

Per-transaction Makhirot bonuses incentivize employees to focus on individual deal closures. While this drives sales activity, it can lead to:
- Neglect of patient retention and care quality
- Short-term thinking over long-term relationship building
- Competition between staff rather than collaboration
- Resistance to process changes that might reduce individual deal opportunities

## Implication

When designing incentive structures, leadership must consider:
1. **What will this bonus cause people to ignore?** Every incentive has an opportunity cost
2. **Is the bonused behavior the highest-value activity?** Or just the easiest to measure
3. **Can the desired outcome be achieved through process design instead?** Automatic processes that produce good outcomes without requiring individual heroics are more scalable

## Application

This insight directly informed [[decision/Transition Bonus Structure From Per-Transaction to Automatic Process]] and reinforces [[decision/Phase Out Makhirot Incentives After December 2025]].
