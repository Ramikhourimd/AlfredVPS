# Suicide Risk Assessment Clinical Framework

## Assessment Hierarchy

Suicide risk is assessed along a clinical gradient. Each level must be evaluated and documented:

| Level | Hebrew Term | Description |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| 1. Suicidal Ideation | אידיאיישן | General thoughts about death or dying |
| 2. Suicidal Thoughts | מחשבות אובדניות | Specific thoughts about harming oneself |
| 3. Suicidal Intention | כוונה | Desire or willingness to act on thoughts |
| 4. Suicidal Plan | תוכנית | Concrete plan with method, time, means |
| 5. Suicidal Act | מעשה | Actual attempt or act of self-harm |

**Critical principle:** Assessment captures danger **at a single point in time only**. Stating "no immediate danger" means no danger was identified at the moment of examination — not that the patient is safe going forward.

## Documentation Standards

The clinician must demonstrate maximum due diligence:

1. **Assessed and documented** — Record findings for each level of the hierarchy
2. **Not assessed — with explanation** — If a level was not directly assessed, explain why (e.g., "symptom not reported, but no behavioral indicators observed that suggest its presence")
3. **Clinical impression** — Even without direct patient report, the clinician can document impressions based on observed behavior ("I am of the impression that X is not present because...")

**Red flag:** The problematic scenario is when a symptom is neither assessed nor mentioned in documentation. This creates liability.

## Differential Diagnosis

### Depression-Driven Suicidality (עובדנות)
- **Pattern:** Gradual escalation
- **Markers:** Closing accounts, farewell conversations, giving away possessions, withdrawal
- **Prediction:** More predictable — behavioral indicators provide a timeline
- **Intervention window:** Larger — allows proactive intervention based on observed trajectory

### Personality-Driven Self-Harm (פגיעה עצמית)
- **Pattern:** Impulsive, sudden
- **Markers:** History of impulsive behavior, emotional dysregulation
- **Prediction:** Very difficult — can occur at any moment without warning
- **Intervention window:** Narrow — requires structural safeguards (weapon removal, close monitoring)

### Organized vs. Personality-Based Patterns
When a patient shows a recurring pattern of risk behavior, differentiate:
- **Organized condition** (מאורגן) — predictable trajectories, responds to structured treatment
- **Personality disorder** (אישיותי) — impulsive, requires different risk management approach

## Risk Prediction

There is no clinical tool that can predict future danger with certainty. Risk prediction is based on:
- **Risk factors** (גורמי סכנה) — prior attempts, access to means, social isolation, substance use, etc.
- **Protective factors** — social support, engagement in treatment, willingness to seek help
- **Clinical trajectory** — is the pattern escalating or stable?

The clinician documents: "At the time of examination, based on assessment of [levels checked], there was/was not indication of immediate danger."

## Reporting Protocols (Israel)

### Military Context (IDF/Security Forces)

| Patient Status | Reporting Channel | Consent Required? |
|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Active duty (חייל סדיר) | Kaban (קב"ן), military psychiatrist, commander | No — within the system |
| Career military (קבע) | Same as active duty | No — within the system |
| Reservist on duty (מילואים active) | Same as active duty during service | No — within the system |
| Civilian / discharged | Patient and their therapist only | Yes — cannot disclose without consent |

**Most effective intervention:** Push for an urgent military psychiatrist evaluation — faster and more actionable than filing a license revocation report (שלילת רישיון), which can take a month.

### Emergency Protocol
If there are signs of acute deterioration (דרדרות):
- Take the patient to the emergency room (מיון) immediately
- Do not wait for administrative channels

## Legal Reference

**McKream Case (US):** Established that when any indication of immediate danger to self or others exists, the clinician has a duty to take maximum assessment and intervention steps, even when the legal framework is ambiguous about what constitutes "immediate."
