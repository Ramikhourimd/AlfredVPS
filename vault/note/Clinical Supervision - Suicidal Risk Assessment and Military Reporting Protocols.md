# Clinical Supervision - Suicidal Risk Assessment and Military Reporting Protocols

## Context

On December 1, 2025, a case manager (Selina) at Telia'z Clinic Israel called Rami Khouri for urgent clinical supervision. The case involved a patient who had previously expressed suicidal thoughts. The patient is connected to the military or security services and has access to a weapon. Selina was anxious because she could not see the patient before a scheduled follow-up, and needed guidance on how to assess risk, document her findings, and report through appropriate channels.

This conversation represents a rich clinical teaching session covering fundamental concepts in psychiatric risk assessment and institutional reporting.

## Suicidal Risk Assessment Framework

### The Assessment Hierarchy

Rami outlined the clinical hierarchy for assessing suicidal risk, moving from least to most severe:

1. **Suicidal ideation** (thoughts about death or dying)
2. **Suicidal thoughts** (machshavot) — more specific thinking about self-harm
3. **Intention** — the patient has formed intent to act
4. **Plan** — the patient has a specific plan for how to carry it out
5. **Act** — the patient has taken preparatory or actual steps

Each level must be assessed during a clinical evaluation. If any level was not assessed, the clinician must document why and what their clinical impression was despite the gap.

### Point-in-Time Assessment

A critical concept: **immediate danger (sakana miyedit) can only be assessed at a specific point in time.** There is no clinical tool that can predict future danger reliably. When a clinician states there is or is not immediate danger, this refers only to the moment of assessment.

Prediction of future risk relies on **risk factors** (gormei sakana), not on assessment of current state. These are fundamentally different clinical activities.

### Differentiating Types of Suicidality

Rami explained the critical distinction between two sources of suicidal behavior:

1. **Depression-driven suicidality (avdanoot)** — This is gradual and potentially detectable. Warning signs include:
   - The patient closing financial accounts
   - Making farewell calls to loved ones
   - Progressive planning behavior
   - Giving away possessions
   
   This type allows for some degree of prediction ("I'm concerned they may act in the next few days").

2. **Personality disorder-driven self-harm (pgia atzmit)** — This is impulsive and much harder to predict. The patient may act at any moment without warning signs. This makes it particularly challenging to justify proactive intervention, since the clinician cannot point to evidence of imminent planning.

## Documentation Requirements

### The McKrim Precedent

Rami referenced the McKrim case (US legal precedent) where clinicians were held liable for failing to properly assess immediate danger. The key lesson: **you must be able to demonstrate that you did the maximum to assess whether immediate danger exists.**

### What Must Be Documented

For every clinical encounter with a potentially suicidal patient:

1. **What was assessed** — Each element of the risk hierarchy that was evaluated
2. **What was found** — The clinical findings for each assessed element
3. **What was NOT assessed and why** — If a symptom was not directly reported, the clinician can document:
   - "The symptom was not reported by the patient"
   - "There was no behavior indicative of this symptom"
   - "Based on clinical impression (hitrashmut), this symptom does not appear present"
4. **Clinical reasoning** — Why the clinician believes immediate danger does or does not exist

### The Problematic Scenario

The worst documentation outcome is when a symptom was **neither assessed nor mentioned** in the record. This creates liability because there is no evidence the clinician considered it. Either assess it directly, explain why it was not assessed, or document your clinical impression about its absence.

## Distinguishing Clinical Anxiety from Clinical Evidence

Rami made an important distinction for Selina's specific case: her current anxiety about the patient stemmed from **not being able to see them** (i.e., inability to follow up), not from clinical evidence of immediate danger observed during the actual assessment.

This distinction matters because:
- If she felt during the assessment that the patient was in immediate danger, she should have pushed for intervention at that time
- The fact that she did not suggests her clinical assessment at the time did not indicate immediate danger
- Her current concern is more general ("something might happen") rather than specific and evidence-based
- The appropriate response is to ensure close follow-up, not retroactive emergency intervention

However: if at the time of assessment she genuinely believed the patient needed weapon removal and did not act on it, that would constitute a clinical judgment error that needs to be addressed.

## Military vs. Civilian Reporting Protocols

### Active Military Personnel (Hayal Sadir/Keva)

If the patient is in active military service (IDF regular or career):
- They are part of the military health system (ma'arechet tzva'it)
- The clinician CAN report to the military mental health officer (kaban) or military psychiatrist
- These professionals are part of the system and can be contacted directly
- The clinician can request weapon removal and urgent psychiatric evaluation
- Even filing a formal report (divuach l'shlilat rishayon) is possible, though it takes time (potentially a month)

### Non-Active Military / Civilian

If the patient is NOT in active military service:
- **Cannot report to their employer/commander without patient consent**
- The clinician can only speak directly with the patient
- The therapist (metapelet) is the primary contact point
- More limited intervention options

### Reservists and Security Personnel

The distinction matters: reservists (miluim) and active duty are part of the military system. Security personnel outside IDF (bitachon) may not be, and different rules apply.

## Action Plan for This Specific Case

Rami prescribed the following concrete steps:

1. **Contact the patient's therapist (metapelet)** immediately
2. **Explain the situation:** patient was assessed, no immediate danger was found at the time, but there is potential for suicidality (potenzial l'avdoot) — specifically, the patient has suicidal thoughts and holds a weapon
3. **Request urgent psychiatric evaluation** — the patient needs to be seen by a psychiatrist as soon as possible (b'hekdem efshari)
4. **Verify military status** — determine whether the patient is active military (within the system) or not, as this affects reporting channels
5. **If signs of deterioration emerge** — take the patient directly to the emergency room (miyon)
6. **Document everything** — record the supervision call, clinical reasoning, and all actions taken
7. **Stay available** — Rami offered continued supervision support for follow-up questions

## Key Clinical Principles

- **Immediate danger is a point-in-time assessment**, not a prediction
- **Document everything** — especially what was NOT found or NOT assessed
- **Clinical anxiety ≠ clinical evidence** — distinguish your worry from observed danger signs
- **The most effective tool** for protecting a dangerous patient is pushing for intervention through the treatment framework (psychiatrist, commander), not just filing formal reports
- **Personality-disorder suicidality** is harder to predict and manage than depression-driven suicidality
- **The McKrim standard** requires demonstrating you did the maximum to assess danger
