# Psychiatric Assessment Residential Patient Bipolar Depression 2025-12-02

## Context

Dr. Rami Khouri conducted a face-to-face psychiatric assessment of an anonymous male patient at a residential psychiatric facility on 2025-12-02. The patient has a long psychiatric history spanning 15+ years, with complex diagnostic and treatment challenges.

## Patient Presentation

### Chief Complaints
- Suffering from depression for over 15 years despite ongoing treatment
- Current medications not helping — expresses despair and frustration with treatment
- Taking 21 pills per day
- Requests change in treatment approach
- Night anxiety with palpitations, fear of isolation
- Head pressure, sensation of eyes bulging, full-body heaviness ("feels like 200 kg from medications")
- Paranoia — does not want to feel paranoid

### Diagnostic Discussion
- **Assessor's clinical impression:** Bipolar depression — not regular depression. Mood stabilizer (Lamictal/Depakote) is appropriate for this condition, not standard antidepressants alone.
- **Schizophrenia diagnosis in records:** Patient firmly disputes this. Denies ever hearing voices or having unusual/supernatural experiences. Reports that a doctor in Holland asked about hallucinations and patient denied. No psychotic features identified during assessment.
- **Patient's self-identification:** Claims PTSD from military service (IDF) — reports being injured in the army but unrecognized by authorities.

### Current Medications
- **Cymbalta (duloxetine):** Half tablet morning, half tablet evening
- **Deprexia (divalproex/valproate):** Patient considers this an outdated medication (40 years old), wants newer alternatives
- **Lamictal mentioned** as mood stabilizer
- **Clonazepam/benzodiazepines:** Patient wants sedatives for anxiety relief; assessor explicitly warned against clonazepam due to memory impairment
- **Paxil (paroxetine):** Patient requests this specifically; some reduction in dosage already occurred
- Patient reports most pills taken at facility are non-psychiatric

### Hospitalization History
- Involuntary psychiatric hospitalization approximately 2004
- Court-ordered psychiatric observation (10-day period)
- Patient strongly disputes necessity of all hospitalizations — attributes them to incompetent physicians
- Multiple admissions reported

### Psychosocial Context
- **Perceived discrimination:** Patient is the only Sephardi/Mizrahi individual among Ashkenazi residents. Believes staff treat him poorly because of ethnic background.
- **Social isolation:** Does not speak to anyone, does not engage with psychologists or social workers at facility. Describes feeling invisible — staff "pretend I don't exist."
- **Specific incident:** Young social worker introduced himself, said "I heard about you, I want to talk" — never followed up after 4 days.
- **Staff interaction pattern:** 5-6 staff members consistently ignore patient. Patient caught one staff member in some incident but remains alone.

## Clinical Assessment

### Key Differential
The assessor explicitly tried to determine whether the patient's current distress is:
1. **Reactive** — due to perceived mistreatment and social isolation at the facility, OR
2. **Psychiatric deterioration** — worsening of underlying bipolar/affective disorder

Patient reported night anxiety with palpitations, fear, and isolation, which resolves when he moves to the balcony — suggesting both situational and biological components.

### Assessor's Observations
- Patient is alert and engaged, able to articulate complaints clearly
- No psychotic features evident during assessment
- Patient shows emotional distress but appears oriented and coherent
- Treatment frustration is prominent — patient feels trapped in ineffective regimen
- Ethnic discrimination claim warrants investigation but certainty is unclear (patient himself acknowledges "how would I know?")

## Clinical Significance

This session illustrates several themes relevant to Telia'z clinical operations:
- Complex patients with long psychiatric histories and polypharmacy
- Diagnostic disagreement between patient and historical records (schizophrenia vs. bipolar vs. PTSD)
- Treatment resistance and medication fatigue
- Institutional care quality concerns and patient-perceived discrimination
- The challenge of differentiating reactive distress from psychiatric deterioration
