# Psychiatric Session: Medication Switch From SSRI to SNRI (2025-12-02)

## Context

Voice memo recording of a psychiatric consultation session at the clinic on 2025-12-02. The treating psychiatrist (Rami, speaker 1) evaluates a patient (speaker 2) presenting with recurring episodes of disconnection and depressive symptoms. The patient's identity is not named in the transcript.

## Patient Presentation

- Recurring episodes of emotional disconnection and dissociation — described as entering a state where familiar surroundings feel altered
- Episodes are episodic, not lifelong — the patient reports there were periods when "everything was fine"
- Pattern fluctuates: "it comes and goes"
- Sleep difficulties noted, partly attributed to the depression
- Patient is also receiving ongoing psychological therapy

## Current Medication Regimen

| Medication | Dose | Purpose | Frequency |
|------------|------|---------|-----------|
| **Sertraline** | 50mg | Antidepressant (SSRI) | Daily, ongoing for years |
| **Mondormin** | PRN | Sleep aid | As-needed, not regular |
| **Clonex (Clonazepam)** | PRN | Anxiolytic / calming | As-needed, not regular |

## Medication History

- Previously tried **Cipralex (Escitalopram)** — also an SSRI, same drug family as Sertraline
- Both Sertraline and Cipralex are SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors)
- The psychiatrist concluded that the patient's body has habituated to this SSRI family, requiring a switch to a different mechanism of action

## Clinical Rationale for Medication Change

The psychiatrist explained that when patients stop responding to serotonin-only medications (SSRIs like Sertraline and Cipralex), research supports switching to a medication with a different mechanism. **Venlafaxine (Efexor/Vipex)** was recommended because:

1. It is an SNRI — works on both **serotonin and norepinephrine** (dual mechanism)
2. The different pharmacological profile can "refresh" the brain's response when SSRIs lose efficacy
3. Well-studied for patients who have stopped responding to SSRIs

## Medication Transition Plan

The psychiatrist wrote a detailed transition plan in a referral letter for the family doctor:

### Phase 1: Taper Sertraline
| Week | Action |
|------|--------|
| Week 1 | Reduce Sertraline to **100mg** |
| Week 2 | Reduce Sertraline to **50mg** |
| Week 3 | **Stop** Sertraline — allow washout period |

### Phase 2: Initiate Venlafaxine
| Week | Action |
|------|--------|
| Week 4 | Start Venlafaxine at **37.5mg** |
| Week 5+ | Increase to **75mg** |

### Continuing Medications
- **Mondormin** — continue as-needed for sleep
- **Clonex (Clonazepam)** — continue as-needed for calming

## Important Safety Notes

- **Blood pressure monitoring required**: Venlafaxine can theoretically raise blood pressure; the family doctor must establish a monitoring plan
- **No changes before seeing family doctor**: The patient must present the referral letter to their family doctor before making any medication changes
- **Potential initial side effects**: Mild dry mouth and dry eyes possible at the start
- The psychiatrist reassured that blood pressure elevation is generally not significant at these doses

## Follow-Up Plan

- Patient takes the referral letter to the family doctor
- Family doctor prescribes Venlafaxine and sets up blood pressure monitoring
- Next psychiatric follow-up in approximately 2 months
- Referral letter also usable for official documentation purposes within the day
