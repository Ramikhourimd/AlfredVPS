# Psychiatric Evaluation: Ketamine Inquiry and Bipolar Assessment — 2025-11-11

## Context

Psychiatric intake evaluation conducted at Telia'z Clinic Israel (HealthyMind) with an unnamed male patient. The patient was referred by a clinician named Eitan (first name only) who suggested a treatment that could help. The patient initially asked about ketamine therapy. The session evolved into a comprehensive psychiatric history-taking and medication planning consultation.

## Patient Background

- **Childhood:** Grew up in Kochav Yaakov in an ultra-Orthodox (Haredi) household, family of seven children
- **Childhood trauma:** Sexual abuse by father (not only the patient — siblings also affected; some siblings went to police, the patient did not). Additionally abused by a family doctor
- **Young adulthood:** Served in the IDF, diagnosed with ulcerative colitis during service, experienced severe depression and inability to function. Sent to military psychiatric unit ("Kaban") but ultimately discharged on medical grounds
- **India episode (age ~22-23):** Traveled to India post-army. Encountered a man named "Parkash" who drugged him, controlled him for several days (took his credit card, fed him by hand, burned his hands, gave him unknown substances in water and cigarettes). The patient escaped when the man attempted sexual advances. Patient was found in distress afterward
- **Therapeutic community:** Spent time at Kfar Izun (therapeutic community/rehabilitation village). Many repressed traumatic memories only surfaced after Kfar Izun, during EMDR therapy (eye movement desensitization)
- **Post-treatment:** Attempted to study social work but experienced relapse — mania/hypomania, insomnia, flashbacks, paranoia (persecutory content related to the India perpetrator), fear of people, agoraphobia
- **Second psychotic-adjacent episode:** Triggered by accidental cannabis ingestion (hash cake eaten unknowingly), but the episode was primarily PTSD-related content (flashbacks, anxiety, war-related material) rather than classical psychosis. Patient did not require hospitalization
- **Recent years:** Unstable living — small farms, meditation centers, Jerusalem, Haifa. Managed to hold a machine operator job for three years but largely unable to work consistently

## Diagnostic Profile

- **Bipolar disorder** (diagnosed; history of manic/psychotic episode and depressive episodes)
- **Complex PTSD** (childhood sexual abuse, exploitation in India, cumulative trauma)
- **Possible psychotic features** in context of bipolar episodes (grandiosity — "lived in a different reality," paranoia, ideas of reference post-mania)
- **Ulcerative colitis** (comorbid medical condition)

## Previous Medications

| Medication | Period | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Clonex (Clonazepam) | ~2017 | Taken during psychotic/manic episode |
| Seroquel (Quetiapine) | ~2017 | Short course during acute episode |
| Unspecified antidepressant | ~2017 | Caused side effects including muscle spasms; patient discontinued |
| Cannabis oil | Post-medication | Self-medication after stopping psychiatric drugs |

Previous treatment was through Meuhedet mental health clinic with a Dr. Yatziv (psychiatrist).

## Ketamine Discussion

The patient inquired about ketamine treatment, having heard from someone that it helped with psychosis/mania. The psychiatrist clarified:
- Ketamine is not Ministry of Health approved for this indication
- Intranasal ketamine (esketamine) exists but is not standard treatment for this presentation
- The patient needs a mood stabilizer as the primary intervention, not ketamine

## Treatment Plan

The psychiatrist recommended a mood stabilizer given the bipolar diagnosis and recurrent depression:

| Option | Rationale | Decision |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Seroquel (Quetiapine)** | More potent and effective, particularly for acute depression in bipolar. 20 years of clinical experience. | **Selected** — Start 100mg at bedtime for 2 weeks, then increase to 200mg |
| **Lamictal (Lamotrigine)** | Almost no side effects, better long-term stabilizer, prevents future episodes | Deferred for now — less effective for acute stabilization |

Patient expressed fear of medications based on past negative experiences but agreed to try Seroquel, expressing trust in the treating psychiatrist and recognition that he is deteriorating without treatment.

## Key Clinical Observations

### Psychosis vs. PTSD Overlap
The patient's "psychotic" episodes appear to have significant PTSD content — paranoia about the India perpetrator, flashbacks to trauma — making differential diagnosis between bipolar psychosis and trauma-related dissociative/paranoid states clinically important. The first episode occurred in context of substance exposure (India), and the second was triggered by accidental cannabis ingestion.

### Grandiosity Assessment
Patient described feeling of "living in a different reality" and elevated sense of self-importance during episodes, but self-corrected that he did not believe he was the Messiah. This suggests hypomanic rather than full manic grandiosity.

### Functional Trajectory
Patient shows a pattern of brief stabilization followed by relapse — Kfar Izun led to improvement, attempted university (social work), then relapsed. This cycling pattern supports the bipolar diagnosis and argues for sustained pharmacological treatment.

### Patient's Readiness
After years of medication avoidance driven by fear of side effects and psychotic recurrence, the patient has reached a point where functional deterioration has overcome his treatment reluctance. He expressed willingness to try medication again.
