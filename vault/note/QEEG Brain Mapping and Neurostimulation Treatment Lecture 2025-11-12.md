# QEEG Brain Mapping and Neurostimulation Treatment Lecture 2025-11-12

## Context

Voice memo recording from 2025-11-12 capturing a Hebrew-language clinical lecture on QEEG (Quantitative EEG) brain mapping and neurostimulation-based treatment approaches. The speaker is a physician at a hospital/treatment center in Beer Sheva that processes approximately 1,000 patients per month — described as one of the largest such units in the world. The lecture is part of the same clinical training series that included psychedelics history, MDMA/psilocybin mechanisms, and PTSD treatment presentations on the same date.

At the end of the recording, Elias briefly appears and there is a brief discussion about the need to combine different knowledge domains to deliver insights properly.

## Four-Step Treatment Framework

The speaker outlines a systematic treatment approach used at the clinic:

1. **Diagnosis** — Establishing a diagnostic framework (e.g., distinguishing lack of motivation due to schizophrenia vs. depression)
2. **Symptom Identification** — Targeting core symptoms that can be linked to specific brain regions, not just surface-level complaints
3. **Brain Activity Mapping (QEEG)** — Quantitative EEG recording to create a color-coded brain map showing deviations from normative data
4. **Neurocognitive Testing** — Particularly critical for patients with MCI (Mild Cognitive Impairment) to identify specific cognitive deficits (e.g., executive function)

## What Is QEEG

- QEEG has appeared in psychiatric textbooks (Synopsis) for the past 25 years
- Standard EEG recordings show different wave patterns across brain regions
- Computers can easily decompose waves mathematically (Fourier transform), measure them quantitatively, compare against population norms (standard deviations), and produce color-coded brain maps
- Color coding: green = normal, purple/blue = deficit, red = excess
- The system is analogous to reading hemoglobin levels — comparison against established normative ranges

## Research on QEEG-Guided Treatment

The speaker references a key study (published by BrainSway researchers, acknowledged as having commercial interest):
- ~180 depressive patients split into two groups
- One group received stimulation on the left side of the brain, the other received stimulation in the middle
- Overall response rates were identical between groups
- However, when patients were subdivided by specific symptom profiles, differential response emerged — certain symptom clusters responded better to left-sided stimulation, others to midline stimulation
- The speaker notes that such studies are rare and the evidence base is still limited, but the Beer Sheva clinic believes in this approach and applies it clinically

## Case Study 1: OCD

- **Presentation:** Classic post-OCD (obsessive-compulsive disorder) patient
- **QEEG Finding:** Dramatic excess of high-beta waves in frontal areas (shown as red on the brain map)
- **Literature Support:** A study by Zandberg(?) took 27 OCD patients, performed QEEG, and identified two distinct clusters — one characterized by excessive frontal beta activity
- **Treatment:** TDCS (Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation) — cathode placed frontally to suppress excess activity. Patient sits comfortably during treatment (can read, talk)
- **Outcome After ~20 Sessions:**
  - QEEG normalized (previously abnormal map became normal)
  - Symptom improvement confirmed by questionnaires
  - Neurocognitive test scores improved on OCD-related cognitive tasks
- **Research Validation:** This patient participated in a double-blind study — half received real treatment, half received placebo (sham). Post-treatment BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) levels:
  - Treatment group with improvement: BDNF increased
  - Placebo group without improvement: BDNF unchanged
  - This provides biochemical evidence of treatment efficacy

## Case Study 2: Depression

- **Presentation:** Patient reports feeling "turned off," no energy, brain not working — classic low-arousal depression
- **QEEG Finding:** Significantly reduced alpha waves in central/posterior regions (shown as deep blue — cold color = deficit)
- **Interpretation:** Low alpha waves in depression suggest dysfunction in thalamocortical circuits (speaker notes that even ChatGPT can identify this pattern)
- **Treatment:** TACS (Transcranial Alternating Current Stimulation) at the patient's individual alpha frequency (~9-10 Hz), designed to boost alpha wave production
- **Outcome After ~20 Sessions:**
  - QEEG improved — the blue (deficit) area in the middle normalized
  - Patient still reports some depression, but the targeted neurological marker responded to treatment

## Case Study 3: OCD with Latent Depression

- **Presentation:** Patient diagnosed with OCD, not overtly depressed
- **QEEG Finding:** Two patterns simultaneously:
  1. Frontal excess activity (typical OCD pattern — too much red)
  2. A crescent-shaped beta signature in the second column suggesting an underlying depressive tendency
- **Clinical Nuance:** The speaker discusses this with a child patient's father — the child has ADHD with this additional depressive marker. The father initially denies depression, but the child's expression suggests otherwise
- **Treatment Risk:** Standard OCD protocol (cathode to suppress frontal activity) could worsen the latent depression
- **Solution:** A custom protocol developed over many patients — simultaneous cathode AND anode placement at different locations
- **Outcome:** Improvement in OCD symptoms WITHOUT worsening depression — in fact, some improvement in the depressive marker as well

## Key Clinical Principles

- QEEG provides objective, quantifiable brain mapping that can guide treatment target selection
- Neurostimulation approaches (TDCS, TACS) can be personalized based on QEEG findings
- Comorbid or latent conditions visible on QEEG must be considered to avoid iatrogenic harm
- Combined protocols (e.g., simultaneous cathode + anode) can address multiple patterns safely
- The evidence base is growing but still limited — the clinic operates partly on clinical belief supported by emerging research and objective pre/post measurements
