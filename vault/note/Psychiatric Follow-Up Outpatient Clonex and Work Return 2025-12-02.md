# Psychiatric Follow-Up: Outpatient Clonex Review and Work Return Directive

## Context

Dr. Rami Khouri conducted an in-person psychiatric follow-up on 2025-12-02 with a female outpatient currently prescribed Clonex (clonazepam). A family member was also present during parts of the session. The consultation covered medication review, work return requirements, and the therapeutic value of occupational activity. The original transcript is in Arabic (Palestinian/Levantine dialect).

## Clinical Status

The patient reports feeling **significantly better** than before — confirmed both by the patient herself and the family member present. The family member attributes the improvement directly to consistent, on-time medication compliance. The doctor acknowledged the improvement and initially considered gradual dose reduction of other medications, but ultimately decided against any changes.

## Current Medication: Clonex (Clonazepam)

- **Patient's reported regimen:** 1 tablet in the morning, 1 tablet at night
- **Doctor's expected regimen:** Includes a noon dose (patient disputes this)
- **Doctor's note:** The patient was prescribed a noon dose but appears not to be taking it
- **Decision this session:** No medication changes — doctor stated that even a small adjustment could destabilize the current positive state
- **Doctor's reasoning:** Current stability is fragile; the medications are working well together and any modification carries risk of regression

## Work Return Directive

This was the central clinical and compliance issue of the session:

- **Duration of absence:** Patient has not attended work for approximately 2 months
- **Manager feedback:** The manager explicitly communicated that the patient must return — others are waiting for her position
- **Ministry of Health compliance:** The Ministry of Health will not accept prolonged absence without proper justification
- **Required attendance:** Monday and Wednesday, during available time slots
- **Doctor's framing:** Work is prescribed as therapy — "We need you working not for money, but for your brain." Occupational engagement is part of the treatment plan, not separate from it
- **Patient's response:** Expressed willingness but also resistance; mentioned interest in secretarial/computer work rather than current role

## Incident Report

The patient described a **cooking accident** — fire or hot substance fell on her while making bread. She described the pain and aftermath but did not indicate serious injury requiring intervention. The doctor acknowledged but did not treat it as a clinical priority.

## Side Effects Discussion

The patient reported **itching and pain** as side effects, which she attributes to the medication. She scratches due to discomfort. The doctor downplayed these as not a significant concern ("this isn't a problem") and redirected focus to the therapeutic plan. The family member echoed that the medications are responsible for the current positive state and should not be discontinued.

## Family and Social Context

- A family member (Speaker 2) was present and actively participated — reinforcing medication compliance and the importance of staying active
- The patient mentioned her father positively ("the best person in the world")
- Religious context discussed: the family is Christian, and a brief exchange about whether taking medication aligns with faith — resolved affirmatively
- Plans were made to eat at "Sheikh restaurant in Tamra" after the appointment — indicating normal social functioning

## Clinical Decisions

1. **No medication changes** — maintain current Clonex regimen despite patient improvement
2. **Mandatory work return** — Monday and Wednesday attendance required for therapeutic and compliance purposes
3. **Gradual reduction deferred** — doctor noted that future dose reduction is possible but premature at this stage
