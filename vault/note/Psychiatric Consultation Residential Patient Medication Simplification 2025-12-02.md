# Psychiatric Consultation — Residential Patient Medication Simplification 2025-12-02

## Context

Rami Khouri conducted a face-to-face psychiatric consultation with an unnamed elderly male patient (approximately 65-70 years old) residing in a residential care facility (beit avot). The consultation was requested to review the patient's psychiatric medication regimen. At least one other staff member was present during the session.

## Patient Background

- **Refugee history:** The patient spent time in refugee camps across Europe — approximately 1.5 years in Sweden, 1.5 years in Holland, and 6 months in Germany. He describes a life of displacement and perceived persecution over approximately 40 years.
- **Military history:** References military service (IDF) — approximately 500 people in his cohort, from which a smaller group (12-20) was selected for specialized placement.
- **Occupational history:** Worked as a clerk in various settings. Reports being told he was suited for management roles. Mentions a driving license attempt. Reports difficulty maintaining employment due to perceived discrimination and systemic obstacles.
- **Psychiatric history:** Has seen multiple psychiatrists over decades. Notably saw a psychiatrist in Germany who told him he was "very intelligent" and did not need medication ("not need any tablet now"). Mentions a Dr. Siso in Tel Aviv and a Dr. Gurovitz in Yokneam (now deceased). Reports a previous period off medication where he felt well.
- **Presenting concerns:** Paranoid ideation (feels persecuted, believes he is being starved on the phone, mentions "mafia-like" people), general distrust of institutional care, dissatisfaction with current treatment, and perceived ethnic/political discrimination.

## Current Medications (at time of consultation)

1. **Seroquel (quetiapine)** — mood stabilizer/antipsychotic. Recently reduced or stopped by facility psychiatrist (patient says approximately 1 month without full dose). Rami identifies this as the most important medication to maintain.
2. **Zyprexa (olanzapine)** — atypical antipsychotic. Currently prescribed. Rami recommends gradual tapering and discontinuation.
3. **Antidepressant** (referred to as "L" — likely Lexapro/escitalopram or possibly Cipralex). Rami considers this inappropriate for the patient's presentation and recommends discontinuation.

## Clinical Assessment

Rami assessed that the patient's primary condition requires a **mood stabilizer**, not antidepressants. The current polypharmacy (Seroquel + Zyprexa + antidepressant) is producing excessive side effects. The clinical goal is to consolidate treatment onto a single agent — Seroquel — which can serve as both mood stabilizer and antipsychotic.

Key clinical observations:
- **Orthostatic hypotension:** Patient experiences dizziness when moving from lying to standing position. Blood pressure monitoring from supine to standing is recommended.
- **Side effect burden:** Multiple medications producing overlapping side effects; goal is to reduce to one medication with manageable side effect profile.
- **Patient's medication requests:** Patient asked for direct prescriptions and newer medications (mentions "Recital" which he hasn't received). Rami explained he cannot prescribe directly since the patient has a treating psychiatrist at the facility.

## Medication Recommendation

Rami's recommendation (to be communicated via letter to facility psychiatrist):

1. **Increase Seroquel** to therapeutic dose as primary/sole agent
2. **Gradually taper and discontinue Zyprexa** as Seroquel is titrated up
3. **Discontinue the antidepressant** ("L") — not indicated for this patient's presentation
4. **Monitor blood pressure** (lying to standing) to manage orthostatic hypotension risk

## Patient Cooperation and Trust Issues

Rami spent significant time addressing the patient's distrust of the facility psychiatrist. The patient perceives racial bias and feels his concerns are dismissed. Rami acknowledged the patient's feelings while firmly recommending he cooperate with the facility psychiatrist ("give them the benefit of the doubt"). Rami emphasized that having two treating psychiatrists simultaneously is clinically problematic and potentially harmful.

## Outcome

Rami committed to writing a recommendation letter to be handed directly to the patient, who will deliver it to the facility psychiatrist. The letter will outline the medication simplification plan. The session concluded with the patient being asked to wait outside while the letter was prepared.
