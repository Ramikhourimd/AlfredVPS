## Summary

Lina, a clinician at the clinic, contacted Rami for urgent supervision about a patient case involving suicidal risk. The patient is a military-affiliated individual (soldier or reservist) who had previously expressed suicidal thoughts (machshavot). Lina was anxious because she hadn't been able to see the patient recently and was unsure how to proceed.

Rami provided comprehensive clinical guidance covering:
1. How to assess immediate danger (sakana miyedit) at a specific point in time
2. The escalation scale: suicidal ideation → thoughts → intention → plan → act
3. Distinction between suicidality from depression (gradual, predictable) vs. self-harm from personality disorder (impulsive, harder to predict)
4. Documentation requirements to protect the clinician legally
5. The McKee legal precedent from the US regarding duty to assess
6. Military vs civilian reporting protocols (IDF system vs external)
7. Concrete action steps: contact therapist, verify military affiliation, request urgent psychiatric assessment

Rami assessed that based on what Lina described, there was no immediate danger at the time of her last assessment, but the patient should be under close follow-up with urgent psychiatric evaluation.
