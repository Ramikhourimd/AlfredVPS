# Management Attributes Personal Motives to Rami, Undermining Professional Credibility

## Insight

When Rami advocates for changes (physician CEO, controlled growth, marketing repositioning), Telia'z leadership interprets his arguments as personal ambition rather than professional judgment. This misattribution creates a vicious cycle: the more Rami pushes for what he sees as objectively correct positions, the more management frames it as "Rami wants the CEO job," which causes them to dismiss the substantive professional arguments entirely.

Rami uses a revealing analogy: a lawyer advising that "a doctor cannot serve as a judge" is not saying "I want to be the judge" — they're stating a professional truth. Similarly, Rami advocating for physician leadership is professional expertise, not career ambition. But management conflates the two.

## Evidence

- 2025-12-05 therapy session: Rami explicitly states "when they focus on what I want, they miss the point I'm making" and describes deep frustration at being attributed personal motives
- 2025-12-02 management discussion: Leadership dynamics around role definition suggest underlying suspicion of Rami's motives
- 2026-02-22 leadership meeting: Restructuring discussions continue to orbit around Rami's role rather than structural needs
- Rami reports that management is "currently focused" on what he wants rather than on what the organization needs

## Implications

This dynamic must be broken for restructuring to succeed. Possible approaches:
1. An external physician CEO who advocates the same positions removes Rami as the messenger
2. Rami explicitly and formally declining the CEO role (which he is leaning toward) may shift the conversation to substance
3. The organizational health score model could reframe growth decisions as data-driven rather than personality-driven

## Applicability

This pattern applies broadly to the Telia'z restructuring — any professional recommendation from Rami risks being filtered through the "personal ambition" lens, reducing his influence on decisions where his clinical/operational expertise is most needed.
