# Psychiatrist Retention Fragile Due to Converging Negative Factors

## Insight

Multiple independent records reveal that psychiatrist retention at Telia'z is sustained by a single factor class (convenience: WFH + pay) while being eroded by at least five converging negative forces: (1) intensity-driven burnout, (2) structural isolation, (3) perceived management abandonment, (4) unreported/uncompensated extra hours, and (5) loss of clinical mission after the Maccabi HMO transition. No single factor alone would trigger departure, but the accumulation creates a fragile retention equilibrium where any disruption to the convenience premium could trigger rapid attrition.

## Evidence

- **Burnout**: Multiple staff report telepsychiatry intensity uniquely draining — worse than hospital residency per hour
- **Isolation**: Staff describe working alone at home with no team cohesion, even within pods
- **Management absence**: Direct quote: "orphaned children without parents" — feeling unsupported
- **Unreported hours**: Psychiatrists silently absorb prep and review time, no longer reporting because company discourages overtime
- **Mission loss**: Transition from meaningful trauma work to routine HMO cases described as "just another HMO clinic"
- **Convenience anchor**: Rivi's assessment that most psychiatrists stay for WFH and pay, not commitment

## Implications

Retention strategy cannot rely on addressing any single factor. The convergence means that improving one area (e.g., adding group supervision) while ignoring others (e.g., unreported hours, mission) will have limited impact. A comprehensive approach is needed — or alternatively, ensuring the convenience premium remains significantly above market to maintain the current equilibrium.

## Applicability

Primarily applies to psychiatrists at Telia'z Clinic Israel. Case managers show a different retention pattern (stronger commitment, less mission-driven frustration). The synthesis may apply to other telepsychiatry organizations with similar WFH-only models.
