# Restructuring Task Assignments Replicate the Overload They Aim to Solve

## Insight

The Feb 22 leadership meeting — explicitly convened to reduce Rami's unsustainable four-role burden — produced seven follow-up tasks, of which Rami is assigned as owner or primary responsible party on at least four: Define CMO Role Proposal, Present Clinic and Innovation Plans to Board, Establish Innovation-CTO Coordination Mechanism, and Arrange Joint Meeting with Oren and Basel. The very process designed to distribute operational load instead concentrates post-meeting execution on the person it aims to unburden.

## Evidence

- **Define CMO Role Proposal**: Assigned to Rami — he must write his own role definition, a task that inherently cannot be delegated
- **Present to Board**: Assigned to Rami — requires gathering financial projections, preparing slides, coordinating with Oren
- **Establish Innovation-CTO Coordination**: Assigned to Rami — he must create the framework for his own department's interface
- **Arrange Joint Meeting**: Rami initiated this to avoid secondhand information — adding coordination overhead
- **Finalize Org Chart**: Assigned to Oren, but depends on Rami's CMO proposal completion
- **Build Detailed Org Chart**: Assigned to Basel, but requires input from Rami's role definition

This pattern mirrors the original problem: Rami accumulates tasks because no one else has the cross-functional knowledge to execute them, which is precisely the condition the restructuring should resolve but cannot until the restructuring completes.

## Implications

The restructuring is caught in a bootstrap problem: reducing Rami's load requires completing the restructuring, but completing the restructuring requires Rami to do most of the work. Without external facilitation or pre-delegation of task execution, the restructuring will stall or add to Rami's burnout risk rather than alleviating it.

## Applicability

This pattern applies to any organizational restructuring where the most overloaded person is also the most knowledgeable — a common trap in founder-stage companies transitioning to professional management.
