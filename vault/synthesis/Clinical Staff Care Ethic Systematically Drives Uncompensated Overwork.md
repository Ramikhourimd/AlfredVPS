# Clinical Staff Care Ethic Systematically Drives Uncompensated Overwork

## Insight

Across multiple independent records, a consistent pattern emerges: clinical staff at Telia'z systematically work beyond their contracted hours not due to organizational pressure, but due to their own professional care ethic — and the organization's systems fail to detect, compensate, or address this.

Rivi opens extra appointment slots because her patients otherwise wait 7+ months for follow-ups. Psychiatrists arrive early to review cases and stay late to complete documentation. In both cases, the motivation is patient welfare, but the result is uncompensated labor that the automated payroll system cannot detect.

## Evidence

1. **Rivi's overtime** (2026-03-17): Worked 1.5 extra clinical hours in February out of concern for patient continuity. Payroll system missed it entirely.
2. **Psychiatrist unreported hours** (multiple 2026-02-18 records): Psychiatrists routinely prepare before sessions and document after, beyond scheduled blocks.
3. **Systemic invisibility**: Basel's automated payroll cannot detect overtime. No self-reporting mechanism existed until the 2026-03-17 email-to-Basel workaround was established.
4. **Care motivation**: In both cases, clinicians describe patient welfare as the driver — not organizational expectation or performance pressure.

## Implications

- The organization benefits from uncompensated labor it cannot see and therefore cannot manage
- Burnout data may understate the problem because total hours worked exceeds reported hours
- Solutions focused on scheduled hours (the 6-8 hour threshold) miss the hidden overflow
- The email-to-Basel workaround addresses Rivi's case but not the systemic pattern for psychiatrists
- Risk: clinicians who burn out from uncompensated care work may leave suddenly, since retention is convenience-driven not commitment-driven

## Applicability

Applies to all clinical staff at Telia'z Clinic Israel. Most acute for clinicians with high caseloads and those working the follow-up bottleneck. Directly relevant to the patient capacity constraint and the discharge model discussion.
