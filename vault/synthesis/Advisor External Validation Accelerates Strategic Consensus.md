# Advisor External Validation Accelerates Strategic Consensus

## Insight

Ziv (described as an advisor) played a disproportionately influential role in resolving the CMO-vs-COO tension and legitimizing the shared services model by drawing parallels to multinational corporation practices. His external perspective — framing the restructuring as a standard scaling pattern rather than a unique organizational problem — provided the intellectual framework that moved the group from deadlock toward the "centralize first, localize at scale" consensus. This pattern suggests that internal stakeholders with competing interests benefit from external validation to break decision paralysis.

## Evidence

- The meeting note explicitly attributes the "start centralized, build local functions only as scale demands" framework to Ziv's input
- Ziv "drew parallels to multinational corporations" — framing Telia'z restructuring as a known pattern rather than uncharted territory
- Ziv "cautioned against copying hospital structures blindly" — redirecting the debate from healthcare-specific models to general business scaling principles
- The "centralize first" assumption now recorded in the vault traces directly to this advisory input
- The shared services decision adopted Oren's "this is one company" position, which Ziv's framework validated

## Implications

- External advisors with cross-industry experience can break internal deadlocks by reframing problems
- The restructuring strategy is now anchored on a multinational scaling framework, not healthcare-specific models — this has consequences for how future decisions are framed
- If the multinational analogy is flawed (healthcare may genuinely require different patterns), the entire centralization strategy may need revisiting
- Future contested decisions in this restructuring may benefit from similar external validation

## Applicability

This pattern applies to the Telia'z restructuring specifically and to any organization where founders with competing visions struggle to reach consensus on structural questions. The insight that external framing accelerates internal consensus is broadly applicable to organizational design decisions.
