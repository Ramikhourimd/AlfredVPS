# Arab Mental Health Services Structurally Dependent on External Jewish Philanthropic Funding

## Insight

Mental health services for the Arab community in Israel face a structural funding dependency: nearly all significant funding flows through Jewish philanthropic channels (primarily from American and Canadian donors), with Arab organizations unable to independently raise the capital needed to establish and sustain services. This creates a bottleneck where service development depends on intermediaries like Radi Al-Hamoud who can bridge the cultural and organizational gap between donors and Arab service providers.

## Evidence

- Conference discussion (2025-11-13) explicitly described how "all the money that came from America and Canada came through Radi Al-Hamoud"
- Speakers stated that "as Arabs, we cannot fundraise directly — it has to go through established channels"
- Masaha in the north operates with approximately 80% of its budget from a single program, suggesting limited funding diversification
- The Islamic Movement has collectivist infrastructure but lacks dedicated mental health funding mechanisms
- A project cost of 350,000 NIS was discussed as a benchmark, illustrating the scale of investment needed
- Attempts to fundraise through Arab community channels have proven difficult due to nepotism and factional dynamics

## Implications

1. **Telia'z has a structural advantage** — as a technology-enabled mental health service that operates through HMO contracts (Maccabi, Clalit), it bypasses the philanthropic funding dependency that hampers community-based Arab mental health organizations
2. **Expansion into the Arab sector** could be a significant growth lever for Telia'z if the HMO-based model can deliver Arabic-language services without requiring external philanthropic funding
3. **Partnership opportunities** may exist with organizations like Masaha, where Telia'z telehealth infrastructure could extend their limited local capacity
4. **The funding gap represents both a challenge and a competitive moat** — few organizations can sustain Arab mental health services, creating both unmet demand and limited competition

## Applicability

This insight applies primarily to [[project/Telia'z Clinic Israel]] strategic planning for Arab sector growth, and potentially to the broader consideration of how Telia'z positions itself as a provider to underserved populations.
