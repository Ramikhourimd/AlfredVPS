# Informal November Role Divisions Hardened Into February Three-Pillar Model

## Insight

The formal three-pillar district model presented by Basel and Leah in February 2026 did not emerge from first-principles organizational design — it codified informal role divisions that had already crystallized by November 2025. In the November 9 discussion between Rami and Dekkel, three functional areas were already pragmatically separated: Renee handled clinical staff management (recruitment, onboarding, day-to-day personnel), Li Yamin managed HR intermediation (contracts, payroll), and Shira ran clinical operations (scheduling, availability, complaints) for Maccabi adults. By February, these ad hoc divisions were architecturally formalized as: Administrative Manager (Renee), Clinical Operations Manager (Shira), and Medical Director (new hire) — with Li Yamin's HR function absorbed into the parent company shared services layer.

## Evidence

- **Nov 9, 2025**: Rami and Dekkel explicitly divided responsibilities — Renee manages clinical staff, Li Yamin handles contracts and payroll, Shira handles Maccabi adult operations. These were pragmatic assignments, not architectural decisions.
- **Feb 22, 2026**: Basel and Leah presented a "proposed" three-pillar model that maps almost exactly to the November division: Administrative Manager = Renee, Clinical Operations Manager = Shira, Medical Director = new hire. The structural innovation was giving these roles formal titles and parallel authority.
- The transition from informal to formal happened without explicit acknowledgment that the structure was already in place — the Feb 22 discussion treated it as a new design.

## Implications

- The three-pillar model may be more robust than it appears, since it reflects 3+ months of operational reality rather than theoretical design.
- Conversely, known limitations of the informal division (Shira's Maccabi-only scope, Renee's ongoing secretarial work) were carried forward into the formal model without resolution.
- The restructuring is partly a naming exercise — giving formal authority to roles people already occupied informally. This reduces implementation risk but may also preserve existing dysfunctions.
- Basel and Leah's design contribution was providing the architectural frame (parallel pillars, pod model, shared services) around existing practice, not inventing new roles.

## Applicability

This pattern matters for implementation planning: the three-pillar model can be deployed quickly because it formalizes existing reality. However, the scaling problems identified in November (Shira's capacity limits, Renee's dual role as secretary and manager) need separate solutions — the formal structure alone won't fix them.
