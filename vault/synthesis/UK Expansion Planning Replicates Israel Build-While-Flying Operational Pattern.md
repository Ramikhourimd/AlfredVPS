# UK Expansion Planning Replicates Israel Build-While-Flying Operational Pattern

## Insight

The UK clinic launch planning exhibits the same operational patterns that created known gaps in Israel operations: using existing staff as stopgaps (Bassem for UK case management), launching before critical product features are scoped (prescription formats, scheduling), setting aggressive timelines (mid-March campaign, March 31 system launch) while fundamental requirements remain undefined, and relying on a single external dependency (Leon's contract signing) as the critical path.

## Evidence

From the 2026-02-15 team meeting:
- Prescription system for UK: "not yet scoped in product backlog" — yet system launch targeted for March 31
- Scheduling feature: "not yet scoped — critical for UK" — added to backlog only during the meeting
- Questionnaire UI: functionality works but visual presentation needs fixing
- Case manager staffing: using Israel-based Bassem as interim rather than hiring UK-based staff first
- Insurance: still awaiting quote finalization (approximately $5,000 based on prior year)
- SOPs: only ADHD protocol is Telia'z responsibility; all others delegated to Leon
- Contract: still in negotiation as of meeting date

This mirrors Israel patterns where the clinic launched services (Maccabi, Clalit, children's clinic) before operational infrastructure was ready, discovering gaps through crises rather than proactive planning.

## Implications

- UK launch risks repeating the "announce-ignore-retrain" cycle documented in Israel
- Critical product features may be delivered incomplete or late, requiring manual workarounds
- Staff burnout risk increases as Israel team absorbs UK responsibilities
- Success depends heavily on Leon's cooperation and CQC compliance — a single point of failure

## Applicability

This pattern is organizational, not market-specific. Any future expansion (additional UK partnerships, other countries) should be evaluated against this pattern. The Israel experience shows that operational gaps discovered post-launch are significantly more expensive to fix than pre-launch preparation.
