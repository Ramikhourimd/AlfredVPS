# Protocol Development Fragmented Across Individuals Without Systematic Inventory or Lifecycle

## Insight

Clinical and operational protocols at Telia'z Clinic Israel are created ad-hoc by individual staff members, stored in personal files, and never systematically inventoried or maintained. Six independent source records reveal the same pattern: protocols exist in fragments — Elinor created children-focused intake protocols stored in her files, Rivi created an intake checklist with Shira, Rami delivers risk assessment protocols via supervision calls, and the broader protocol mapping task (November 2025) was created precisely because no one knows what protocols exist, which are current, or where the gaps are.

## Evidence

1. **Elinor's protocols** (task: Organize Elinor Protocol Documents) — Children-focused intake and case management protocols stored in Elinor's personal files, needed to be collected into a shared folder after her role change
2. **Follow-up session protocol gap** (task: Define Follow-Up Session Protocol) — Case managers reported that 3rd/4th sessions have no defined protocol; Rivi created an intake checklist independently
3. **Suicidality screening** (task: Strengthen Suicidality Questions) — Parent intake screening was found inadequate only after a live incident; no systematic review had identified the gap
4. **Clinical supervision protocols** (note: Clinical Supervision) — Risk assessment documentation standards delivered verbally by Rami during supervision, not codified in accessible documents
5. **Protocol mapping** (task: Map All Required Protocols) — This task's existence confirms no systematic inventory existed; it proposes the first comprehensive mapping
6. **Feature training** (task: Conduct Frontal Training) — New features deployed without corresponding protocol updates, creating workflow confusion

## Implications

Protocol fragmentation creates three compounding risks: (1) knowledge loss when staff depart or change roles (Elinor's protocols nearly lost), (2) inconsistent clinical practice across case managers and psychiatrists, and (3) inability to audit compliance because there is no registry of what protocols should exist. The decision to require protocol definition before feature development and the two-domain protocol framework are responses to this problem, but without a protocol lifecycle system (creation → review → training → compliance → update), new protocols will fragment the same way.

## Applicability

Applies to all clinical and administrative operations at Clinic Israel. Particularly critical as the clinic scales toward 7,000+ patients — protocol consistency becomes a safety requirement, not just a quality preference.
