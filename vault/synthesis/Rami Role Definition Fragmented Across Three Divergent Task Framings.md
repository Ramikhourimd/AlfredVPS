# Rami Role Definition Fragmented Across Three Divergent Task Framings

## Insight

The single core question — "What is Rami's role in the restructured organization?" — has been captured as three separate tasks with divergent scopes, acceptance criteria, and framings. This fragmentation reveals that the leadership team has not agreed on what the actual deliverable is, let alone what the answer should be.

## Evidence

Three distinct tasks exist in the vault, all nominally about the same problem:

1. **"Define CMO Role Proposal"** — Frames the problem as CMO-vs-COO boundary definition. Acceptance criteria focus on clinical vs operational delineation and interface with Basel's role. Assigned to Rami.

2. **"Define Rami Role for 2026"** — Frames the problem as 4→2 role consolidation with specific external dependencies (Medical Director hire, org chart finalization, shared services agreement). Lists concrete action items including drafting two separate role descriptions.

3. **"Rami to Define Preferred Role for 2026"** — Frames the problem as a personal reflection exercise. Acceptance criteria ask for what Rami "wants to retain" vs "wants to delegate" — a preference question, not a structural one.

Each task implies a different theory of the problem:
- Task 1: It's an authority boundary question
- Task 2: It's a structural decomposition with dependencies
- Task 3: It's a personal preference question

## Implications

- **No clear "done" definition**: If Rami completes Task 3 (personal preference) but not Task 1 (CMO boundary), has the work been done?
- **Risk of duplicate/conflicting work**: Three tasks could produce three different documents that don't align
- **Reveals underlying disagreement**: The fragmentation is itself evidence that the team sees the problem differently — Oren sees a boundary question, the operational team sees a dependency chain, and the meeting consensus was to ask Rami what he wants
- **Recommended action**: Consolidate into a single task with unified acceptance criteria before Rami starts drafting

## Applicability

This pattern — the same strategic question appearing as multiple tasks with different framings — may recur in other restructuring decisions. It signals that the team is creating tasks before achieving consensus on the problem definition.
