# Shared Services Boundary Is the Core Unresolved Design Question

## Insight

Beneath the individual debates about Renee's allocation, secretary assignment, and HR localization lies a single unresolved design question: where exactly does the shared services boundary sit between the parent company and the clinic? The Feb 22 meeting produced a clear decision (shared services for HR, finance, marketing, tech) but immediately generated three boundary disputes — Renee's allocation, secretary pooling, and local HR presence — that reveal the boundary is not as clear as the decision implies.

## Evidence

- The shared services model decision explicitly lists which functions are shared vs in-house
- Contradiction: Dedicated vs shared admin staff (Renee) — sits exactly at the boundary
- Contradiction: Secretary pool vs dedicated per pod — a second boundary question at the operational level
- Assumption: Staff need a local HR "address" — challenges the boundary from a people-management perspective
- Rami and Basel both pushed back on full centralization during the meeting
- Ziv's advice ("start centralized, localize as scale demands") provides a framework but not a specific boundary

## Implications

Every new function, hire, or process added to the clinic will trigger the same question: "Is this a shared service or an in-house function?" Without a clear framework for making this determination (e.g., based on clinical proximity, response-time requirements, regulatory constraints), each instance will be debated ad hoc. The restructuring needs a shared services boundary policy — explicit criteria for when a function should be local vs centralized — not just a list of current allocations.

## Applicability

This pattern will recur for every new clinic (UK, future markets). Each market will have different regulatory requirements, cultural norms, and scale characteristics that shift the optimal boundary. Establishing the boundary framework now — for the Israeli clinic — creates the template for international expansion.
