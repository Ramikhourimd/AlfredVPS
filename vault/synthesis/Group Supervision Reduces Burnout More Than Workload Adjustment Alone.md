# Group Supervision Reduces Burnout More Than Workload Adjustment Alone

## Insight

Clinical staff burnout at Telia'z is driven not just by hours and workload but significantly by isolation and lack of peer connection. Group supervision sessions appear to reduce perceived burnout even without changing actual workload, suggesting that the **relational dimension** of work is a more powerful lever than scheduling optimization alone.

## Evidence

1. One case manager reported burnout suddenly decreased after group supervision started, even though workload remained the same
2. Psychiatrists who report highest burnout also report the least team connection ("children without parents")
3. The transition from Clalit-south (mission-driven, socially meaningful work) to Maccabi (routine HMO) increased burnout despite similar hours — indicating meaning and connection matter
4. Remote work intensifies isolation: staff describe being "alone at a computer" with no organic interaction
5. WhatsApp group messages going unanswered reinforces the sense of disconnection

## Implications

- **Prioritize group supervision expansion** before scheduling changes
- The internal tasks system (replacing WhatsApp) may inadvertently worsen isolation by removing even casual group communication
- Consider building structured peer interaction touchpoints beyond supervision (case discussions, shared forums, social events)
- Staff retention interventions should focus on belonging and meaning-making, not just compensation and convenience

## Applicability

Applies to all Telia'z clinic districts, and is especially relevant for the UK expansion where building team culture from scratch will be critical.
