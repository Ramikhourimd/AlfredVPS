# Parent Reporting Significantly Underestimates Child Suicidal Ideation

## Insight

Parent/caregiver reporting during intake significantly underestimates the severity of a child's suicidal ideation. In a concrete case at Telia'z Clinic Israel, parents described a single suicidal statement from months ago with no current concerns, while the child directly reported an active suicide plan (wanting to hang himself) and had accessed his father's firearm. This gap between parent perception and child reality reinforces that direct psychiatric assessment — not parent-mediated screening — is the only reliable method for evaluating suicidality in children.

## Evidence

- 2025-11-09 child intake case: Parents minimized suicidal history; child revealed active plan and weapon access to Ahmad (Ahmed Masalha) during psychiatric intake
- Senior clinician confirmed this pattern is expected: no clinical setting relies on parent intake to rule out child suicidality
- This aligns with existing clinical literature on parent-child discordance in reporting psychiatric symptoms

## Implications

- The psychiatric intake must remain the definitive suicidality assessment — case managers cannot be expected to detect what parents themselves do not report accurately
- Parent intake questions should be strengthened but cannot substitute for direct clinical assessment
- This case provides concrete evidence supporting the clinic's workflow design where psychiatric evaluation is the safety net

## Applicability

- All child and adolescent intake workflows at Telia'z Clinic Israel
- Relevant to UK expansion planning where similar workflows will be replicated
- Training material for case managers on the limitations of parent reporting
