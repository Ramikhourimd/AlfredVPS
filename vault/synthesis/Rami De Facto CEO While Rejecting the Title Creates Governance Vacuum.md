# Rami De Facto CEO While Rejecting the Title Creates Governance Vacuum

## Insight

Rami confirms in therapy that he is functionally the CEO of Telia'z Clinic Israel — making budget decisions, directing operations, and managing strategic direction — while Alex Taliaz holds the title but "doesn't do the work" and "behaves like a consultant." Yet Rami explicitly rejects taking the CEO title because the personal cost (burnout, distance from technology and family) would be unsustainable. This creates a governance vacuum: the person doing the work has no formal authority, while the person with formal authority isn't executing. Neither can the situation resolve itself — Rami won't take the title, and Alex's continued tenure prevents a replacement from being hired.

## Evidence

- 2025-12-05 therapy session: Therapist asks "are you de facto CEO?" — Rami confirms "yes"
- Rami describes Alex as not working, behaving as a consultant who uses motivational phrases
- Oren has indicated awareness that Alex may not continue
- Rami explicitly declines CEO interest, citing burnout from prior three-day-per-week experience
- Budget and decision cycles currently rely on Rami despite his lack of formal authority

## Implications

This is the structural core of the Telia'z leadership crisis. Resolution requires:
1. Formal replacement of Alex with a new CEO (preferably a physician)
2. Formal definition of Rami's role that matches his actual contribution without requiring CEO-level on-site commitment
3. Operational staff (Basel model) who can execute without Rami's constant direction

Without resolving this, the organization operates on informal authority that can be dismissed when convenient and overburdened when expedient.

## Applicability

This pattern directly impacts the Telia'z Organizational Restructuring project and is likely to surface in every strategic decision until the CEO position is formally resolved.
