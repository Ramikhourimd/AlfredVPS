# Restructuring Sized for Target Scale Not Current Operations

## Insight

The organizational restructuring is designed for a scale the clinic has not yet reached. The 7,000+ patient target, combined with HMO contract dependency and international expansion ambitions, means the new management triad (Clinical Director, Operations Manager, Administrative Manager), pod structure (10 psychiatrists per pod), and secretary team (5 FTE) are calibrated for a future state. This creates a structural tension: the organization is building ahead of revenue, risking cost pressure before patient volume catches up.

## Evidence

- Clinic Israel targets 7,000+ patients but AI research data covers ~6,000, suggesting current volume is below target
- Secretary hiring plan (2 current to 5 by year end) anticipates growth not yet realized
- Three-pillar management model adds cost layers justified by future scale
- Budget constraints are already cited as limiting new management hires — tension between build-ahead structure and current revenue
- Financial viability validation is listed as a constraint, acknowledging the risk

## Implications

- The restructuring has an implicit timing dependency: new management costs must be justified by revenue growth within a specific window
- If HMO contract expansion or patient growth stalls, the new structure becomes financially unsustainable
- Phased implementation (hiring management roles incrementally as volume grows) could mitigate the cost-before-revenue risk
- The "build for scale" approach contrasts with the "centralize first, localize later" assumption — potentially creating over-investment at the district level too early

## Applicability

Applies to all financial planning for the clinic restructuring, board presentations, and hiring sequencing decisions. Particularly relevant for the board presentation to Alex, which must reconcile structure costs against growth projections.
