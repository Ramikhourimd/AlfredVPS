# Internal Task System May Deepen Staff Isolation by Replacing Informal Social Channel

## Insight

The planned internal tasks system (replacing WhatsApp-based operational coordination) optimizes for task management efficiency but may inadvertently worsen the staff isolation problem it partially needs to solve. WhatsApp groups, despite being an imperfect coordination tool, serve a dual function: operational messaging AND informal social presence. Staff see each other's messages, know others exist, and maintain a loose sense of community. A structured task system strips this informal layer.

## Evidence

- Rivi Idelman reported most staff don't feel part of a team — "I'm alone in front of my computer with patients"
- WhatsApp groups are the primary (only) channel where staff experience collective presence
- Staff frustration about unanswered WhatsApp messages is partly about operational failure (no response) and partly about emotional isolation (nobody is there)
- The task system will reduce reasons for staff to check group channels, further reducing incidental social contact

## Implications

- The internal task system should be complemented with deliberate social/community features — not just task tracking
- Consider: peer chat channels, team huddles, visible "who's online" indicators, shared wins/case discussions
- Group supervision sessions appear to partially address this need (see burnout assumption) — expanding them could compensate
- This is a classic system design trap: optimizing one dimension (task efficiency) at the cost of another (social cohesion) that wasn't measured

## Applicability

Applies to all Telia'z clinics and any remote-first clinical operation where informal social infrastructure is at risk of being accidentally dismantled by operational optimization.
